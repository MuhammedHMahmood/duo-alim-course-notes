    TFS 101 - Tafseer of Surah Al-Alaq (Part 3)

    Date: 2023-11-23

    Surah Covered

    Surah Al-Alaq (96) - Ayat 6-14 (with review of Ayah 5 and preview of Ayat 15-19)

    Structure of Surah Al-Alaq (Overview)

    The surah is divided into three segments:

    1. Segment 1 (Ayat 1-5): Islamic epistemology (covered in previous classes)
    2. Segment 2 (Ayat 6-14): Two types of human beings and the causes behind their behavior (main focus of this session)
    3. Segment 3 (Ayat 15-19): The ruling on coexistence and limits of subjugation (to be covered next class)

    Review: Completion of Segment 1

    Why Allah Used the Name "Al-Akram" (The Most Generous)

    • Allah's generosity manifests in two ways:
    • Creation (Khalq): Our very existence is a blessing and an act of generosity
    • Teaching (Ta'lim): Allah did not just create us and leave us to figure things out on our own. He taught us through:
      • Wahi (Revelation): Divine guidance sent through prophets
      • The Pen (Al-Qalam): The ability to record experiences, extract knowledge, and pass it down through writing
    • This is why "Al-Akram" was chosen over other attributes like Ar-Rahman, Ar-Rahim, or Al-Ghaffar -- it encompasses both creation and guidance

    Islamic Epistemology (Summary from Previous Sessions)

    • Sources of knowledge in Islam:
    • Aql (Intellect/Reason): Logical and rational arguments
    • Naql (Textual Evidence): Quran and Sunnah
    • Fitrah (Innate Knowledge/Intuition)
    • Five Senses
    • True Testimony

    • Introspection

    • Key principle: Naql is superior to Aql

    • If we do not understand the wisdom behind a Quranic ayah or hadith, the deficiency is in our understanding, not in the ruling
    • We have no authority to reject an ayah or hadith simply because we do not understand it
    • Claiming otherwise implies that our intellect is superior to divine revelation, which is incorrect

    Segment 2: Two Types of Human Beings (Ayat 6-14)

    Main Theme

    Allah describes two categories of people and explains the root causes that define each group.

    Group 1: The Transgressors (At-Taaghi)

    Ayah 6: Kalla innal insana la yatgha -- "Nay, indeed the human surely transgresses."

    • Taaghi / Yatgha: To transgress, to rebel, to overstep boundaries
    • Inna: Particle of emphasis (harf mushabbah bil fi'l)
    • Lam (in la yatgha): Lam of ta'kid (emphasis)

    Ayah 7: An ra'ahu staghna -- "That he sees himself self-sufficient."

    • Istighna: Self-sufficiency, feeling of independence from Allah
    • Ra'a: To see
    • The root cause of transgression is the feeling of self-sufficiency

    What creates the feeling of self-sufficiency: 1. Money (Amwal) 2. Power (authority, influence)

    Historical examples of transgressors driven by istighna: - Fir'awn (Pharaoh): Had unlimited wealth and extraordinary power; declared himself lord of the world - Namrud: The king who threw Ibrahim (AS) into the fire; suffered from the same delusion of self-sufficiency

    Quranic reference: The mushrikeen said: "Nahnu aktharu amwalan wa awlada, wa ma nahnu bi mu'adhdhabin" -- "We have more wealth and more children, and we will never be punished." (Surah Saba 34:35) - More children = more power in that society - Wealth + power = false sense of invincibility

    The reality: "Ya ayyuhan-nas, antumul fuqara'u ilallah" -- "O mankind, you are the ones in need of Allah." (Surah Fatir 35:15) - Fuqara: Plural of faqir; not "beggars" but "dependents" - No matter how much wealth or power one has, every human is dependent upon Allah

    Modern manifestation -- Humanism: - A philosophical movement that rejects any supernatural authority dictating human conduct - Core belief: Humans alone determine their own fate, morality, and way of life - No supernatural entity has authority to dictate what is moral or immoral - This philosophy is a modern form of istighna (self-sufficiency/independence from God) - This attitude of declaring complete independence from Allah makes people transgressors (taaghi)

    The Inevitability of Return to Allah

    Ayah 8: Inna ila rabbikar ruj'a -- "Indeed, to your Lord is the return."

    • Ruj'a: The return (to Allah)
    • Challenge to those who claim self-sufficiency: If you are truly independent, avoid death
    • Death is the one reality that every human -- atheist, agnostic, deist, polytheist -- acknowledges as inevitable
    • If you must return to Allah, you are dependent upon Him
    • If you cannot go anywhere else, you are not self-sufficient

    Group 2: The Guided and Righteous

    Ayat 9-10: Ara'ayta alladhi yanha abdan idha salla -- "Have you seen the one who forbids a slave when he prays?"

    • Yanha (from naha): To forbid, to prohibit
    • Abdan: A slave (of Allah)
    • Salla: Prays
    • The one who prays is upon guidance and cannot be a transgressor
    • This group is characterized by being upon guidance (huda) and guiding others

    Ayah 11: Ara'ayta in kana alal huda aw amara bit-taqwa -- "Have you seen if he is upon guidance or commands righteousness?"

    • Amara bit-taqwa: Enjoining righteousness
    • What makes a person righteous is when they command others toward righteousness

    Ayah 12: Ara'ayta in kadhdhaba wa tawalla -- "Have you seen if he denies and turns away?"

    • Kadhdhaba: To deny
    • Tawalla: To turn away

    Ayah 13: Alam ya'lam bi annallaha yara -- "Does he not know that Allah sees?"

    • A stern warning: Allah is watching everything

    The Incident of Abu Jahl and the Prophet (SAW)

    Context: Before the Hijrah, in the Haram of Makkah

    • Rasulullah (SAW) would come to the Haram to pray and recite Quran
    • Abu Jahl and his associates opposed this because the Quran condemned their idols and ways
    • Abu Jahl declared he would stop the Prophet from praying
    • When Abu Jahl approached the Prophet (SAW) during salah, he saw a massive barrier of fire between them (only visible to Abu Jahl)
    • Abu Jahl became frightened and retreated
    • He went with the intention of stopping the Prophet but was unable to carry it out

    Why Abu Jahl acted this way: - He was a transgressor (taaghi) - He believed himself to be self-sufficient and independent from Allah - This gave him the arrogance to try to prevent worship

    The Divine Warning (Ayat 9-14 combined message): - You are trying to stop a slave of Allah from praying - What if it becomes clear that he is upon guidance and you are not? - He is calling people toward Allah and righteousness - You are denying and turning away - Do not think Allah is not watching - Since you must return to Allah, prepare to face the consequences

    Key lesson: The first group (transgressors) can never cause permanent harm to the second group (the guided) because Allah is continuously watching and protecting.

    Lessons and Practical Takeaways

    1. Allah's generosity is twofold: He gave us existence AND guidance. He did not leave us to figure out morality on our own.

    2. Naql (revelation) takes precedence over Aql (reason): When we fail to understand a divine ruling, the problem lies with our comprehension, not with the ruling itself.

    3. Wealth and power are the root causes of arrogance and transgression. When people feel self-sufficient, they begin to overstep boundaries and reject divine authority.

    4. Modern ideologies like humanism echo ancient arrogance. Declaring independence from God and claiming the right to define morality without divine input is a form of istighna.

    5. Every human is dependent on Allah regardless of their status, wealth, or power. The proof is the inevitability of death and the return to Allah.

    6. What makes a person righteous is not just personal piety but commanding others toward righteousness (amara bit-taqwa). Active engagement in calling to good is a defining trait.

    7. Transgressors cannot cause permanent harm to the righteous because Allah is always watching. This is a source of comfort for believers who face opposition.

    8. Allah sees everything. This should serve as both a warning to wrongdoers and an encouragement to the faithful.

    Q&A Topics Covered

    The Relationship Between Aql (Mind), Qalb (Heart), and Consciousness

    • Modern science view: The heart is merely a pumping organ; consciousness is just brain activity; non-material mind does not exist
    • Recent scientific discovery (2014): Approximately 40,000 neurons (neural cells) were found in the human heart, performing similar functions to brain neurons (sending signals)
    • Quranic view: The qalb (heart) is associated with understanding and iman (faith)
    • Analogy given: Like a TV system:
    • TV box = Body (the physical structure)
    • Antenna = Brain + Heart (receivers, with the heart as a secondary antenna)
    • Satellite signal = Mind/Consciousness (non-material, location unknown)
    • Key point: Consciousness/mind is a non-materialistic entity connected to both brain and heart; empirically we cannot yet determine which connection is stronger
    • Hadith reference: The place of iman is in the qalb (heart), in the left side of the chest

    War Captives vs. Hostages in Islam

    • The correct term is "war captives" (prisoners of war), not "hostages"
    • "Hostages" implies innocent civilians were taken, which would be terrorism
    • Quranic ruling (Surah Muhammad): "Fashuddul wathaqa, fa imma mannan ba'du wa imma fida'a"
    • After capturing enemy soldiers, either:
      1. Mannan: Release them as a favor (for free)
      2. Fida': Use them for ransom (exchange for your own captured soldiers)

    Suicide Bombing

    • Mainstream scholarly position: Not permissible (majority opinion, supported by the instructor)
    • Dissenting opinion: Sheikh Yusuf al-Qaradawi (rahimahullah) issued a fatwa permitting it for self-defense, with his own reasoning
    • The mainstream scholars did not accept this fatwa

    La Ikraha fid-Din vs. the Law of Apostasy

    • La ikraha fid-din (No compulsion in religion - Surah Al-Baqarah 2:256): Refers to forced conversion; you cannot forcefully convert anyone to Islam because forced iman is not sincere and produces hypocrisy
    • Law of apostasy (from hadith): Not about forced conversion but about an already-Muslim person who leaves Islam AND begins activism against Islam
    • A person becomes kafir simply by thinking they no longer believe -- no one can detect this, no punishment applies at this stage
    • The law applies when the person engages in public activism against Islam within an Islamic state (dar al-Islam)
    • The punishment is for fasad fil ard (corruption in the land), not merely for changing belief
    • Analogy: Even in secular liberal democracies, activism against foundational constitutional principles (e.g., against secularism in France) carries legal consequences

    The Ottoman Janissary System (Devshirme)

    • Historical practice where Christian youth from the Balkans were taken and trained as elite soldiers
    • Some accepted Islam during the program; the extent of forced conversion is debated among historians
    • Instructor's position: Actions of Muslim rulers or empires are not necessarily Islamic; Islam should be defended on its own merits, not by defending every action Muslims have taken throughout history
    • Examples of un-Islamic acts by Muslim rulers: The Abbasid dynasty exhuming Umayyad caliphs' bodies and hanging them at intersections
