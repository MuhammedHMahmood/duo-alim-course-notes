    TFS 101 - Surah Al-Alaq (The Clot) - Session 1

    Date: November 9, 2023 Surah: Al-Alaq (Chapter 96) -- First five ayat Focus: Introduction to the surah, Islamic epistemology, and the four levels of Tawheed

    1. Surah Overview

    • Surah Al-Alaq is Chapter 96 in the Quran but was the first revelation upon the Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wa sallam).
    • Its position as Chapter 96 does not indicate rank or importance; every ayah in the Quran carries full weight regardless of placement.
    • The surah is named "Al-Alaq," meaning "the clot."
    • Only the first five ayat were revealed initially as the very first revelation.
    • The entire surah was not revealed all at once.

    2. Connection to Previous Surah (Surah At-Teen)

    • Surah At-Teen discussed two foundational beliefs (aqeedah):
    • Iman billah -- belief in Allah
    • Iman bil-Akhirah -- belief in the Day of Judgment (necessary for accountability)
    • Surah Al-Alaq builds on this: these two beliefs are what make a human truly human.
    • Without these beliefs, a person loses his humanity and becomes like an animal.
    • The Quran refers to the mushrikeen as "an'am" (animals/cattle): "Ula'ika kal-an'am bal hum adall" -- they are like cattle, even worse.

    3. Position of Surah Al-Alaq in the Quran's Structure

    • Surah Al-Fatiha = the preface of the Quran.
    • From Surah Al-Alaq to Surah An-Nas = the conclusion of the Quran, summarizing what has been said throughout.

    Three Categories of Surahs by Length

    Category Arabic Term Description Examples
    Long surahs Tiwal Cover multiple topics with detailed arguments Al-Baqarah, Al-Imran, Al-A'raf, Al-An'am
    Medium surahs Mutawassitat Focus on mainly one subject with evidence (dalail) Al-Hujurat, Al-Fath, Al-Mujadala
    Short surahs Qisar (plural of qasir) Cover the subject matter only; little or no explicit argumentation, sometimes indirect indication Al-Alaq and surrounding surahs
    • Surah Al-Alaq belongs to the qisar (short surahs).

    4. Why These Five Ayat Were Revealed First -- Islamic Epistemology

    The Central Question: How Do We Know What We Know?

    • This is the field of epistemology -- the study of the sources of knowledge.
    • It is logically the first thing that must be established before any other teachings can be received.
    • If a person does not accept the correct sources of knowledge, he will not accept anything built upon them (belief in Allah, Akhirah, angels, prophets, etc.).

    Western Epistemological Schools

    1. Empiricism -- Knowledge comes only through the five senses (sensory experience). This is the dominant modern scientific worldview. Only observable things can be known; anything beyond observation is dismissed as dogma.

    2. Rationalism -- The mind/reason is also a source of knowledge (emerged roughly 300 years ago in Western philosophy).

    3. This debate occurred in the Western world approximately 300 years ago, but was already addressed in the Islamic tradition 1,200+ years ago through the Quran.

    The Islamic Epistemological Framework -- Three Sources of Knowledge

    Source Arabic Scope Description
    1. Senses Al-Hawas Narrowest Limited to what falls within our sensory field; cannot detect anything beyond it
    2. Rationality/Intellect Al-Aql Broader Can derive conclusions from events beyond direct sensory experience (e.g., past events, logical deduction)
    3. Revelation Al-Wahi (Al-Khabar us-Sadiq) Broadest Provides information about the unseen world (al-ghayb) that is beyond both sensory detection and rational deduction

    Implications of Limiting Sources of Knowledge

    • Pure empiricist: Cannot believe in consciousness, soul (ruh), free will, Allah, Akhirah, angels, or anything beyond the material.
    • Empiricist + rationalist: Can believe in consciousness and non-material realities but limited to what reason can deduce.
    • Full Islamic framework (senses + reason + revelation): Can believe in the complete picture -- the material world, consciousness, the soul, the unseen (ghayb), Allah, the Day of Judgment, angels, jinn, and all that revelation informs us of.

    Connection to Free Will

    • If one denies consciousness (as a pure empiricist), one also denies free will.
    • There is a modern scientific movement claiming humans are like robots with no free will because they deny consciousness.
    • Islam affirms free will because it affirms consciousness through both rationality and revelation.

    5. The First Five Ayat of Surah Al-Alaq

    Arabic Text and Translation

    Iqra bismi Rabbika alladhi khalaq (1) Khalaq al-insana min alaq (2) Iqra wa Rabbuka al-akram (3) Alladhi allama bil-qalam (4) Allama al-insana ma lam ya'lam (5)

    "Read in the name of your Lord who created. Created man from a clot. Read, and your Lord is the Most Generous. Who taught by the pen. Taught man that which he knew not."

    Repeated Words and Their Significance

    • Iqra (read) -- appears in ayah 1 and ayah 3
    • Khalaq (created) -- appears in ayah 1 and ayah 2
    • Allama (taught) -- appears in ayah 4 and ayah 5

    Allah does not use any word unnecessarily. He is the Perfect Existent (al-Kamil), so no imperfect action can come from Him. Every repetition carries a distinct purpose and meaning (to be explored in future sessions).

    6. The Four Levels of Tawheed

    Level Arabic Meaning Universally Agreed?
    1. Tawheed fil-Khalq توحيد في الخلق Oneness of the Creator Yes -- all people agree there is a single point/source of creation, though they name it differently
    2. Wujub al-Wujud وجوب الوجود Necessary Existence -- the Creator's existence is self-sustained, not given by anyone else; eternal, pre-eternal, everlasting Yes -- even atheists, when presented with logical arguments, can become deists and accept this
    3. Tawheed fit-Tadbir توحيد في التدبير Oneness in managing/governing the affairs of the universe No -- Deists, atheists, and many pagans deny this. Pagans assign multiple gods to manage different aspects of creation
    4. Tawheed fil-Ibadah توحيد في العبادة Oneness in being worthy of worship No -- Only believers in Allah affirm this. Mushrikeen worship multiple entities alongside or instead of the Creator

    Key Entity Term

    • Wajib al-Wujud = the entity whose existence is necessary (i.e., Allah)
    • Wujub al-Wujud = the concept/rank of necessary existence

    Why the First Ayah Mentions Only the First Level

    • The first revelation addresses the level of Tawheed that all of humanity already agrees upon: Tawheed fil-Khalq.
    • Whether pagan, Christian, Jewish, atheist, or agnostic, everyone acknowledges a single point of creation (they just name it differently -- God, nature, another universe, etc.).
    • This is a rhetorical and logical starting point: begin with common ground before building toward the higher levels.
    • This demonstrates the divine wisdom of the Quran -- a human author might have started differently, but Allah began with the most universally accessible truth.

    8. Q&A Highlights

    On Christians and the Trinity

    • Christians do affirm all four levels of Tawheed theologically (one creator, necessary existent, manager, worthy of worship).
    • However, their concept of "one" is a compound of three components (the Trinity), which differs from the Islamic concept of absolute, indivisible oneness.
    • Anything compound can be divided, so this is not absolute monotheism.
    • Practically, especially in Catholicism, the worship of statues of Jesus makes them functionally similar to pagans in the fourth level.

    On the Question "Where Did Allah Come From?"

    • The question itself is logically flawed because it presupposes Allah is bound by time and space.
    • Allah is the creator of time and space, so He cannot be contained within them.
    • Analogy: If you build a laptop, you exist outside the laptop, not inside it. Similarly, Allah exists outside of time and space.
    • Questions involving "when" and "where" only apply to time-bound and space-bound entities.
    • These are logical fallacies and should be identified as such.

    9. Lessons and Practical Takeaways

    1. Epistemology comes first. Before accepting any teaching, one must understand and accept the valid sources of knowledge. This is why these five ayat were chosen as the first revelation.
    2. Islam's intellectual framework is comprehensive. It does not reject empirical observation or rational thought but adds revelation as the broadest source of knowledge, allowing believers to access truths beyond the material world.
    3. Tawheed has layers. Understanding the four levels helps in dialogue with people of different beliefs -- start with common ground (Tawheed fil-Khalq) and build from there.
    4. Every word in the Quran is intentional. Repetitions are not redundant; they carry distinct purposes that require careful study.
    5. Belief in the unseen is what defines a believer. "Yu'minuna bil-ghayb" -- the Quran describes the muttaqeen as those who believe in what cannot be observed, which is only rational when revelation is accepted as a valid source of knowledge.
    6. Humanity depends on correct belief. Without iman in Allah and the Akhirah, a person's humanity is diminished -- the Quran compares such people to animals.
    7. When faced with flawed questions about Allah (e.g., "Where did Allah come from?"), identify the false premise -- Allah is not bound by time or space, so "when" and "where" questions do not apply to Him.
