    TFS 101 - Study Notes

    Date: 2023-09-21 Surah Covered: Surah Al-Duha (Chapter 93), with references to Surah Al-Inshirah / Alam Nashrah (Chapter 94)

    Session Overview

    This session introduces Surah Al-Duha and its relationship to Surah Al-Inshirah as twin surahs (tawamain). The primary theme is Allah's consolation of the Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wasallam) during a period of sadness. The session also includes an important academic discussion on a narration in Sahih al-Bukhari regarding the period of Fatrat al-Wahi.

    Key Themes and Topics

    • Consolation of the Prophet (SAW): Both Surah Al-Duha and Surah Al-Inshirah were revealed to console the Prophet during a difficult period.
    • The pain of criticism from one's own people: The emotional weight of being attacked by family members, not strangers.
    • Fatrat al-Wahi (the pause in revelation): The period when revelation ceased, causing sadness to the Prophet (SAW).
    • Sadness vs. Depression: An important distinction -- sadness is a normal human emotion; depression is a mental illness. The Prophet experienced sadness, never depression.
    • Hadith criticism and academic integrity: A detailed discussion on a narration in Sahih al-Bukhari, the concept of mursal narrations, and the principle that even Sahih al-Bukhari contains narrations that scholars have critiqued.
    • The Principles of Khair and Yusr: Two complementary principles given across the twin surahs.
    • Night and day as metaphors for hardship and relief.

    Main Tafseer Points and Explanations

    Twin Surahs (Tawamain)

    • Surah Al-Duha and Surah Al-Inshirah share the same subject matter and address the same issue.
    • Both were revealed to console the Prophet (SAW).

    Context of Revelation

    • After the Prophet (SAW) began giving da'wah in Mecca, he faced harsh resistance.
    • His own uncle Abu Lahab would walk behind him in public calling him "Majnun" (mad).
    • Criticism from one's own family is more painful than criticism from strangers.
    • Additionally, the Prophet was saddened by Fatrat al-Wahi -- the pause in revelation.
    • Two scholarly opinions on its duration: six months or two and a half years.
    • Both opinions are supported by historical narrations; it is a matter of historical record, not creed.
    • The Prophet had no indication of when (or whether) revelation would resume.
    • He would wait every day and night for Jibreel (AS) to return.

    Timeline Clarification

    • The pause in revelation (Fatrat al-Wahi) occurred right after the first revelation (Iqra/Surah Al-Alaq).
    • After the pause, Surah Al-Muddaththir was revealed.
    • Surah Al-Duha was revealed sometime after Surah Al-Muddaththir.
    • The consolation did not have to be immediate.

    Discussion on the Sahih al-Bukhari Narration

    • A narration in Sahih al-Bukhari, reported by Sayyida Aisha (RA), describes the early period of Islam.
    • Within this narration, there is a portion stating that the Prophet attempted to take his own life during Fatrat al-Wahi and was stopped by Jibreel (AS).
    • This portion is NOT from Sayyida Aisha's narration. It is the inserted statement of Imam al-Zuhri, a Tabi'i in the chain of transmission.
    • Imam al-Zuhri's statement is classified as mursal -- he did not provide the links between himself and the Prophet (SAW).
    • It is the consensus of the muhaddisin that Imam al-Zuhri's mursal narrations are not accepted as valid.
    • Furthermore, this claim contradicts the Quran directly: Surah Al-Qalam states that the Prophet is not majnun (mad) by the blessing of his Lord.
    • Suicidal tendencies are symptoms of mental illness, which is incompatible with prophethood, since prophets must have sound decision-making ability to guide their nations.
    • Imam al-Zuhri was not lying; he was a reliable and distinguished scholar who helped compile hadith during the time of Umar ibn Abdul Aziz. However, even reliable scholars can be less cautious at times, narrating information without full verification.

    Critique of Sahih al-Bukhari -- Academic Perspective

    • Imam Bukhari was human and capable of mistakes.
    • Imam al-Daraqutni criticized 82 ahadith in Sahih al-Bukhari.
    • Various schools of thought (Hanafi, Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali) have not accepted certain narrations in Sahih al-Bukhari, either considering them abrogated (mansoukh) or having stronger counter-narrations.
    • The popular belief that every hadith in Sahih al-Bukhari is beyond question is a common public perception but is not academically sound.
    • The first person to establish the rule of giving Sahih al-Bukhari supreme preference was Ibn al-Salah, and his position has been critiqued by other scholars.

    Ayah 1: "Wa al-Duha" (By the morning brightness)

    • The "waw" is waw al-qasam (oath particle), placing the following noun in the jar (genitive) case.
    • Al-Duha refers to the morning brightness, the time starting approximately 15-20 minutes after sunrise.
    • The word "duha" cannot accept a kasra but is still grammatically in the jar case (kasra is the most common but not the only sign of the jar case).

    Ayah 2: "Wa al-Layli idha saja" (And by the night when it covers with darkness)

    • Also uses waw al-qasam; "al-layl" is in the jar case.

    Connection of the Oaths to the Consolation

    • Night never stays forever; morning always follows. Similarly, morning passes and night returns.
    • This is a subtle indication to the Prophet (SAW): the phase of sadness and hardship will end, just as night gives way to dawn.
    • Duha symbolizes a new beginning -- brightness after darkness.
    • The message: this long phase of sadness will come to an end, and a brighter time will follow.

    Ayah 3: "Ma wadda'aka Rabbuka wa ma qala"

    • "Your Lord has not forsaken you, nor has He been displeased with you."
    • Direct consolation: Allah has not abandoned the Prophet, and revelation will return.

    Ayah 4: "Wa la-l-akhiratu khayrun laka min al-ula"

    • First meaning (general): The Hereafter is better for you than this worldly life (dunya). This applies to all believers. The hadith states: "Dunya is a prison for the believer and a paradise for the disbeliever."
    • Second meaning (specific to the Prophet): The subsequent time -- the future -- will be better for you than this current time of hardship. This was a promise that his situation would improve. In this reading, "akhira" means "the later time" and "ula" means "the current/present time."

    The Two Principles Across the Twin Surahs

    1. Principle of Khair (Surah Al-Duha): The hereafter/future is better -- the principle of goodness.
    2. Principle of Yusr (Surah Al-Inshirah): "With hardship comes ease" -- the principle of ease.
    3. Both khair and yusr are needed for success in dunya and akhira.
    4. Sometimes a person has khair but not yusr (e.g., wealth is good but hard to earn; knowledge is good but requires effort).
    5. Sometimes a person has yusr but not khair (e.g., haram money is easy to earn but is not good; overnight fame through inappropriate means is easy but not beneficial).

    Salat al-Duha and Salat al-Ishraq

    • It is sunnah to offer four rakat at the duha time.
    • The time for Salat al-Duha begins approximately 15-20 minutes after sunrise.
    • Muhaddisin and majority of fuqaha: Ishraq and Duha are the same prayer -- one prayer in the morning after sunrise.
    • Sufi scholars: There are two separate prayers -- Salat al-Ishraq (earlier, right after sunrise) and Salat al-Duha/Chasht (later, about an hour after). The muhaddisin's position is considered stronger.

    Lessons and Practical Takeaways

    • Sadness is normal and human. The Prophet (SAW) experienced sadness, which validates that feeling sad during hardship is a natural and acceptable response. Not being affected by anything would be abnormal.
    • Hardship is temporary. Just as night always gives way to morning, difficult periods in life will pass. This is a universal Quranic principle.
    • Criticism from loved ones hurts most. The surah acknowledges that the deepest pain often comes from those closest to us, not from strangers.
    • Allah does not abandon the believer. The message "Your Lord has not forsaken you" is a source of comfort for anyone going through difficulty.
    • The future holds goodness. Whether understood as the akhira or simply the times ahead, the believer is assured that better days are coming.
    • Success requires both khair and yusr. Goodness without ease is incomplete, and ease without goodness is hollow. Strive for both.
    • Academic rigor in hadith study. Not every narration in a famous collection is beyond critique. Scholars have always exercised critical analysis, and students of knowledge should do the same rather than accepting everything uncritically.
    • Distinguish between sadness and depression. Sadness is a temporary emotional state; depression is a clinical illness. The distinction matters both theologically and practically.

    Historical Context and Stories Mentioned

    • Abu Lahab's public harassment: The Prophet's uncle would follow him in public, calling him "Majnun" (mad), causing significant emotional pain.
    • Fatrat al-Wahi: The period after the first revelation when Jibreel (AS) did not return for an extended time (six months or two and a half years), leaving the Prophet waiting anxiously.
    • Imam al-Zuhri: A prominent Tabi'i and one of the earliest compilers of hadith during the era of Umar ibn Abdul Aziz. His mursal narrations are not accepted by consensus of muhaddisin.
    • Imam al-Daraqutni's critique: He identified and critiqued 82 ahadith in Sahih al-Bukhari, demonstrating that scholarly criticism of even the most respected collections is a legitimate and established practice.
    • Ibn al-Salah: The scholar who first established the principle of giving Sahih al-Bukhari supreme preference over other hadith collections. His position has been critiqued by subsequent scholars.
    • Am al-Huzn (Year of Sadness): Briefly referenced as the year the Prophet's son died -- described in hadith as the "year of sadness," not "year of depression," reinforcing the distinction.
