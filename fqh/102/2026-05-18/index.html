    Fiqh 102 - Study Notes

    Date: 2026-05-18

    Session Overview

    This session covered two main areas: (1) a brief Q&A on the ruling of Qasr (shortened) prayer for travelers, clarifying conditions around residency duration and city limits; and (2) a detailed walkthrough of supplementary rulings (furu') related to wudu, followed by a full enumeration of the eighteen Sunnahs of wudu. The class also included nahw (grammatical) analysis of key sentences from the text, deepening understanding of the Arabic constructions used.

    Key Themes

    1. Traveler vs. Temporary Resident — When a traveler's stay exceeds 15 days, he is reclassified and must pray full prayers.
    2. Supplementary Rulings of Wudu (Furu') — Detailed conditions about beards, nails, rings, and unusual circumstances that affect validity of wudu.
    3. Eighteen Sunnahs of Wudu — A comprehensive list of recommended acts that perfect the wudu.
    4. Grammatical Analysis (Nahw) — Application of shart-jaza (conditional) structure and the difference between fi'l and ism in context.

    Fiqh Rulings & Evidence

    Qasr Prayer and the Traveler's Status

    • A person who intends to stay 15 days or more at a destination is considered a temporary resident (وَطَن الإِقَامَة) and must pray full (4 raka'ah) prayers.
    • A person who intends to stay fewer than 15 days remains classified as a traveler (musafir) and prays Qasr (2 raka'ah).
    • The traveler status begins from the moment he leaves his city limits with the intention of travel.
    • Example given: Traveling from New Jersey → Boston → Maine. If Boston is merely a pit stop (under 15 days), one prays Qasr throughout. If staying in Boston for a month, upon reaching Boston he prays full.

    Conditions Related to Wudu (Furu')

    Condition 6 — Freedom from Major Impurity (حَدَث أَكْبَر)

    • Wudu alone is not sufficient for someone upon whom ghusl (full ritual bath) is obligatory.
    • فَلَا يَكْفِي الوُضُوء لِلَّذِي قَدْ وَجَبَ عَلَيْهِ الغُسْل

    Condition 7 — Restriction of Time (ضِيق الوَقْت)

    • If there is ample time remaining, wudu is not obligatory immediately (عَلَى الفَوْر); delaying is permissible.
    • If time is tight, wudu becomes immediately necessary.

    Conditions & Categories

    Supplementary Rulings of Wudu (فُرُوع تَتَعَلَّق بِالوُضُوء)

    The Beard

    1. Thick beard (كَثَّة): It is obligatory to wash only the outer surface. Khilal (running wet fingers through) is recommended.
    2. Thin beard (خَفِيفَة): Water must reach the skin beneath the beard (بَشَرَة). Washing just the outer surface is insufficient.
    3. Hanging hairs (مُسْتَرْسِل): The hairs that hang below the beard line — it is not obligatory to wash them, nor is it obligatory to wipe them.

    Nails

    1. Obstructions under nails (wax, dough): If there is wax (شَمَع) or dough/flour (عَجِن) under the nails preventing water from reaching the skin, it must be removed and the area washed.
    2. Long nails: If nails grow so long that they cover the fingertips (أَنْمِلَة), it is obligatory to cut them so water can reach the skin beneath.
    3. Dirt and flea droppings: Regular dirt under the nails and droppings of a flea do not prevent water from reaching the skin and do not invalidate wudu.

    Rings

    1. Tight ring (خَاتَم ضَيِّق): If a ring is tight and water cannot reach the skin beneath it without moving it, it is obligatory to rotate/move the ring during wudu.
    2. Loose ring: If the ring is loose and water readily reaches beneath it, moving it is not obligatory.

    Cracked Feet / Medicine

    1. Cracked feet (شُقُوق رِجْلَيْه): If washing the cracks of one's feet would cause harm, it is permissible to pass water over the medicine/bandage applied to those feet (grammatical note: this is a shart-jaza construction — "if washing harms him [shart], then passing water over the medicine is permissible [jaza]").

    Post-Wudu Hair/Nail Changes

    1. Wiping the head then cutting hair: If a person wipes his head in wudu and then gets a haircut or shaves his head, he does not need to re-wipe his head.
    2. Cutting nails or trimming mustache after wudu: Does not require repeating the wudu's washing of those parts.

    The Eighteen Sunnahs of Wudu (سُنَن الوُضُوء)

    These acts are Sunnah — one should perform them so that wudu is done in the most perfect manner (عَلَى وَجْهٍ أَكْمَل):

    # Sunnah Arabic
    1 Making intention before beginning wudu النِّيَّة قَبْل الشُّرُوع
    2 Reciting Bismillah at the start بِسْمِ اللهِ الرَّحْمٰنِ الرَّحِيْم
    3 Washing both hands up to and including the wrists غَسْل اليَدَيْن إلى الرُّسْغَيْن
    4 Using the miswak (tooth-stick) اَلاسْتِيَاك
    5 Using the finger if no miswak is available فَبِالإِصْبَع إِن لَم يَجِد السِّوَاك
    6 Rinsing the mouth (madmada) المَضْمَضَة
    7 Cleaning the nose (istinshaq) الاِسْتِنْشَاق
    8 Thoroughness in rinsing mouth and cleaning nose (not while fasting) المُبَالَغَة فِي المَضْمَضَة وَالاِسْتِنْشَاق إِذَا لَم يَكُن صَائِمًا
    9 Washing each body part three times غَسْل كُل عُضْو ثَلَاث مَرَّات
    10 Wiping the entire head once مَسْح جَمِيع الرَّأْس مَرَّة
    11 Wiping the outer and inner ears مَسْح الأُذُنَيْن ظَاهِرَهُمَا وَبَاطِنَهُمَا
    12 Performing khilal of the beard from below (upward) تَخْلِيل اللِّحْيَة مِن السُّفَل
    13 Performing khilal of the fingers تَخْلِيل الأَصَابِع
    14 Rubbing the limbs when washing them (not just pouring water) تَدْلِيك الأَعْضَاء عِنْد الغَسْل
    15 Washing the second limb before the first dries غَسْل العُضْو الثَّانِي قَبْل جَفَاف العُضْو الأَوَّل
    16 Maintaining the proper order (tartib) of washing: face → hands → wipe head → feet مُرَاعَاة التَّرْتِيب فِي غَسْل الأَعْضَاء
    17 Washing the right hand before the left, and the right foot before the left غَسْل اليَد اليُمْنَى قَبْل اليُسْرَى وَكَذَا الرِّجْل
    18 Beginning the head wipe from the front (forelock area) اِبْتِدَاء المَسْح مِن مُقَدَّم الرَّأْس
    18+ Wiping the nape (back of neck) — قَفَا مَسْح القَفَا

    Note: Wiping the throat (حُلْقُوم) is bid'ah (a reprehensible innovation) and should be avoided. Only the nape is the sunnah.

    Scholarly Opinions

    • The rulings on the beard (thick vs. thin), nails, and rings are consistent with the Hanafi madhab as presented in the text (a continuation from Ascent to Felicity / Maraqi al-Falah, which was referenced as having covered these topics in detail).
    • The 15-day rule for establishing temporary residency and returning to full prayers is the standard Hanafi position.
    • The distinction between watan al-iqama (temporary place of residence where one prays full) and the musafir (traveler praying Qasr) is a well-established Hanafi categorization.

    Lessons & Practical Application

    1. Travel planning matters for salah: Before traveling, determine your intended length of stay. If 15 days or more, plan to pray full prayers from the moment you enter that city. If less, you continue Qasr.

    2. Check your rings: If you wear a tight ring, make it a habit during wudu to slide it and rotate it to ensure water reaches beneath it. Forgetting this invalidates the wudu of that limb.

    3. Keep nails trimmed: Long nails that cover the fingertips are not just a hygiene issue — they make wudu invalid in those areas. Regular nail trimming is a practical Islamic obligation connected to purification.

    4. Beard khilal: During wudu, run wet fingers through the beard from the bottom upward. For a thin beard, ensure water reaches the skin of the face.

    5. Don't rush wudu: The Sunnahs of wudu — especially rubbing the limbs, thorough mouth/nose cleaning, washing three times, and maintaining order — elevate a valid wudu to a perfected one. Aim for these consistently.

    6. Don't wipe the throat: A common mistake is wiping the throat thinking it is sunnah — it is in fact bid'ah. The sunnah is to wipe the nape (back of the neck).

    7. Post-wudu changes don't break it: Getting a haircut, trimming your mustache, or cutting your nails after wudu does not require you to re-do any part of it.

    8. Grammatical awareness enhances understanding: The class illustrated how knowing Arabic grammar (e.g., shart-jaza structures, ism vs. fi'l after كَان) allows one to understand Islamic legal texts with precision and extract rulings correctly.
