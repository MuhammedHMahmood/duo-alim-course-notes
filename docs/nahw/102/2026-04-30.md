## Grammar Rules Covered

### Sound Plurals (Jamʿ Sālim)
**Masculine Sound Plural (Jamʿ Mudhakkar Sālim):**
- Rafaʿ (nominative): ends with ون (wāw + nūn)
- Naṣb and Jarr (accusative/genitive): ends with ين (yāʾ + nūn)
- Example: مُسْلِمُونَ (Muslims came), رَأَيْتُ مُسْلِمِينَ (I saw Muslims)

**Feminine Sound Plural (Jamʿ Muʾannath Sālim):**
- Rafaʿ: ends with ات (alif + tāʾ + ḍammatān)
- Naṣb and Jarr: ends with ات (alif + tāʾ + kasratān)
- Example: جَاءَتْ مُسْلِمَاتٌ, رَأَيْتُ مُسْلِمَاتٍ

### Numbers (20-99) with Nouns
- The numbered noun remains in naṣb (accusative) singular form
- Example: جَاءَ عِشْرُونَ رَجُلاً (Twenty men came)
- The noun stays fixed regardless of the sentence's grammatical requirement

### Relative Adjectives (Ism Mansūb)
- Formation: Add ي (yāʾ) + ّ (shaddah) to place names or concepts
- Purpose: To relate someone to a place, school of thought, or characteristic
- Examples: بَغْدَادِيّ (from Baghdad), شَافِعِيّ (follower of Shāfiʿī)

## I'rab (Sentence Analysis) Examples

**Sound Plural Examples:**
- جَاءَ مُسْلِمُونَ (Muslims came) - مُسْلِمُونَ is marfūʿ (nominative)
- رَأَيْتُ عَامِلِينَ (I saw workers) - عَامِلِينَ is manṣūb (accusative)
- مَرَرْتُ بِمُسْلِمِينَ (I passed by Muslims) - مُسْلِمِينَ is majrūr (genitive)

**Counting Examples:**
- جَاءَ عِشْرُونَ رَجُلاً (Twenty men came) - رَجُلاً remains manṣūb despite جَاءَ
- رَأَيْتُ عِشْرِينَ رَجُلاً (I saw twenty men) - رَجُلاً remains manṣūb

**Relative Adjectives:**
- عَبْدُ الرَّحْمٰنِ الْبَغْدَادِيُّ (Abdurrahman al-Baghdadi)
- الرَّجُلُ الشَّافِعِيُّ (The Shāfiʿī man)

## Practice Examples

### Diminutive Forms (Taṣghīr)
- رَجُل → رُجَيْل (little man)
- حَسَن → حُسَيْن (little Hassan - showing affection)
- عَبْد → عُبَيْد (little slave)
- جَعْفَر → جُعَيْفِر (little Jaʿfar)

### Definite vs. Indefinite
**Definite categories:**
- Pronouns (ضَمَائِر): هُوَ، هِيَ، أَنْتَ
- Proper names (أَعْلام): أَحْمَد، مَكَّة، زَمْزَم
- Demonstrative pronouns: هٰذَا، تِلْكَ، أُولٰئِكَ
- Relative pronouns: الَّذِي، الَّتِي، الَّذِينَ
- Nouns with definite article: الرَّجُل، الْكِتَاب
- Vocative nouns: يَا رَجُلُ (without ال or tanwīn)

**Indefinite:**
- Common nouns without ال: رَجُل، كِتَاب، بَيْت

## Key Takeaways

1. **Sound plurals** maintain their characteristic endings regardless of their grammatical function, making them easier to identify than broken plurals

2. **Context determines meaning** when the same form appears in both naṣb and jarr cases - look at the prepositions and sentence structure

3. **Relative adjectives** are crucial for distinguishing between people with similar names in Islamic texts, especially in ḥadīth chains

4. **Counting from 20-99** requires the counted noun to be in singular naṣb form, regardless of the sentence's grammatical requirements

5. **Feminine markers** (tāʾ marbūṭah, alif maqṣūrah, alif mamdūdah) indicate grammatical gender, but actual usage depends on the referent's real gender

6. **Diminutive forms** serve both grammatical and emotional functions, often expressing affection or endearment

7. **Definiteness** in Arabic has multiple sources beyond just the definite article ال, including proper names, pronouns, and demonstratives
