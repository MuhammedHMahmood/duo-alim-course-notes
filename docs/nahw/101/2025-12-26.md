## Session Overview

Introduction to Al-Mu'rab wal-Mabni (Declinable and Indeclinable Words), covering the fundamental distinction between words that change their endings based on grammatical position versus those that remain constant. This is a crucial concept in Arabic grammar that forms the foundation for understanding sentence structure and word function.

---

## Grammar Rules Covered

### Al-Mu'rab (Declinable Words)
- **Definition**: A word whose ending can change based on its grammatical position
- Can accept all three ḥarakāt (Ḍamma, Fatḥa, Kasra) or two out of three
- Changes according to case: Rafʿ (Ḍamma), Naṣb (Fatḥa), Jarr (Kasra)
- Examples: Zaydun → Zaydan → Zaydin; Al-Masjidu → Al-Masjida → Al-Masjidi

### Al-Mabnī (Indeclinable Words)
- **Definition**: A word whose ending never changes regardless of grammatical position
- Maintains constant ending in all three cases (Rafʿ, Naṣb, Jarr)
- Case determined by context and position, not by visible ending changes
- Example: Hādhā remains Hādhā whether subject, object, or after preposition

### Types of Mabnī Words

#### Original Mabnī (Al-Mabnī Al-Aṣl)
1. **All particles (Ḥurūf)**: Min, ʿalā, fī, an - never change
2. **Fiʿl Māḍī**: All 14 forms remain constant (Faʿala, Faʿalā, Faʿalū, etc.)
3. **Fiʿl Amr**: All 6 forms of imperative verbs (Ifʿal, Iqra', Uḍrib, Unṣur)

#### Derived Mabnī
4. **Certain Fiʿl Muḍāriʿ forms**: 
   - Jamʿ Mu'annas forms (Yafʿalna, Tafʿalna)
   - Forms with Nūn at-Tawkīd (heavy and light nūn)

5. **Nouns with resemblance to original Mabnī** (4 types of resemblance):

**a) Resemblance in Meaning**
- Nouns giving meaning of verbs
- Example: Ruwaydā (meaning "give respite") - resembles Amhil

**b) Resemblance in Dependency** 
- Nouns dependent on other words for complete meaning
- Example: Hādhā (this) - needs Mushār ilayh like Hādhā al-baytu
- Resembles particles which also need other words

**c) Resemblance in Having Less Than Three Letters**
- Example: Man (who) - resembles Min (from) in having only two letters

**d) Resemblance in Originally Containing a Ḥarf**
- Example: Aḥada ʿashara (eleven) - originally Aḥadun wa ʿashrūn
- The wāw (conjunction) was originally present

## I'rab (Sentence Analysis) Examples

### Demonstrating Mabnī Words:
1. **Jāʾa Hādhā** (This came)
   - Hādhā: Fāʿil, Rafʿ case, but ending doesn't change

2. **Ra'aytu Hādhā** (I saw this)
   - Hādhā: Mafʿūl, Naṣb case, but ending doesn't change

3. **Marartu bi Hādhā** (I passed by this)
   - Hādhā: Majrūr, Jarr case, but ending doesn't change

### Context Determines Case:
- Case recognition through sentence position and context
- No visible ḥaraka changes in Mabnī words
- Grammatical function understood through syntax

## Practice Examples

### Sentence Analysis (identifying Mabnī words):

1. **Qara'tu Hādhā al-Kitāba** (I read this book)
   - Qara'tu: Mabnī (Fiʿl Māḍī + pronoun)
   - Hādhā: Mabnī (Ism Ishāra)
   - al-Kitāba: Muʿrab (changes based on case)

2. **Dhahaba ilā ʿAmmihi** (He went to his uncle)
   - Dhahaba: Mabnī (Fiʿl Māḍī)
   - ilā: Mabnī (Ḥarf Jarr)
   - ʿAmmihi: Muʿrab (changes based on case)

3. **Kitābuhu fī Baytihi** (His book is in his house)
   - fī: Mabnī (Ḥarf Jarr)
   - Other words: Muʿrab

## Key Takeaways

1. **Fundamental Distinction**: Every Arabic word is either Muʿrab (declinable) or Mabnī (indeclinable)

2. **Recognition Strategy**: Mabnī words maintain constant endings; context determines their grammatical case

3. **Five Categories**: Original Mabnī includes particles, past tense verbs, and imperatives; derived Mabnī includes certain present tense forms and nouns with specific resemblances

4. **Four Types of Resemblance**: Meaning, dependency, letter count, and original ḥarf content determine when nouns become Mabnī

5. **Practical Application**: Understanding this concept is essential for proper sentence analysis and grammatical comprehension
