## Session Overview

This session covered two hadith:

## Hadith Text and Chain of Narration

### Hadith 34

**Narrator:** Abu Sa'id al-Khudri رضي الله عنه

**Text:**

> مَنْ رَأَى مِنْكُمْ مُنْكَرًا فَلْيُغَيِّرْهُ بِيَدِهِ، فَإِنْ لَمْ يَسْتَطِعْ فَبِلِسَانِهِ، فَإِنْ لَمْ يَسْتَطِعْ فَبِقَلْبِهِ، وَذَٰلِكَ أَضْعَفُ الْإِيمَانِ

> "Whosoever sees any evil, he should change it with his hand. If he is unable to, then with his tongue. If he is unable to, then with his heart — and that is the weakest form of iman."

**Source:** Narrated by Imam Muslim. The session also references a supporting narration on the authority of Tariq ibn Shihab regarding an incident with Marwan ibn al-Hakam.

---

### Hadith 35

**Topic:** Huquq al-Ukhuwwah — Rights of Brotherhood in Islam

**Text (paraphrased from lesson):**

> "Do not be resentfully jealous of one another. Do not artificially inflate prices against one another (najash). Do not loathe one another. Do not give a cold shoulder to one another. Do not undercut one another in business transactions. And be servants of Allah as brothers to one another. A Muslim is a brother to another Muslim — he does not oppress him, does not let him down, and does not look down upon him. Taqwa is here" — and the Prophet ﷺ pointed to his chest — "It is evil enough for a person that he looks down upon his Muslim brother. A Muslim is entirely inviolable."

---

## Key Themes

1. **Al-Amr bil-Ma'ruf wa al-Nahy 'an al-Munkar** — Commanding good and forbidding evil is an established Islamic obligation (fard kifayah — communal obligation).
2. **Three Tiers of Changing Evil** — Hand (action) → Tongue (speech) → Heart (conviction); each level reflects diminishing capacity but sustained obligation.
3. **Wisdom and Gentleness in Reminding** — The manner of reminding matters; harshness can push people further away from the deen.
4. **Limits of Individual Responsibility** — Once a person has fulfilled their part of reminding, the burden shifts; no one is held accountable beyond their capacity.
5. **Brotherhood in Islam** — Comprehensive framework of rights Muslims owe one another, grounded in equality before Allah and consciousness of His oversight.
6. **Taqwa as the True Measure** — Allah does not look at wealth, status, or appearance but at the heart and deeds; taqwa is the sole distinguishing criterion.

## Explanation and Commentary

### Hadith 34 — Forbidding Evil: Three Tiers

**The Obligation:** The heading for this hadith describes it as an *established Islamic obligation* (faridhah islamiyyah muhkamah). Changing evil is not optional or voluntary — it is an obligation upon the Muslim community.

**Nature of the Obligation (Fard Kifayah):** This is a *communal* obligation. If a sufficient number of Muslims fulfil it, the entire community is absolved. If no one fulfils it, the entire community is sinful. However, if an evil is something that only a particular individual has knowledge of, it becomes a personal obligation solely upon that individual.

**The Three Tiers Explained:**

| Level | Method | Condition |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | Hand (action/authority) | When one has the power and authority to physically change the situation |
| 2nd | Tongue (speech/reminding) | When one lacks authority but can still speak out |
| 3rd | Heart (internal rejection) | When neither action nor speech is possible; minimum iman |

The word **فَلْيُغَيِّرْهُ** (he should change it) is a command form, indicating obligation, not mere recommendation.

---

**Supporting Incident — Marwan ibn al-Hakam and the Eid Khutbah:**

Imam Muslim narrates on the authority of Tariq ibn Shihab that Marwan ibn al-Hakam (who was the ruler/authority at the time) began to deliver the Eid khutbah *before* Salatul Eid — contrary to the established Sunnah (the prayer should precede the sermon). A man from the crowd stood up and told him this was incorrect.

Marwan's response was that people had already abandoned the practice of staying for the khutbah after the prayer, so he moved it before — a pragmatic but unacceptable deviation from the Sunnah.

Abu Sa'id al-Khudri رضي الله عنه commented that this man had "discharged his duty" — he had fulfilled his obligation by speaking up to a person in authority.

**Why did Abu Sa'id himself not speak up?** The teacher offered two explanations:
1. Abu Sa'id may not have been present when Marwan initially stood up; he arrived later and saw the man already addressing Marwan.
2. Abu Sa'id, out of *hikmah* (wisdom), may have judged that his own intervention would cause greater fitnah or difficulty — knowing Marwan's character, his public objection could have made the situation worse. He therefore remained silent, which was itself an exercise of wisdom, not negligence.

This shows that the *method* and *timing* of forbidding evil requires judgment — wisdom (hikmah) is an integral part of the obligation.

---

**Key Nuances Discussed:**

- **Non-compliance by the addressee:** If a person is reminded and they refuse or react adversely, the Sharia lifts the obligation from the one who reminded. You are not required to physically force compliance. The Quranic principles apply: *"Allah does not burden a soul beyond its capacity"* and *"No soul shall bear the burden of another."*

- **Personal perfection is not a condition:** A person does not need to be in a perfect spiritual state themselves before they can remind others. The obligation to remind does not depend on one's own level of practice.

- **Not assuming someone won't accept:** A person should not use the excuse that "I know this person — they won't listen" as a reason to forgo the reminder. Even if you know the person is stubborn or unlikely to accept, the obligation to remind is not thereby waived.

- **Gentleness is essential:** The manner of reminding must be gentle and kind. Harshness can push the person further from the deen. The teacher cited the example of the Bedouin who urinated in the masjid — the Prophet ﷺ restrained the Sahabah from rebuking him harshly, allowed him to finish, and then gently explained the sanctity of the masjid.

- **No spying:** A person does not search out people's sins or spy on private homes. The obligation to forbid evil applies to what one comes across openly or has clear knowledge of. Only in extreme cases would entering a private space to stop a major sin be considered.

---

**Reminding Scholars and Religious Authorities (Student Question):**

The teacher addressed a question about reminding scholars or imams who may be in error:

- No one is above the Sharia. Scholars, imams, and those in religious authority are all human and can err.
- The standard is always the Sharia and the consensus of the Ummah.
- Regarding scholarly ijtihad: if a scholar does his due diligence and is correct, he receives two rewards; if he errs, he receives one reward (since he exerted effort for the sake of Allah). This does not mean errors are ignored.
- The **method** of reminding a scholar should be adapted:
  - If there is someone close to the scholar — a trusted friend, sibling, or community member — it may be more effective for a third party to raise the concern on behalf of others.
  - The goal is the well-being of the scholar and the community, not to embarrass or undermine.
  - One's intention must be sincere (nasiha), not to assert superiority or control.

### Hadith 35 — Rights of Brotherhood in Islam

The Prophet ﷺ enumerated six prohibitions and then established the positive principle of brotherhood:

**1. Do not harbour hasad (resentful jealousy)**
- *Hasad*: wishing to have what another has AND wishing that the other person loses it — this is blameworthy.
- *Ghibtah* (envy without ill-will): wishing you also had what the other person has, while making dua for them — this is permissible.

**2. Do not engage in najash**
- Najash is when someone acts as a "shill" for a seller — making fake bids to artificially inflate the price, with no intention of actually purchasing, in order to squeeze a higher price out of a genuine buyer. This is explicitly forbidden.

**3. Do not loathe one another (la tabaghadu)**
- Hatred is a matter of the heart. The Prophet ﷺ instructs that hearts should always be kept clean of hatred towards fellow Muslims.

**4. Do not give one another the cold shoulder**
- Muslims should not cut off, ignore, or shun fellow Muslims (beyond the legitimate limits set by Sharia).

**5. Do not undercut one another in business transactions**
- Example: If a person has bought an item and is still within the return period, a rival seller approaches them and offers to sell the same item cheaper, encouraging them to return the first item — this is forbidden. It undermines existing transactions and creates enmity.

**6. Be brothers to one another**
- Muslims should deal with each other as blood brothers: wanting the best for one another, supporting each other, acting with compassion, kindness, and generosity.

**Core Principle — A Muslim is a brother to a Muslim:**
- Does not oppress him
- Does not let him down
- Does not humiliate or look down upon him

**Taqwa is in the heart:** The Prophet ﷺ pointed to his chest three times. This connects to another hadith: *"Allah does not look at your bodies and your faces, but He looks at your hearts and your deeds."* Consciousness of Allah is the foundation — a person who truly fears Allah will not wrong his brother, because he knows Allah is watching.

**Looking down upon a Muslim:** The Prophet ﷺ said it is enough evil for a person to look down upon his Muslim brother. In the masjid, everyone stands equal — wealth, status, and social rank mean nothing. The only distinction before Allah is taqwa.

## Fiqh Rulings Derived

1. **Changing evil with the hand is obligatory** where a person has the authority and capacity to do so. It is a command (amr), not a recommendation.
2. **Fard kifayah (communal obligation):** The obligation of forbidding evil is satisfied for the community if some do it; if none do it, all are sinful.
3. **Personal obligation:** Where only one person has knowledge of an evil, it falls solely upon them.
4. **Obligation is lifted** once a person has reminded without result — they are not accountable for the choices of others.
5. **Najash is forbidden** — artificial price manipulation by a shill buyer is haram.
6. **Undercutting a transaction within its return window is forbidden** — approaching a buyer to cancel an existing sale and switch to you is impermissible.
7. **Hasad is forbidden**; ghibtah (non-resentful envy) is permissible.
8. **Oppression of a Muslim is forbidden** — a Muslim must help his brother if he is being oppressed, to the best of his ability.
9. **Looking down upon a Muslim is sinful** — it is described as sufficient evil in itself.
10. **Scholarly ijtihad:** A scholar who performs proper ijtihad receives one reward if wrong, two if correct — this does not exempt errors from being addressed through appropriate channels.

## Lessons and Takeaways

1. **Enjoining good and forbidding evil is not optional.** It is a foundational Islamic obligation and an expression of wanting the best for one's community — an extension of *al-din al-nasiha* ("religion is sincere counsel").

2. **The three tiers are a complete framework.** No Muslim is ever truly without an avenue — if action and speech are impossible, the heart must at minimum reject evil. Losing this last level signifies a serious weakness of iman.

3. **Wisdom is part of the obligation.** Speaking out requires knowing *how*, *when*, and *to whom* — as seen in Abu Sa'id's calculated silence versus the other man's courageous public reminder. Both responses were legitimate given their different circumstances.

4. **The obligation does not require personal perfection.** A Muslim in an imperfect state is still obligated to remind their brothers and sisters. Waiting until one is "perfect" would mean the obligation is never fulfilled.

5. **Don't pre-judge the outcome.** Assuming someone will not accept a reminder is not a valid excuse to withhold it. The responsibility is to deliver the reminder — the response belongs to the other person and to Allah.

6. **Brotherhood is active and practical.** It means zero tolerance for resentful jealousy, business manipulation, and social contempt — and a genuine commitment to another's wellbeing as if they were family.

7. **Taqwa is the equalizer.** Wealth, status, appearance — none of it distinguishes people before Allah. The only thing that elevates one Muslim above another is their consciousness of Allah. This should reshape how we look at and treat every Muslim we encounter.

8. **Allah is always watching.** The Prophet's gesture to his chest — "taqwa is here" — is a reminder that the heart is the seat of all action. If a person truly internalises that Allah sees all, they will never oppress, humiliate, or wrong their Muslim brother or sister.
