## Session Overview

- Surah Al-Ma'un consists of 7 ayat
- Named after the last word of the surah: "Al-Ma'un"
- Position of the majority of Mufassirin: it is a Makki (Meccan) surah
- Some Mufassirin hold that the surah was revealed in two parts (first part in Makkah, second in Madinah), because the latter portion deals with Munafiqeen who did not exist in Makkah. However, the majority view is that the entire surah is Makki, since Allah knew Munafiqeen would later exist and wanted to prepare the believers in advance.

---

## Connection to Previous Surahs

This surah is part of a sequence following Surah Al-Asr, identifying obstacles that prevent a person from acting upon its message (iman, good deeds, mutual enjoining of truth, and mutual enjoining of patience):

| # | Obstacle | Surah |
|---|----------|-------|
| 1 | **Capitalism** - excessive love of wealth/capital | Surah Al-Humazah |
| 2 | **Imperialism / Colonialism** - love of power and domination | Surah Al-Fil |
| 3 | **Tribalism / Nationalism** - pride in tribe or nation | Surah Quraysh |
| 4 | **Fake Religiosity** - insincere worship and showing off | Surah Al-Ma'un |

**Direct connection to Surah Quraysh:** The Quraysh were given guardianship (tawliyah) of the Ka'bah and commanded to worship its Lord. Over time, they developed tribal pride from this guardianship while maintaining a pretense of religiousness. This insincerity (nifaq) eventually led to shirk.

## Ayah-by-Ayah Tafseer

### Ayah 1: "Have you seen the one who denies the Day of Judgment?"
- A person who practices religion in a fake manner is, through his behavior (not necessarily his words), denying the Day of Judgment (Yawm al-Din)
- If he truly believed in accountability before Allah, he would not merely pretend to be religious
- Fake religiosity is itself an implicit denial of divine accountability

### Ayah 2: "That is the one who repulses the orphan"
- **First side effect of fake religiosity:** Loss of mercy and kindness
- When a person believes there is no accountability, he becomes tyrannical (like Fir'awn, Haman, Shaddad)
- He pushes away orphans and refuses to help them

### Ayah 3: "And does not encourage the feeding of the poor"
- **Second side effect:** Extreme stinginess/miserliness
- Two meanings: (1) He does not feel the urge to feed the poor himself, (2) He does not even encourage others to feed the poor
- Encouraging others to spend is far easier than spending oneself, yet he refuses even that -- indicating the highest level of stinginess

### Ayah 4-6: "Woe to those who pray, who are neglectful of their prayer, who show off"
- **Third side effect:** Prayer without sincerity; showing off (riya')
- They pray but have no heart in their prayer; they simply go through the motions
- No consideration of sunan (recommended acts), mustahabbat (desirable acts), or adab (etiquette) of salah
- Allah condemns them despite their praying because the prayer is not for His sake

### Ayah 7: "And they deny small kindnesses (Al-Ma'un)"
- **Fourth side effect:** Withholding even trivial favors
- Examples: refusing to give someone a cup of water, refusing to make dua for someone, refusing to use one's influence to recommend someone when it would be easy to do so
- Demonstrates extreme harshness and selfishness

---

## Nahw (Grammar) Analysis of Ayat 4-6

- "Fa" is a conjunction particle (harf 'atf)
- "Waylun" is khabar muqaddam (fronted predicate)
- "Lil-musalleen alladhina hum 'an salatihim sahoon" is mubtada' mu'akhkhar (delayed subject)
- "Alladhina" is ism mawsul (relative pronoun); "hum 'an salatihim sahoon" is its silah (relative clause)
- "Hum" is mubtada'; "sahoon 'an salatihim" is the khabar

## Historical Accounts and Stories

### Abu Jahl and the Orphans
- Abu Jahl (whose real name was Abu al-Hakam) would exploit dying men: he would offer to become the caretaker of their wealth and children
- After the person's death, he would seize the entire wealth and give nothing to the orphan
- One such orphan came to Rasulullah (sallallahu alayhi wa sallam) and complained
- The Prophet went to Abu Jahl and advised him; Allah placed such awe (ru'b) in Abu Jahl's heart that he returned the wealth to the orphan
- This illustrates the behavior of those who "repulse the orphan"

### Hadith: Developing Kindness Through Caring for Orphans
- A sahabi complained to the Prophet that he felt harshness in his heart
- The Prophet advised him to place his hand on the head of an orphan (i.e., show kindness and care for orphans)
- Over time, this practice would develop genuine kindness in one's heart

### Hadith: Two Types of People
- The Prophet said that Allah has removed the pride of Jahiliyyah (pre-Islamic ignorance)
- Now people are only of two types:
  - **Barrun Taqiyyun Kareemun 'ala Allah** -- righteous, God-fearing, and noble in the sight of Allah
  - **Fajirun Shakiyyun** -- sinful, wretched, and non-practicing

## Summary: Four Side Effects of Fake Religiosity

1. **Repulsing the orphan** -- loss of mercy and kindness
2. **Not feeding / not encouraging feeding the poor** -- extreme stinginess
3. **Showing off in prayer** -- worship without sincerity (riya')
4. **Denying small favors (al-ma'un)** -- withholding even trivial kindnesses

These can also be condensed into two core problems:
- **Not having genuine kindness**
- **Being fake in one's religious practice**

---

## Lessons and Practical Takeaways

1. **Sincerity (ikhlas) is the foundation of all worship.** Without it, even prayer becomes condemnable. Religiosity without sincerity is worse than it appears, because it is a form of implicit denial of the Day of Judgment.

2. **Self-check against fake religiosity.** If a person finds in himself the combination of showing off, stinginess, harshness toward the weak, and unwillingness to do even small favors, this is a warning sign.

3. **Caring for orphans softens the heart.** The Prophet's practical advice to develop kindness was to spend time with and care for orphans.

4. **Even small acts of kindness matter.** Islam emphasizes not only major acts of charity but also trivial daily favors -- giving someone water, making dua for someone, or using one's influence to help others.

5. **Surah Al-Ma'un is the fourth in a series of warnings** about what prevents a person from fulfilling the program of Surah Al-Asr (faith, good deeds, enjoining truth, and enjoining patience). After greed, imperialism, and tribalism, fake religiosity is the final obstacle identified.

6. **Fake religiosity leads to spiritual degeneration.** The Quraysh example shows that insincerity (nifaq) eventually degenerates into outright shirk over time.

7. **There is no middle ground.** As the hadith states, a person is either righteous and God-fearing, or sinful and wretched. Pretending to be religious while lacking sincerity places one in the latter category.

## Additional Topics Discussed

### Shah Waliullah's Theory of Irtifaqat (Civilizational Progression)
- Found in his books *Hujjatullah al-Balighah* and *Al-Budur al-Bazighah*
- Four levels (irtifaqat):
  1. **Individual level** -- personal conduct and development
  2. **Societal level** -- how a society should behave
  3. **Regional level** -- how different societies in the same region interact; includes formation of regional government
  4. **International level** -- how nations deal with each other internationally; includes the concept of an international governing body (similar to the United Nations) -- proposed roughly 300 years ago

### Brief Discussion on Attitude Toward Muslim Rulers Who Secularize
- **First approach (majority position):** One should not rebel against rulers or cause anarchy, even if one disagrees with their policies
- **Second approach:** Based on the hadith that obedience to the ruler is required unless one witnesses "kufran bawahan" (open, undeniable disbelief) -- some scholars argue secularization constitutes open kufr
- Key principle: Even legitimate criticism of rulers must not lead to anarchy, bloodshed, or loss of life and property, which the Sharia strictly opposes
- **Abu Hanifa's position:** He gave moral support to rebellion against unjust rulers but did not openly participate or (to the shaykh's knowledge) provide material support
