## Session Overview

This session focused on the passive voice (Majhul) forms of past tense verbs in Arabic morphology, including how to form the passive from the active (Ma'ruf), how to negate verbs using **ما** and **لا**, and an extensive practical exercise parsing Arabic sentences. The teacher led a detailed grammatical analysis of ten practice sentences, covering Jumlatul Ismiyah vs. Jumlatul Fi'liyah identification, Mubtada-Khabar structures, and the critical rule that when the Khabar is a full sentence it must contain a pronoun (Dhamir) referring back to the Mubtada.

## Word Patterns (Awzan) Covered

### Active (Ma'ruf) → Passive (Majhul) — Madi Form

The conversion rules for the past tense verb from active to passive:

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| **First letter** | Always takes a **Dhamma** (ضَمَّة) in Majhul |
| **Middle (second root) letter** | Always takes a **Kasra** (كَسْرَة) in Majhul |
| **Last letter** | Unchanged — keeps the same Haraka as the active form |

### Conversion Examples

| Active (Ma'ruf) | Passive (Majhul) | Meaning (Active → Passive) |
|---|---|---|
| نَصَرَ (nasara) | نُصِرَ (nusira) | He helped → He was helped |
| سَمِعَ (sami'a) | سُمِعَ (sumi'a) | He heard → He was heard |
| كَرُمَ (karuma) | كُرِمَ (kurima) | He was noble → He was honored |

> Note: Regardless of whether the active form has Fatha, Kasra, or Dhamma on the middle letter, the Majhul form **always** standardizes to Dhamma on first letter + Kasra on middle letter.

### Negation Patterns (Manfi)

| Particle | Used With | Condition |
|---|---|---|
| **ما** (ma) | Madi (past tense) | Can be used freely before the verb |
| **لا** (la) | Madi (past tense) | Must be **repeated** (cannot appear only once) |

**Examples:**
- نَصَرَ → **مَا نَصَرَ** (He did not help)
- نُصِرَ → **مَا نُصِرَ** (He was not helped)

### Vocabulary Multiplication Insight

Knowing one root pattern multiplies vocabulary systematically:
- 1 active verb pattern × 14 conjugations = **14 forms**
- + passive (Majhul) = **28 forms**
- + negation with ما = **28 more → 56 forms**
- + negation with لا = even more

## Verb Conjugation Tables

### Madi Majhul — Full Pattern

The Majhul Madi follows the same conjugation slots as the Ma'ruf Madi. Only the voweling changes (Dhamma on first letter, Kasra on middle letter). Example root: ن-ص-ر

| Pronoun | Ma'ruf (Active) | Majhul (Passive) |
|---|---|---|
| هُوَ | نَصَرَ | نُصِرَ |
| هِيَ | نَصَرَتْ | نُصِرَتْ |
| هُمَا (m) | نَصَرَا | نُصِرَا |
| هُمَا (f) | نَصَرَتَا | نُصِرَتَا |
| هُمْ | نَصَرُوا | نُصِرُوا |
| هُنَّ | نَصَرْنَ | نُصِرْنَ |
| أَنْتَ | نَصَرْتَ | نُصِرْتَ |
| أَنْتِ | نَصَرْتِ | نُصِرْتِ |
| أَنْتُمَا | نَصَرْتُمَا | نُصِرْتُمَا |

> The last letter retains its Haraka from the Ma'ruf pattern. Only first and middle letters change.

## Root Analysis

### Na'ib Fa'il — The Deputy Doer

- In a **Ma'ruf** (active) verb sentence, the verb requires a **Fa'il** (doer). If not explicitly stated, it is a hidden pronoun (Dhamir Mustatir).
- In a **Majhul** (passive) verb sentence, the actual doer is unknown or omitted. The grammatical subject position is filled by the **Na'ib Fa'il** (deputy doer).
- The Na'ib Fa'il is always **Marfu** (nominative), just like a Fa'il.
- The Na'ib Fa'il is typically what was the **Maf'ul Bihi** in the active sentence — i.e., the receiver of the action is promoted to the grammatical subject position, but it is still semantically the receiver.

**Example:**
- Active: هُوَ قَرَأَ الْقُرْآنَ → He read the Quran (الْقُرْآنَ = Maf'ul bihi, Mansub)
- Passive: قُرِئَ الْقُرْآنُ → The Quran was read (الْقُرْآنُ = Na'ib Fa'il, Marfu — note Dhamma)

### Critical Rule: Khabar as a Sentence

When the **Khabar** of a Jumlatul Ismiyah is itself a full sentence (Jumlatul Fi'liyah or Jumlatul Ismiyah), that sentence **must contain a Dhamir (pronoun) that refers back to the Mubtada**. Without this back-reference pronoun, the sentence is grammatically broken.

**Example from class:**
- هُوَ قَرَأَ الْقُرْآنَ
  - هُوَ = Mubtada
  - قَرَأَ الْقُرْآنَ = Khabar (Jumlatul Fi'liyah)
  - The hidden Fa'il (هُوَ) inside قَرَأَ is the back-reference pronoun → **valid**
  - If you wrote هُوَ قَرَأَتْ الْقُرْآنَ → the Ta refers to a female (هِيَ), not هُوَ → **invalid**

### Why هُوَ قَرَأَ and Not Just قَرَأَ?

- قَرَأَ الْقُرْآنَ alone is a valid complete sentence (Jumlatul Fi'liyah): "He read the Quran."
- Adding هُوَ at the start (making it Jumlatul Ismiyah) adds **emphasis and specificity**: it was specifically he — no one else — who did the reading. The Mubtada asserts exclusivity or confirmation.

## Practice Examples

### Sentence Parsing Exercises (from class)

**Sentence 1: هُوَ قَرَأَ الْقُرْآنَ**
- Type: **Jumlatul Ismiyah** (starts with detached pronoun هُوَ)
- هُوَ = Mubtada (Marfu, detached pronoun)
- قَرَأَ الْقُرْآنَ = Khabar (Jumlatul Fi'liyah)
  - قَرَأَ = Fi'l Madi, Mabni
  - Fa'il = Dhamir Mustatir (هُوَ) — back-reference to Mubtada ✓
  - الْقُرْآنَ = Maf'ul Bihi (Mansub)

**Sentence 2: قُرِئَ الْقُرْآنُ (Majhul)**
- Type: **Jumlatul Fi'liyah** (starts with verb)
- قُرِئَ = Fi'l Madi Majhul
- الْقُرْآنُ = Na'ib Fa'il (Marfu — note Dhamma on Nun, vs. Fatha in sentence 1)

**Sentence 3: هُمَا كَتَبَتَا مَكْتُوبَيْن**
- Type: **Jumlatul Ismiyah**
- هُمَا = Mubtada
- كَتَبَتَا مَكْتُوبَيْن = Khabar (Jumlatul Fi'liyah)
  - كَتَبَتَا = Fi'l + Fa'il built-in (Alif of duality = Na'ib reference back to هُمَا) ✓
  - مَكْتُوبَيْن = Maf'ul Bihi (Mansub — duality in Mansub/Majrur form, hence يْن not ان)
- Why كَتَبَتَا and not كَتَبَ? Because هُمَا (two females) governs the verb; the Alif duality ending refers back to the Mubtada هُمَا.

**Sentence 4: اَلْبِنْتَانِ طُلِبَتَا**
- Type: **Jumlatul Ismiyah**
- اَلْبِنْتَانِ = Mubtada (definite noun, Marfu — duality form with ان)
- طُلِبَتَا = Khabar (Jumlatul Fi'liyah — Fi'l Majhul)
  - طُلِبَتَا = Fi'l Majhul; Alif duality = Na'ib Fa'il (refers back to البنتان) ✓
- Translation: "The two daughters were sought/called."
- Why طُلِبَتَا and not طُلِبَتْ? The duality Alif is the Na'ib Fa'il and must match البنتان.

**Sentence 5: أَنْتَ أَكَلْتَ التُّفَّاحَةَ**
- Type: **Jumlatul Ismiyah** (starts with detached pronoun أَنْتَ)
- أَنْتَ = Mubtada
- أَكَلْتَ التُّفَّاحَةَ = Khabar (Jumlatul Fi'liyah)
  - أَكَلْتَ = Fi'l + attached Ta (Dhamir Muttasil, Marfu, Fa'il — refers back to أَنْتَ) ✓
  - التُّفَّاحَةَ = Maf'ul Bihi (Mansub)
- Note: تَ in أَكَلْتَ is the attached pronoun for أَنْتَ and is always Marfu as the Fa'il.

**Sentence 6: أَنْتُمْ بُعِثْتُمْ إِلَى مَكَّةَ**
- Type: **Jumlatul Ismiyah**
- أَنْتُمْ = Mubtada
- بُعِثْتُمْ إِلَى مَكَّةَ = Khabar (Jumlatul Fi'liyah)
  - بُعِثْتُمْ = Fi'l Majhul; تُمْ = Na'ib Fa'il (Dhamir Muttasil, Marfu — refers back to أَنْتُمْ) ✓
  - إِلَى مَكَّةَ = Harf Jarr + Majrur (Jar-Majrur phrase); Mutaliq (connected) to بُعِثَ
- Translation: "You all were sent to Mecca." (The sentence-initial أَنْتُمْ implies: you — specifically, no choice — must go)

**Sentence 7: أَنْتِ طَلَبْتِ الْعِلْمَ**
- Type: **Jumlatul Ismiyah**
- أَنْتِ = Mubtada
- طَلَبْتِ الْعِلْمَ = Khabar (Jumlatul Fi'liyah)
  - طَلَبْتِ = Fi'l + تِ (Dhamir Muttasil, Marfu, Fa'il — refers back to أَنْتِ) ✓
  - الْعِلْمَ = Maf'ul Bihi (Mansub)
- Any conjugation other than طَلَبْتِ would break the back-reference to أَنْتِ.

**Sentence 8: أَنْتُنَّ بُعِثْتُنَّ إِلَى الْمُسْتَوَى**
- Type: **Jumlatul Ismiyah**
- أَنْتُنَّ = Mubtada
- بُعِثْتُنَّ إِلَى الْمُسْتَوَى = Khabar (Jumlatul Fi'liyah)
  - بُعِثْتُنَّ = Fi'l Majhul; نَّ = Na'ib Fa'il (Dhamir Muttasil, Marfu — refers back to أَنْتُنَّ) ✓
  - إِلَى الْمُسْتَوَى = Harf Jarr + Majrur; Mutaliq to بُعِثَ

**Sentence 9: نَحْنُ شَرِبْنَا لَبَنًا**
- Type: **Jumlatul Ismiyah**
- نَحْنُ = Mubtada
- شَرِبْنَا لَبَنًا = Khabar (Jumlatul Fi'liyah)
  - شَرِبْنَا = Fi'l + نَا (Dhamir Muttasil, Marfu, Fa'il — refers back to نَحْنُ) ✓
  - لَبَنًا = Maf'ul Bihi (Mansub, indefinite)

**Sentence 10: مَا فَهِمْنَا كَلَامَكَ**
- Type: **Jumlatul Fi'liyah** (negated verbal sentence)
- مَا = Harf Nafi (negating particle) — does not determine sentence type; look at what follows
- فَهِمْنَا = Fi'l Madi (negated by ما); نَا = Fa'il (Dhamir Muttasil, Marfu)
- كَلَامَكَ = Maf'ul Bihi (Mansub)
- Translation: "We did not understand your speech."
- Note: ما here is one of its 10+ uses; context determines which use applies.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Majhul formation is mechanical**: Dhamma on first letter + Kasra on middle letter, last letter unchanged. Apply this to any Ma'ruf verb to get its Majhul.

2. **Na'ib Fa'il is not a Fa'il**: It is the receiver of the action, not the doer — but it fills the grammatical doer position and is always Marfu. This distinction matters for meaning and parsing.

3. **Every detached pronoun (Dhamir Munfasil) beginning a sentence = Jumlatul Ismiyah, and that pronoun is always the Mubtada.** No analysis needed — it's definitive. Examples: هُوَ، هِيَ، هُمَا، أَنْتَ، أَنْتِ، أَنْتُمْ، نَحْنُ etc.

4. **The Mubtada is the "king"**: Once the Mubtada is established, everything in the sentence — especially the Khabar — must comply with it in terms of number, gender, and pronoun back-reference.

5. **Critical rule — Khabar as a sentence**: If the Khabar is a Jumlatul Fi'liyah (or Jumlatul Ismiyah), it **must contain a pronoun that refers back to the Mubtada**. The verb ending (attached pronoun as Fa'il or Na'ib Fa'il) serves as this back-reference. Using the wrong conjugation breaks this link and invalidates the sentence.

6. **Sentence type is determined by what comes after a particle**: Particles like ما and لا do not themselves indicate whether a sentence is nominal or verbal — look at the word that follows the particle.

7. **Jar-Majrur phrases (حرف جرّ + مجرور) are Mutaliq**: They must attach to a verb or verb-like entity. They are not standalone sentence components.

8. **Maf'ul Bihi takes Mansub; Na'ib Fa'il takes Marfu**: This is the key distinction seen in الْقُرْآنَ (Maf'ul, active sentence) vs. الْقُرْآنُ (Na'ib Fa'il, passive sentence).

9. **Vocabulary compounds rapidly**: One root × patterns × passive × negation = dozens of derivable forms. Mastering structure, not memorization, is the goal.

10. **Serious study outside class is essential**: Class sessions are primers. Solid Arabic acquisition requires dedicated personal study time beyond scheduled classes.
