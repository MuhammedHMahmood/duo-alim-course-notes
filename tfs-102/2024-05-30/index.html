    TFS 102 - Study Notes

    Date: May 30, 2024 Surah Covered: Surah Al-Quraysh (Chapter 106)

    Key Themes and Topics

    • Tribalism and Nationalism: The central theme of the surah is Allah's condemnation of tribalism. The Quraysh developed a sense of pride based on their noble lineage and status as guardians of the Ka'bah. Allah reminds them that their privilege is a blessing requiring gratitude, not arrogance. This critique extends to modern nationalism -- taking excessive pride in one's nation, placing national identity above all else including the Quran and Sunnah.

    • Connection of Four Surahs to Surah Al-Asr: Four consecutive surahs address barriers that prevent a person from success (as outlined in Surah Al-Asr):

    • Surah Al-Humazah -- condemns capitalism (hoarding wealth, materialism)
    • Surah Al-Feel -- condemns colonialism/imperialism (Abraha's attempt to colonize Makkah)
    • Surah Al-Quraysh -- condemns tribalism/nationalism

    • Surah Al-Ma'un -- condemns fake religiosity (to be covered in the next class)

    • Gratitude and Worship: Allah instructs the Quraysh to worship the Lord of the Ka'bah in recognition of the blessings He bestowed upon them -- provision (food) and security.

    • The Ka'bah and the Refutation of Idol Worship: An important theological clarification: Muslims face the Ka'bah in prayer but do not worship it. The distinction between facing a direction and worshipping an object.

    Grammatical Points

    • Li-ilafi -- The lam is a harf jar (preposition) and must be connected to something. Two opinions exist on what it connects to (see Tafseer Points below).
    • Ilaf is a masdar (verbal noun). Just as a verb has a fa'il (subject) and maf'ul (object), a masdar can also have its own fa'il and maf'ul.
    • Rihlata is in the nasab case because it is the maf'ul of the masdar ilaf (mudaf/mudaf ilayh construction).
    • Quraysh is a tasgheer (diminutive) form of the original word "Qurashi." Here the tasgheer is used in an honorary sense.
    • Ism al-maf'ul (passive participle) like ma'kul can take harf jar and majrur connections just as a verb can -- this is an advanced nahw (grammar) concept.
    • The Basran and Kufan schools of grammar (nahw) did contribute to differences in ijtihad among the Imams. Imam Abu Hanifa's approach aligned with the Kufan school, as he was from Kufa.

    Main Tafseer Points and Explanations

    Structure and Connection of "Li-ilaf"

    Two scholarly opinions on what the preposition "li" in "li-ilafi Quraysh" connects to:

    1. Connected to the last word of Surah Al-Feel ("ma'kul"): Allah made the army of Abraha like eaten-up straw because of the familiarity/status of the Quraysh. The lam connects backward to the previous surah.

    2. Connected to "fal ya'budu" later in the surah: Because of the familiarity of the Quraysh with their journeys, they should worship the Lord of this House. The lam connects forward within the same surah.

    Historical Background

    • Nadr bin Kinanah -- the 12th grandfather of the Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wasallam). His descendants are called Quraysh.
    • Hashim -- the Prophet's great-grandfather (grandfather of Abdul Muttalib). He was the one who advised the people of Makkah to adopt trade as a profession since agriculture was impossible in the barren valley of Makkah.
    • Ibrahim (alayhi salam) described Makkah as a valley with no farming produce when he settled Ismail (alayhi salam) there.
    • The Quraysh traded in two directions:
    • Yemen (south) in winter
    • Sham/Syria (north) in summer (since Sham's winters were harsh with snow)
    • Some Quraysh built large commercial empires. They had an equitable profit-sharing system that controlled the wealth gap among them.
    • The nearby market in Jeddah was where they purchased grains (rice, wheat, barley).

    Two Blessings Mentioned in the Surah

    1. "At'amahum min ju'" (Fed them against hunger): Before Hashim's recommendation of trade, the people of Makkah lived in dire economic conditions. Their only income came from the Hajj season. Hashim's advice to pursue trade, blessed by Allah, solved their hunger problem.

    2. "Amanahum min khawf" (Gave them security from fear): After the defeat of Abraha, the entire Arabian Peninsula recognized Makkah as a special, divinely protected place. The Quraysh were respected everywhere. Their caravans traveled without guards -- simply identifying themselves as Qurayshi was enough to guarantee safe passage.

    The Ka'bah -- Direction vs. Object of Worship

    • Allah says "Rabb hadhal bayt" (Lord of this House), not just "this House." Even the Ka'bah has a Lord above it.
    • Evidence that Muslims do not worship the Ka'bah:
    • Throughout the entire salah, from takbir to taslim, the word "Ka'bah" is never mentioned in praise. All praise is directed to Allah.
    • If the Ka'bah were moved from its location, Muslims would continue facing the original direction, not follow the structure. Idol worshippers, by contrast, follow the idol wherever it is placed.
    • If the Ka'bah were destroyed, prayer would not be interrupted. The Ka'bah has been rebuilt multiple times in history, and Muslims continued praying during reconstruction.
    • What matters is the direction (qiblah), not the physical structure itself.

    Lessons and Practical Takeaways

    1. Privilege demands gratitude, not pride. Being born into a noble family, a powerful nation, or favorable circumstances is a blessing from Allah. Greater blessings carry greater responsibility. Taking pride in them is a form of ingratitude.

    2. The Prophet's example of humility: He said he will be the leader of the children of Adam on the Day of Judgment, "wa la fakhr" -- but he takes no pride in it. Leadership and honor are met with humility, not arrogance.

    3. Nationalism is the modern form of tribalism. Just as the Quraysh took excessive pride in their tribal identity, modern people do the same with their national identities. The Quranic condemnation of tribalism applies equally to nationalism.

    4. "My country first" is a problematic statement when it means placing national interest above the Sharia, the Quran, and the Sunnah. Borders are man-made and temporary. The land belongs to Allah. People routinely leave their nations for better opportunities, which exposes nationalism as an inconsistent ideology.

    5. The Islamic framework recognizes Ummah, not nation. The concept of nation-state is a modern construct. While living in a nation-state is a practical reality, one's belief system should maintain that Islam and its teachings are superior to any national allegiance.

    6. The four barriers to success (capitalism, colonialism, tribalism, fake religiosity) are interconnected obstacles that prevent practicing the four pillars of success in Surah Al-Asr (iman, righteous deeds, enjoining truth, enjoining patience).

    7. Story of Abu Jahl at Badr illustrates extreme tribalism: even while mortally wounded and defeated, Abu Jahl mocked Abdullah ibn Mas'ud (radiyallahu anhu) as "the son of a shepherd" and boasted of his own Qurayshi lineage. Tribalistic pride can persist even in the face of death.

    8. Worship must be directed to the Creator, not the creation. The Ka'bah is honored as a direction of prayer, but all worship, praise, and devotion belong exclusively to Allah -- the Lord of the House, not the House itself.

    Q&A Highlights

    • Q: Can one say "my Muslim nation is superior because it aligns more closely with Sharia"? A: The word "nation" carries specific modern connotations tied to the nation-state concept. It is better to use the word "Ummah." One can say that a particular country is closer to the Sharia, and therefore living there is preferable -- that is not haram. But the framing of national superiority should be avoided.

    • Q: Did the differences between the Basran and Kufan schools of grammar affect the ijtihad of the Imams? A: Yes, in some cases. Imam Abu Hanifa's grammatical understanding aligned with the Kufan school, as he was from Kufa.
