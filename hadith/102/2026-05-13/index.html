    Hadith 102 - Study Notes

    Date: 2026-05-13

    Session Overview

    This session covered Hadiths 30–35 from the Hadith 102 curriculum. The class primarily focused on translating and explaining four hadith, with detailed commentary on two of them: Hadith 30 (on respecting Allah's boundaries and remaining silent on unlegislated matters) and Hadith 31 (on Zuhd — non-attachment to the world). Hadiths 32–35 were translated with brief introductory remarks, to be discussed in more detail in future sessions. Key overarching themes include submission to divine command, contentment, legal principles in Islam, and the rights of Muslim brotherhood.

    Hadith Text and Chain of Narration

    Hadith 30 — Hudud Allah (The Boundaries of Allah)

    Narrator: Abu Tha'laba al-Khushani (رضي الله عنه)

    "Verily Allah has obligated certain works — so do not squander them. And Allah has set boundaries — فَلَا تَعْتَدُوهَا — so do not transgress them. And Allah has made certain things impermissible — فَلَا تَنْتَهِكُوهَا — so do not violate them. And He has remained silent about certain things — رَحْمَةً لَكُمْ — out of mercy for you, not out of forgetfulness — فَلَا تَبْحَثُوا عَنْهَا — so do not go searching for them."

    Chapter Title: Al-Wuquf 'Inda Hudud al-Shar' — Standing by / stopping at the boundaries of Islamic law.

    Hadith 31 — Zuhd and Its Fruits

    Narrator: Abu'l-Abbas Sahl ibn Sa'd al-Sa'idi (رضي الله عنه)

    A man came to the Prophet ﷺ and said: "O Messenger of Allah, tell me of an action that, if I were to perform it, Allah would love me and the people would love me." The Prophet ﷺ said:

    اِزْهَدْ فِي الدُّنْيَا يُحِبُّكَ اللهُ، وَازْهَدْ فِيمَا عِنْدَ النَّاسِ يُحِبُّكَ النَّاسُ

    "Show non-attachment to the world, and Allah will love you. Show non-attachment to what the people possess, and the people will love you."

    Chapter Title: Al-Zuhdu wa Thamaratuhu — Zuhd and Its Fruits.

    Hadith 32 — No Harm in Islam

    Narrator: Abu Sa'id Sa'd ibn Malik ibn Sinan al-Khudri (رضي الله عنه)

    لَا ضَرَرَ وَلَا ضِرَارَ

    "There is no harm, and there is no reciprocating harm."

    Chapter Title: Same as the hadith text.

    Hadith 33 — The Foundation of Judgment in Islam

    Narrator: Abdullah ibn Abbas (رضي الله عنه)

    "If people were given based merely on their claim, people would certainly claim the wealth and lives of others. However:

    لَكِنِ الْبَيِّنَةُ عَلَى الْمُدَّعِي، وَالْيَمِينُ عَلَى مَنْ أَنْكَرَ

    Presenting proof is upon the one who makes the claim, and an oath is upon the one who denies."

    Chapter Title: Asas al-Qada' fil-Islam — The Pillar/Foundation of Judgment in Islam.

    Hadith 34 — Removing Evil is an Obligation

    Narrator: Abu Sa'id al-Khudri (رضي الله عنه)

    "Whosoever from among you sees an evil — فَلْيُغَيِّرْهُ بِيَدِهِ — he should change it with his hand. If he is unable, فَبِلِسَانِهِ — then with his tongue. If he is unable, فَبِقَلْبِهِ — then with his heart. And that is the weakest form of Iman."

    Chapter Title: Izalat al-Munkar Farida Islamiyya Muhkama — The Removal of Evil is a Clear and Binding Islamic Obligation.

    Hadith 35 — Rights of Brotherhood in Islam

    Narrator: Abu Hurayra (رضي الله عنه) (implied from context)

    "Do not be jealous of one another. Do not artificially inflate prices against one another (Najash). Do not loathe one another. Do not give one another the cold shoulder (Tadabur). Do not undercut one another in business transactions.

    وَكُونُوا عِبَادَ اللهِ إِخْوَانًا

    Be, O servants of Allah, brothers.

    الْمُسْلِمُ أَخُو الْمُسْلِمِ، لَا يَظْلِمُهُ، وَلَا يَخْذُلُهُ، وَلَا يَكْذِبُهُ، وَلَا يَحْقِرُهُ

    A Muslim is the brother of another Muslim — he does not oppress him, does not let him down, does not lie to him, and does not look down upon him.

    Taqwa is here — (pointing to his chest three times). It is evil enough for a man to look down upon his fellow Muslim.

    كُلُّ الْمُسْلِمِ عَلَى الْمُسْلِمِ حَرَامٌ: دَمُهُ وَمَالُهُ وَعِرْضُهُ

    Everything belonging to a Muslim is inviolable to another Muslim: his life, his property, and his honor."

    Chapter Title: Huquq al-Ikhwa fil-Islam — The Rights of Brotherhood in Islam.

    Key Themes

    1. Submission to Divine Command Without Demanding Rationale — The primary obligation is to follow what Allah has commanded and avoid what He has forbidden, because Allah has commanded or forbidden it. Searching for a justification before complying undermines the essence of faith.
    2. Silence of the Shariah as Mercy — What Allah has not legislated is an intentional mercy; exploring and forcing rulings on such matters can become a trial, as was the case with Bani Israel.
    3. Zuhd — Non-Attachment to the Dunya — The antidote to jealousy, restlessness, and social rivalry. Detachment from worldly possessions earns Allah's love; detachment from coveting others' possessions earns people's love.
    4. Contentment (Qana'ah) — True peace of mind comes from gratitude for what one has, not from accumulation. Even those with millions may lack contentment, while the person of modest means who is grateful is truly rich.
    5. Legal Principles — Burden of Proof — The claimant must present evidence; the defendant swears an oath. This foundational Islamic legal principle remains operative in courts today.
    6. No Harm Principle (La Darar) — A core maxim of Islamic jurisprudence: causing harm and retaliating with harm are both prohibited.
    7. Commanding Right and Forbidding Wrong — A graded obligation upon every Muslim, from physical intervention to verbal objection to heartfelt rejection.
    8. Rights of Muslim Brotherhood — Islam establishes comprehensive mutual rights: protection of life, wealth, and honor; prohibition of jealousy, deception, contempt, and social exclusion.

    Explanation and Commentary

    Hadith 30 — Hudud Allah

    The hadith establishes four divine categories governing human conduct:

    1. Obligatory acts (Fara'id): Allah has commanded these; they must not be wasted or abandoned.
    2. Prohibited acts: Allah has set firm limits; these must not be crossed or violated.
    3. Forbidden things (Muharramat): Explicitly declared impermissible; must not be violated.
    4. Unlegislated matters (Maskut 'anhu): Allah has deliberately remained silent — out of mercy, not forgetfulness. These must not be probed or forced into legislation.

    The Rationalism Trap: The teacher emphasized a major contemporary challenge — the demand for rational justification before obeying religious commands ("Why do I have to pray five times? Why is this haram?"). The teacher was clear: the primary reason to obey is that Allah has commanded it. That itself is sufficient cause.

    Related Hadith: The Prophet ﷺ said: "Leave me alone for as long as I leave you" — meaning, act on what is commanded without excessive questioning. The people before (Bani Israel) were destroyed because their incessant questioning became a trial for them. Their prophets would give answers, and those very answers would bind them in new difficulties.

    Key Lesson: Silence of the Shariah is divine mercy. Forcing questions into domains Allah left open can convert that mercy into difficulty. The scholar's role is not to create rulings where none exist without need.

    Hadith 31 — Zuhd

    A companion asked the Prophet ﷺ for a single action that would earn both the love of Allah and the love of people. The answer was a two-part formula:

    • Detachment from the dunya → Allah's love. A person who does not chase worldly accumulation, status, and material excess draws near to Allah.
    • Detachment from what others have → People's love. When a person stops coveting what others possess, he ceases to compete, envy, or resent — and people naturally feel at ease around him.

    Why coveting others' wealth destroys relationships: The teacher explained the "rat race" phenomenon — constant social comparison creates resentment. A person who always looks at what others have will never be satisfied, will always try to compete, and will end up despising those around him.

    The Gratitude Ladder: The teacher gave a powerful descending analogy: - Person with a car → be grateful, someone walks - Person who walks → be grateful, someone takes the bus - Person on a bus → be grateful, someone uses a wheelchair - Person in a wheelchair → be grateful, someone is fully immobile - The fully immobile → be grateful, someone is not alive at all

    The conclusion: there is always someone with less. Gratitude is always possible.

    Related Hadith: "كُنْ فِي الدُّنْيَا كَأَنَّكَ غَرِيبٌ أَوْ عَابِرُ سَبِيلٍ" — "Be in this world as if you are a stranger or a passerby." We are guests in this world; a guest cannot take the host's furniture home. The only thing we take with us is our deeds.

    The Greatest Ni'mah: Above health, wealth, and peace of mind — the greatest blessing is Islam itself. It gives a person the framework to have genuine peace of mind, which even millionaires who take sleeping pills to sleep cannot find.

    Personal Anecdote from Teacher: Teachers who earn minimal salaries, sometimes with only dollars left at month's end, remain content and happy. Meanwhile, those with millions in liquid assets cannot sleep without medication. Contentment is not a function of wealth.

    Hadith 32 — La Darar wa La Dirar

    This is one of the five foundational legal maxims of Islamic jurisprudence (al-qawa'id al-fiqhiyya al-kubra). It prohibits: - La Darar: Initiating harm upon another - La Dirar: Retaliating with harm in return

    This maxim undergirds vast areas of Islamic law — from contracts and transactions to medical ethics and family law. Full explanation deferred to a later session.

    Hadith 33 — The Foundation of Islamic Judiciary

    This hadith establishes the foundational legal principle that governs all Islamic courts:

    • The burden of proof lies on the claimant (المدعي). A person cannot receive something simply by asserting it belongs to them. They must bring bayyinah — clear evidence.
    • The defendant (من أنكر) takes an oath (يمين) by Allah that the claim against them is false.

    Why this matters: Without this principle, anyone could claim another's property or even demand their execution simply by making an assertion. The Prophet ﷺ pointed out this obvious danger: "People would certainly claim the wealth and lives of those around them."

    The Weight of Allah's Name: The oath system works because swearing falsely by Allah is not a trivial act. A person who swears falsely in court may escape human judgment, but will face divine accountability — the consequence of which is far more severe. This reverence for Allah's name is what gives the oath its legal weight and moral deterrent force.

    Hadith 34 — Changing Evil (Three Levels)

    The Prophet ﷺ established three graduated levels of obligation for addressing wrong:

    Level Method Who It Applies To
    1st Change with the hand (physical intervention) Those with authority/power
    2nd Speak out with the tongue Those with the ability to speak
    3rd Reject it in the heart Every Muslim without exception

    The third level is described as the weakest form of Iman — not because it is optional, but because it is the bare minimum. A Muslim who cannot even feel displeasure in his heart at an evil has a serious deficiency in faith.

    Hadith 35 — Rights of Brotherhood

    The hadith prohibits several social diseases:

    • Hasad (jealousy/envy): Wishing the removal of another's blessing
    • Najash (artificial price inflation): Fraudulent bidding to harm another in business
    • Tabaghhud (loathing): Harboring hate for another Muslim
    • Tadabur (cold shoulder): Deliberately turning away from another Muslim
    • Undercutting in trade: Corrupt business competition aimed at harming another

    The positive commands balance these prohibitions: be servants of Allah and brothers to one another. The mutual rights are clearly enumerated — no oppression, no abandonment, no lies, no contempt.

    Taqwa lives in the heart: The Prophet ﷺ pointed to his chest three times when mentioning taqwa (God-consciousness). Its seat is the heart, not the tongue or appearance.

    The test of contempt: The Prophet ﷺ said it is sufficient evil for a person to look down on his Muslim brother — meaning this alone, without any other sin, is a serious enough moral failure.

    Fiqh Rulings Derived

    1. Obligatory acts must not be abandoned; divine limits must not be crossed — both are binding upon every Muslim. (Hadith 30)
    2. Inquiring into matters Allah left unlegislated is disliked (makruh) or prohibited when it risks creating hardship where none was intended. (Hadith 30)
    3. The burden of proof in Islamic courts rests on the claimant (المدعي), not the defendant. (Hadith 33)
    4. The defendant's oath (يمين) by Allah is a recognized evidentiary mechanism in Islamic courts. (Hadith 33)
    5. Causing harm (darar) and retaliating with harm (dirar) are both prohibited under Islamic law — this is a foundational legal maxim (qawida fiqhiyya). (Hadith 32)
    6. Forbidding evil (nahy 'an al-munkar) is obligatory upon every Muslim according to capacity — with the hand, tongue, or heart respectively. (Hadith 34)
    7. Najash (artificial price inflation) is prohibited in Islamic commercial law. (Hadith 35)
    8. A Muslim's life (dam), wealth (mal), and honor ('ird) are inviolable (haram) to every other Muslim. (Hadith 35)
    9. Oppression (zulm), abandonment (khidhlan), lying (kadhib), and contempt (ihtihar) toward a fellow Muslim are all prohibited. (Hadith 35)

    Lessons and Takeaways

    1. Obedience comes first, understanding comes second. The question "why?" has its place, but it must not become a prerequisite for compliance. The earlier nations were destroyed by excessive questioning. Trust Allah's wisdom even when the rationale is not apparent.

    2. The silence of Shariah is a gift. Allah did not legislate everything deliberately — this is mercy, not oversight. Forcing rulings onto silent matters can convert ease into hardship.

    3. Zuhd is not poverty — it is freedom. Non-attachment to the dunya does not mean owning nothing; it means that what you own does not own you. The result is the best of both worlds: divine love and people's love.

    4. Stop the comparison trap. Coveting what others have is the root of jealousy, resentment, and relational breakdown. Shift focus to what Allah has given you — and that list is immeasurably long.

    5. Gratitude is always available. No matter one's condition, there is always someone with less. Contentment is a choice of perspective, not a product of circumstances.

    6. The greatest blessing is Islam. Wealth cannot buy peace of mind; Islam provides the framework for it. Recognizing this is the beginning of genuine contentment.

    7. Justice requires evidence, not just assertion. The Islamic legal system protects against false claims by requiring proof from the accuser. This principle continues to operate in legal systems worldwide.

    8. The oath is sacred. Swearing by Allah's name in a court is not a formality — it is a recognition that human courts have limits, and divine accountability has none.

    9. Silence in the face of evil is the bare minimum of faith. Every Muslim must at minimum feel displeasure in their heart at wrong. Losing this sensitivity is a mark of weakened iman.

    10. Brotherhood is not a sentiment — it is a set of rights. The Prophet ﷺ enumerated specific prohibitions and obligations that define Muslim brotherhood. It is an active, practiced commitment, not merely a feeling.
