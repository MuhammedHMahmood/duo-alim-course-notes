    Nahw 101 - Study Notes¶

    Date: 2025-10-16

    Session Overview¶

    This session covered lesson 6 on Arabic pronouns (al-dhamir). The class completed a comprehensive review of pronoun types and their grammatical cases. Students learned about visible vs. invisible pronouns, attached vs. detached pronouns, and their usage in different grammatical cases (Rafa, Nasab, Jar). The instructor emphasized the importance of memorizing pronoun tables and understanding their contextual applications.

    Grammar Rules Covered¶

    Types of Pronouns (Al-Dhamir)¶

    Primary Classification: 1. Bariz (Visible) - pronouns that can be seen and pronounced 2. Mustatir (Invisible/Hidden) - pronouns that are grammatically present but not visible

    Secondary Classification of Bariz: 1. Muttasil (Attached) - pronouns attached to other words 2. Munfasil (Detached) - pronouns written separately

    Grammatical Cases for Each Type:¶

    Damir Bariz Muttasil (Visible Attached Pronouns): - Used in all three cases: Rafa, Nasab, Jar - Rafa case: fa'alta (you did) - pronoun serves as fa'il (doer) - Nasab case: darabaka (he hit you) - pronoun serves as maf'ul (object) - Jar case: kitabuhu (his book) - pronoun in idafa construction

    Damir Bariz Munfasil (Visible Detached Pronouns): - Used in only two cases: Rafa and Nasab - Rafa case: hua Abdullah (he is Abdullah) - pronoun as mubtada (subject) - Nasab case: iyyaka na'budu (You we worship) - pronoun as maf'ul for emphasis

    Damir Mustatir (Hidden Pronouns): - Used only in Rafa case - Found in verbs where the subject pronoun is implied - Example: qara'a (he read) - "he" is understood but not written

    Important Vocabulary¶

    Arabic Term Meaning
    al-dhamir pronoun (singular)
    al-dhamair pronouns (plural)
    bariz visible
    mustatir invisible/hidden
    muttasil attached
    munfasil detached
    fa'il doer/subject
    maf'ul object
    mubtada subject (in nominal sentence)
    khabar predicate
    mudhaf first part of possessive construction
    mudhaf ilayh second part of possessive construction
    murakab idafi possessive construction
    na'ib al-fa'il substitute of the doer (passive voice)

    I'rab (Sentence Analysis) Examples¶

    fa'alta (you did) - fa'alta: fi'l madi (past verb) - ta: damir bariz muttasil fi mahal rafa fa'il

    darabaka (he hit you) - daraba: fi'l madi (past verb) - (invisible pronoun): damir mustatir fi mahal rafa fa'il - ka: damir bariz muttasil fi mahal nasab maf'ul bih

    kitabuhu (his book) - kitabu: mudhaf marfu bi'l-dhamma - hu: damir bariz muttasil fi mahal jar mudhaf ilayh

    hua Abdullah (he is Abdullah) - hua: damir bariz munfasil fi mahal rafa mubtada - Abdullah: khabar marfu bi'l-dhamma

    Practice Examples¶

    Visible vs. Hidden Pronouns: - kitabuhu (his book) - "hu" is visible (bariz) - qatala (he killed) - "he" is hidden (mustatir)

    Attached vs. Detached: - kitabuhu (his book) - "hu" attached to "kitab" (muttasil) - hua Abdullah (he is Abdullah) - "hua" separate from "Abdullah" (munfasil)

    Case Identification: - Look at grammatical function, not just vowel marks - fa'il and mubtada → Rafa case - maf'ul → Nasab case
    - mudhaf ilayh and after harf jar → Jar case

    Key Takeaways¶

    1. Memorization Required: Students must memorize both pronoun tables from lesson 6 - unattached pronouns (table 6.1) and attached pronouns

    2. Case Recognition: Vowel marks (dhamma, fatha, kasra) are common but not exclusive indicators of grammatical cases. Context and grammatical function determine the actual case

    3. Pronoun Distribution:

    4. Bariz Muttasil: can be in all three cases (Rafa, Nasab, Jar)
    5. Bariz Munfasil: only in two cases (Rafa, Nasab)

    6. Mustatir: only in Rafa case

    7. Practical Application: Understanding pronouns is essential for sentence construction and translation between Arabic and English

    8. Future Learning: Detailed charts for mustatir pronouns will be covered in the next textbook (Tasheer Al-Nahu)

    9. Study Method: Repeat pronoun conjugations 50+ times for memorization, following the pattern: hua, huma, hum, hiya, huma, hunna, etc.
