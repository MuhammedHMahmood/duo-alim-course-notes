    Nahw 102 - Study Notes

    Date: 2026-05-05

    Session Overview

    This session covered Arabic grammatical number (العدد) in depth, focusing on how Arabic distinguishes between singular, dual, and plural. The class walked through the formation rules for the dual (التثنية), sound plurals (الجمع السالم), and broken plurals (الجمع المكسّر), with special attention to the restricted plural (جمع القلة) and unrestricted plural (جمع الكثرة). The session also touched on counting rules for numbers 3–10, the gender-flipping rule in counting, and the concept of plural of plurals (جمع الجمع). I'rab analysis methodology (sentence vs. phrase identification) was practiced throughout using live examples.

    Grammar Rules Covered

    1. Arabic Grammatical Number (العدد)

    Arabic distinguishes three distinct numbers:

    Number Arabic Term Definition
    Singular المفرد Default form; refers to exactly one
    Dual المثنى / التثنية Refers to exactly two
    Plural الجمع Refers to three or more

    2. Forming the Dual (التثنية)

    Rule: Add ألف + نون مكسورة (Alif + Noon with Kasra) to the end of the singular word.

    Critical sub-rule — the Alif constraint: - Alif cannot carry any haraka (it is always preceded by a Fatha). - Therefore, whatever letter immediately precedes the Alif must have a Fatha — even if the original letter had a Dhamma, Kasra, Dhammatain, or Kasratain. - The Noon carries a Kasra (Noon Maksura) — this is fixed by design.

    Formation by grammatical state:

    State (الحال) Ending Added Example (رجل → two men)
    Marfu (مرفوع) ـَانِ (Alif + Noon Kasra) رَجُلَانِ
    Mansub / Majrur (منصوب / مجرور) ـَيْنِ (Ya Sukun + Noon Kasra) رَجُلَيْنِ

    Why the Dhamma disappears: When Alif is inserted after the Lam of رجل, the Lam's double Dhamma (ضمتان) is replaced by a single Fatha to accommodate the Alif. This is not optional — it is a structural requirement.

    Examples of dual formation:

    Singular Meaning Dual (Marfu) Dual (Majrur/Mansub)
    رَجُلٌ one man رَجُلَانِ رَجُلَيْنِ
    كِتَابٌ one book كِتَابَانِ كِتَابَيْنِ
    دَارٌ one house دَارَانِ دَارَيْنِ
    مِصْبَاحٌ one lamp مِصْبَاحَانِ مِصْبَاحَيْنِ
    أَحْمَد Ahmad (a name) أَحْمَدَانِ أَحْمَدَيْنِ

    3. The Noon Drops in Idhafa (الإضافة)

    Rule: When a dual word becomes the Mudaf (المضاف) in an Idhaafa construction, the Noon is dropped.

    Examples:

    Original Dual Idhaafa Form Meaning
    قَلَمَانِ قَلَمَا زَيْدٍ The two pens of Zayd
    مِصْبَاحَانِ مِصْبَاحَا حَسَّانٍ The two lamps of Hassan

    Note: Noon dropping in Idhafa is not exclusive to the dual. It also occurs in the sound male plural (جمع المذكر السالم) when it becomes Mudaf. Example: مُسْلِمُو مِصْرَ (the Muslims of Egypt) — original was مُسْلِمُونَ but Noon dropped due to Idhafa.

    4. Types of Plural (أنواع الجمع)

    A. Sound Plural (الجمع السالم)

    The singular word's structure remains intact (سالم = sound/unbroken).

    i. Sound Male Plural (جمع المذكر السالم): - Marfu: add ـُونَ → مُسْلِمُونَ - Mansub/Majrur: add ـِينَ → مُسْلِمِينَ

    ii. Sound Female Plural (جمع المؤنث السالم): - Marfu: add ـَاتٌ (Alif + Ta + Damma) → مُسْلِمَاتٌ - Majrur/Mansub: add ـَاتٍ (Alif + Ta + Kasratain) → مُسْلِمَاتٍ

    B. Broken Plural (الجمع المكسَّر / جمع التكسير)

    The internal structure of the singular word is broken and reformed. There is no single predictable rule — the pattern (وزن) must be learned word by word, though scholars have identified common scales (أوزان).

    5. Restricted Plural — Jam'ul Qilla (جمع القلة)

    Definition: A plural that denotes a number from 3 to 10. It signals a relatively small (though still plural) quantity.

    The four common Awzan (scales) of Jam'ul Qilla:

    Wazan Example Singular Example Plural Meaning
    أَفْعُل — أَقْلَام pens (قَلَم → أَقْلَام)
    أَفْعَال قَوْل أَقْوَال statements
    أَفْعِلَة طَعَام أَطْعِمَة foods
    فِعْلَة غُلَام غِلْمَة young men/boys

    Note: This does not mean these words cannot refer to more than 10 in context. In Quranic usage especially, context determines the precise meaning. The classification is a grammatical and morphological label used by scholars to group plural patterns.

    Related term — بِضْع (Bid'): A specific Arabic word meaning any number between 3 and 9 (sometimes stated as 3–10). Example usage: in Surah Ar-Rum, فِي بِضْعِ سِنِينَ ("within a few years") — this was the context of the famous wager between the Muslims and the disbelievers regarding the Roman-Persian war.

    6. Unrestricted Plural — Jam'ul Kathira (جمع الكثرة)

    Definition: A plural that denotes 10 or more (or an unspecified large quantity).

    Common Awzan (scales) of Jam'ul Kathira:

    Wazan Example Singular Example Plural Meaning
    فِعَال عَبْد عِبَاد servants/slaves
    فُعُول رَسُول رُسُل messengers
    فُعَلَاء عَالِم عُلَمَاء scholars
    فُعَلَاء فَقِيه فُقَهَاء jurists
    فُعَلَاء شَهِيد شُهَدَاء martyrs
    فُعَلَاء فَقِير فُقَرَاء poor people

    Important pedagogical note from the Sheikh: Do not over-focus on memorizing the Wazan labels at the early stage. Instead, learn the pair — singular and its plural — and gradually recognize the pattern through exposure. The Wazan is a reference model (like a "model house") that helps you identify the pattern, not something to be mechanically applied.

    7. Plural of Plurals (جمع الجمع)

    Definition: A plural formed from another plural word, indicating an exceptionally large or emphatic quantity.

    Example:

    Singular First Plural Plural of Plural Meaning
    نِعْمَة أَنْعُم أَنَاعِم countless/overwhelming blessings
    بَيْت (house) بُيُوت أَبْيَات (contextual) very many houses

    8. Noun Gender (التأنيث) — Sami'i Feminines (التأنيث السماعي)

    Some nouns are grammatically feminine without any visible sign (no تاء مربوطة or other marker). These are called السماعي (heard/oral) — we know they are feminine because the Arabs used them as feminine words.

    Examples of Sami'i feminines: - نَار (fire) — feminine, no sign - رُوح (spirit/soul) — feminine - دَار (house) — feminine - نَفْس (self/soul) — feminine - قَلْب (heart) — feminine

    9. Counting Rule: Gender Flip for Numbers 3–10

    Rule: When counting with numbers 3 to 10, the gender of the number is opposite to the gender of the singular of the noun being counted.

    Singular Noun Gender of Singular Gender Used for Number Example
    كِتَاب (book) Masculine Feminine number ثَلَاثَةُ كُتُبٍ
    نَفْس (soul) Feminine Masculine number ثَلَاثُ أَنْفُسٍ
    رَجُل (man) Masculine Feminine number أَرْبَعَةُ رِجَالٍ

    Critical point: The gender of the number is determined by the gender of the singular of that word — NOT the plural. So even if the broken plural of كِتَاب (which is كُتُب) is treated as non-human feminine, you still look at كِتَاب (masculine) to determine the number's gender.

    Counting buckets (different rules apply for each range): - 1–2: Standard singular/dual - 3–10: Gender-flip rule applies - 11–19: Different rule - 20–99: Different rule - 100+: Different rule - 1000+: Different rule

    The Sheikh noted this is a challenging area even for non-native Arabic scholars and requires extensive practice and exposure to become natural.

    10. I'rab Analysis Methodology

    Step-by-step process for any group of words:

    1. Identify word types — Is any word a verb (فعل)? If not, all words are nouns (أسماء).
    2. Ask: sentence or phrase? — A sentence requires a verb, or a Mubtada-Khabar structure.
    3. If phrase: what type? — At least 15 types of phrases exist. Use the D.I.N.G. framework to identify adjective phrases (المركّب الوصفي / الموصوف والصفة):

    D.I.N.G. — Four agreement conditions for Mausuf-Sifa (موصوف وصفة):

    Letter Condition Meaning
    D تعريف / تنكير Definiteness (both definite or both indefinite)
    I إعراب Same case ending (Raf', Nasb, or Jarr)
    N عدد Same number (singular, dual, or plural)
    G جنس Same gender (masculine or feminine)

    Idhafa identification clues: - The Mudaf (المضاف) has no Alif-Lam (ال) and no Tanwin - The Mudaf ilayh (المضاف إليه) is in the Majrur (مجرور) state - The Mudaf ilayh may or may not have Alif-Lam

    Important Vocabulary

    Arabic Term Meaning
    التثنية / المثنى Dual; the grammatical form for exactly two
    الجمع Plural (three or more)
    الجمع السالم Sound plural (structure of singular preserved)
    جمع المذكر السالم Sound masculine plural
    جمع المؤنث السالم Sound feminine plural
    الجمع المكسَّر / جمع التكسير Broken plural (internal structure altered)
    جمع القلة Restricted plural (3–10)
    جمع الكثرة Unrestricted plural (10+)
    جمع الجمع Plural of plurals
    الوزن (pl. الأوزان) Morphological scale / pattern
    السماعي Heard/oral; referring to grammatical gender known only by transmission from Arabs
    الإضافة Possessive/genitive construction
    المضاف First word in an Idhafa (the possessed)
    المضاف إليه Second word in an Idhafa (the possessor), always Majrur
    المرفوع Nominative case
    المنصوب Accusative case
    المجرور Genitive case
    الموصوف والصفة Noun-adjective phrase (described + descriptor)
    نَار Fire (feminine, no visible sign — Sami'i)
    نَفْس Soul/self (feminine, Sami'i)
    مِصْبَاح Lamp/lantern (appears in Ayat al-Nur: اللهُ نُورُ السَّمَاوَاتِ وَالأَرْضِ)
    بِضْع A specific number between 3 and 9
    عُلَمَاء Scholars (plural of عَالِم, on Fuʿalaʾ scale)
    فُقَهَاء Jurists (plural of فَقِيه)
    شُهَدَاء Martyrs (plural of شَهِيد)
    فُقَرَاء Poor people (plural of فَقِير)
    رُسُل Messengers (plural of رَسُول)
    أَنْعُم / أَنَاعِم Blessings / overwhelming blessings (plural of plural)

    I'rab (Sentence Analysis) Examples

    Example 1: الجمعُ السالمِ

    Analysis: - Two words. No verb present → this is a phrase, not a sentence. - الجمعُ: has Alif-Lam, last letter has Dhamma → Marfu, singular masculine - السالمِ: has Alif-Lam, last letter has Kasra → Majrur state - The Mudaf cannot have Alif-Lam, but الجمع does have Alif-Lam → so this is NOT Idhafa - Both words agree in definiteness (both have ال), case would differ for Idhafa, but here السالم has Kasra suggesting Majrur... - Conclusion: Both words share Alif-Lam, same number (singular), same gender (masculine). الجمع is Marfu and السالم agrees → this is a Mausuf-Sifa (موصوف وصفة) construction — adjective phrase. The word الجمع itself is singular (meaning is plural, but the word is singular; if the word were plural, its Sifa would be singular feminine for non-human plural).

    Example 2: الجمعُ المكسَّرِ

    Same analysis as above → Mausuf-Sifa. Both words have Alif-Lam, both singular, both masculine. Adjective phrase.

    Example 3: أقسامُ الجمعِ (Idhafa)

    Analysis: - Two words. No verb → phrase. - أقسامُ: no Alif-Lam, no Tanwin, last letter Dhamma → Marfu, and this absence of ال and Tanwin signals it is a Mudaf - الجمعِ: has Alif-Lam, last letter Kasra → Majrur → this is the Mudaf ilayh - Conclusion: Idhafa construction. "The types/kinds of the plural."

    Example 4: قَلَمَا زَيْدٍ (Dual in Idhafa)

    Analysis: - قَلَمَا: original was قَلَمَانِ (two pens, Marfu dual) → Noon dropped because it became Mudaf - زَيْدٍ: Kasra + Tanwin → Majrur Mudaf ilayh (no Alif-Lam, proper noun) - Meaning: The two pens of Zayd

    Example 5: رَأَيْتُ أَرْبَعَةَ رِجَالٍ (Counting example)

    Analysis: - رَأَيْتُ: verb "I saw" + subject pronoun - أَرْبَعَةَ: number four, feminine form (تاء مربوطة) because رَجُل (singular of رِجَال) is masculine → gender flip rule - رِجَالٍ: Majrur because it is Mudaf ilayh to the number (number + counted noun is Idhafa structure) - Common mistake to avoid: Do not say أَرْبَعُ رِجَالٍ (would imply رجال is feminine) or use the gender of the plural رِجَال rather than the singular رَجُل.

    Phrase Construction Clue
    الجمعُ السالمُ Mausuf-Sifa Both have ال, same case, same gender, same number
    أقسامُ الجمعِ Idhafa First word has no ال and no Tanwin; second is Majrur
    طَالِبُو العِلمِ Idhafa (Noon dropped) Original طَالِبُونَ, Noon dropped in Idhafa

    Key Takeaways

    1. The Alif constraint is foundational: Whenever Alif enters a word (as in dual formation), the letter immediately before it is forced to have a Fatha. This is why رَجُل (Lam had Dhammatain) becomes رَجُلَانِ — the Dhammatain is replaced by a single Fatha.

    2. Dual has two forms: ـَانِ for Marfu, and ـَيْنِ for Mansub/Majrur. The Noon always drops when the dual becomes a Mudaf in Idhafa.

    3. Arabic number has three levels: Singular (1), Dual (2), and Plural (3+). Arabic uniquely maintains distinct forms for all three — a feature not found in most languages.

    4. Broken plural has no single rule but follows identifiable scales (أوزان). At this stage, learn singular-plural pairs by heart. Recognize patterns through exposure rather than mechanical wazan application.

    5. Jam'ul Qilla (3–10) vs. Jam'ul Kathira (10+): Arabic formally distinguishes small plurals from large plurals morphologically. The four Qilla scales (أَفْعُل، أَفْعَال، أَفْعِلَة، فِعْلَة) signal limited quantity; Kathira scales signal abundance.

    6. Gender-flip rule for counting (3–10): Always look at the gender of the singular of the noun being counted — not the plural — and use the opposite gender for the number. This is one of the most commonly misapplied rules.

    7. D.I.N.G. framework for adjective phrases: Definiteness, I'rab (case), Number, Gender — all four must agree between Mausuf and Sifa. This is the checklist for identifying and constructing adjective phrases correctly.

    8. The analysis habit: Before labeling any multi-word phrase, always ask: (a) Is there a verb? (b) Is it a sentence or a phrase? (c) If a phrase, what type? This systematic approach prevents common errors and builds lasting grammatical intuition.

    9. Noun-Adjective word is singular even when meaning is plural: الجمع السالم — the word الجمع is singular even though its meaning is "plural." Evidence: its Sifa السالم is singular. If الجمع were grammatically plural, a non-human plural Sifa would be singular feminine (as-Salima), not السالم.

    10. Homework: Read through the remaining sections of the broken plural chapter. Focus on recognizing singular-plural pairs. Note any words you encounter that seem to fit the four Jam'ul Qilla scales.
