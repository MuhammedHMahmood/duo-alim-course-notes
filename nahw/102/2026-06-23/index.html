    Nahw 102 - Study Notes

    Date: 2026-06-23

    Session Overview

    This session focused on Na't Sababi (Indirect Description) — a type of grammatical follower (tabi') where the descriptive word does not directly describe the word it follows, but rather describes something connected to it. The class reviewed the rules governing Na't Sababi, analyzed sentence examples in detail, and worked through exercises contrasting Na't Sababi with Na't Haqiqi. The session also covered the distinction between Ism al-Fa'il and Fa'il, and concluded with exercises distinguishing between Mausoof-Sifah, Mubtada-Khabar, and Hal constructions.

    Grammar Rules Covered

    1. Tawabi' — Recap

    • A tabi' (follower) is a word that follows another word and takes its grammatical case from it.
    • The word being followed is called matbu'; the word following is called tabi'.
    • Mausoof and Sifah are a type of tabi'/matbu' relationship — the Sifah (adjective) is the tabi', the Mausoof (noun described) is the matbu'.

    2. Na't Haqiqi vs. Na't Sababi

    Feature Na't Haqiqi Na't Sababi
    What does the Na't describe? Directly describes the Matbu'/Mausoof Describes something connected to the Matbu'
    Structure Na't — Man'ut (adjective after noun) Na't comes first, Man'ut comes second (reversed)
    Example جَاءَنِي وَلَدٌ عَالِمٌ جَاءَنِي وَلَدٌ عَالِمٌ أَبُوهُ
    Translation A learned boy came A boy whose father is learned came

    Key insight: In Na't Sababi, the Na't (descriptive word) and the Man'ut (the one actually being described) swap positions — Na't comes first, Man'ut comes second. This is the reverse of Na't Haqiqi where Man'ut (= Mausoof) comes first.

    3. Rules of Na't Sababi

    Rule 1 — Agreement between Matbu' and Tabi' The first word (Matbu') and the second word (Tabi') must agree in only two aspects: - I'rab (case ending) — both must be in the same case - Ma'rifah/Nakirah — both must either be definite or indefinite

    They do not need to agree in gender or number.

    Example: وَلَدٌ (Nakirah, Raf') — عَالِمٌ (Nakirah, Raf') ✓

    Rule 2 — Na't is always singular The Na't (the descriptive word/adjective) remains singular regardless of whether the Matbu' is singular, dual, or plural.

    Example: صُورَتَانِ جَمِيلٌ إِطَارُهُمَا "Two pictures whose frames are beautiful." — صُورَتَانِ is dual (Matbu'), but جَمِيلٌ remains singular (Na't)

    Rule 3 — Na't agrees in gender with the word after it (the Man'ut) The Na't must agree in gender with the Man'ut (the word it actually describes — the connected noun), not with the Matbu'.

    Example: جَاءَتِ السَّيِّدَةُ الْعَاقِلُ وَلَدُهَا "The lady whose son is intelligent came." — الْعَاقِلُ is masculine because وَلَدُهَا (son) is masculine, even though السَّيِّدَةُ is feminine.

    Summary of Na't Sababi agreement:

    Aspect Agrees with
    I'rab Matbu' (first word)
    Ma'rifah/Nakirah Matbu' (first word)
    Gender Man'ut (the connected word after Na't)
    Number Neither — Na't is always singular

    4. Ism al-Fa'il vs. Fa'il

    This distinction arose during sentence analysis:

    • Fa'il = the doer of the action in a sentence (the subject of the verb)
    • Ism al-Fa'il = any noun on the pattern فَاعِلٌ — regardless of whether it is actually the doer of the action in that sentence

    Example: رَأَيْتُ سَاجِدًا — "I saw a prostrating person." - أَنَا (I) is the Fa'il (doer — I am the one who saw) - سَاجِدٌ is the Ism al-Fa'il (on the فَاعِل pattern, but not the doer of the verb رَأَيْتُ)

    Application in Na't Sababi: When an Ism al-Fa'il appears as the Na't, it can take its own Fa'il (the Man'ut), forming a Jumlah Fi'liyyah or making the following noun its Fa'il.

    Example: جَاءَتِ امْرَأَةٌ عَالِمٌ ابْنُهَا - عَالِمٌ is Ism al-Fa'il; ابْنُهَا is its Fa'il (in Raf' case) - Together عَالِمٌ ابْنُهَا = the Tabi' of امْرَأَةٌ

    5. Identifying Na't Haqiqi vs. Na't Sababi — Practical Test

    Look at the middle word in the construction: - If the middle word is an adjective → likely Na't Sababi (the adjective describes the following noun, not the first noun) - If the middle word is a regular noun (not an adjective) → likely Na't Haqiqi using a Jumlah Ismiyyah as the Sifah

    Example: شَاهَدْنَا قِطَارًا سَيْرُهُ سَرِيعٌ - سَيْرٌ (speed/movement) = regular noun, not an adjective - سَيْرُهُ سَرِيعٌ = Jumlah Ismiyyah acting as Sifah of قِطَارًا - This is Na't Haqiqi with a sentential description

    6. Hal — Brief Review

    During the final exercise, Hal was revisited: - Hal = a circumstantial accusative describing the state of the doer/object at the time of the action - Usually Nakirah and in the Nasb case - Always requires a Dhul-Hal (the person/thing in that state) preceding it

    Example: جَاءَ الْوَلَدُ ضَاحِكًا - الْوَلَدُ = Dhul-Hal; ضَاحِكًا = Hal (Nakirah, Nasb) - Together → Fa'il of جَاءَ

    7. Distinguishing Mubtada-Khabar from Mausoof-Sifah

    The session highlighted how word order and the insertion of "is/are" in translation reveals the structure:

    هَذَا الْوَلَدُ ضَاحِكٌ - "This boy is laughing." - هَذَا الْوَلَدُ = Ism Ishara + Musha'run Ilayh → together = Mubtada - ضَاحِكٌ = Khabar

    هَذَا وَلَدٌ ضَاحِكٌ - "This is a laughing boy." - هَذَا = Mubtada - وَلَدٌ ضَاحِكٌ = Mausoof-Sifah → together = Khabar

    Tip: Where you insert "is" in the English translation tells you what the Mubtada is — everything before "is" = Mubtada.

    Important Vocabulary

    Arabic Term Meaning
    تَابِع (Tabi') The following/dependent word
    مَتْبُوع (Matbu') The word being followed
    نَعْت (Na't) Adjective/descriptive word
    مَنْعُوت (Man'ut) The word being described by the Na't
    مَوْصُوف (Mausoof) The described noun (synonym for Man'ut in Na't Haqiqi)
    صِفَة (Sifah) Adjective/description (synonym for Na't)
    نَعْت حَقِيقِي (Na't Haqiqi) Direct description — adjective directly describes the Matbu'
    نَعْت سَبَبِي (Na't Sababi) Indirect description — adjective describes something connected to Matbu'
    اسْمُ الْفَاعِل (Ism al-Fa'il) A noun on the فَاعِل pattern
    فَاعِل (Fa'il) The doer of the verb in a sentence
    جُمْلَة اسْمِيَّة (Jumlah Ismiyyah) Nominal sentence (Mubtada + Khabar)
    جُمْلَة فِعْلِيَّة (Jumlah Fi'liyyah) Verbal sentence (Fi'l + Fa'il)
    حَال (Hal) Circumstantial accusative (state of doer/object)
    ذُو الْحَال (Dhul-Hal) The person/thing whose state is described by Hal
    مَعْرِفَة (Ma'rifah) Definite noun
    نَكِرَة (Nakirah) Indefinite noun
    تَوَابِع (Tawabi') Followers (grammatical dependents) — plural of Tabi'
    مُهَذَّب (Muhazzab) Polite, refined, well-mannered

    I'rab (Sentence Analysis) Examples

    Example 1: جَاءَتِ امْرَأَةٌ عَالِمٌ ابْنُهَا

    "A woman whose son is learned came."

    Word Role
    جَاءَتْ Fi'l (verb)
    امْرَأَةٌ Matbu'
    عَالِمٌ Na't (Ism al-Fa'il)
    ابْنُهَا Man'ut — also Fa'il of عَالِمٌ (Mudaf + Mudaf Ilayh, in Raf')
    عَالِمٌ ابْنُهَا (together) Tabi' of امْرَأَةٌ
    امْرَأَةٌ + عَالِمٌ ابْنُهَا (together) Fa'il of جَاءَتْ

    Agreement check: عَالِمٌ agrees with امْرَأَةٌ in I'rab (Raf') and Nakirah. عَالِمٌ agrees with ابْنٌ in gender (both masculine).

    Example 2: هَاتَانِ صُورَتَانِ جَمِيلٌ إِطَارُهُمَا

    "These are two pictures whose frames are beautiful."

    Word Role
    هَاتَانِ Mubtada (Ism Ishara)
    صُورَتَانِ Matbu'
    جَمِيلٌ Na't (singular, masculine — agrees with إِطَار in gender)
    إِطَارُهُمَا Man'ut — Fa'il of جَمِيلٌ (Mudaf + Mudaf Ilayh, Raf')
    جَمِيلٌ إِطَارُهُمَا (together) Tabi' of صُورَتَانِ
    صُورَتَانِ + جَمِيلٌ إِطَارُهُمَا Khabar of هَاتَانِ

    Note: صُورَتَانِ is dual but جَمِيلٌ remains singular — Rule 2 in action.

    Example 3: جَاءَتِ السَّيِّدَةُ الْعَاقِلُ وَلَدُهَا

    "The lady whose son is intelligent came."

    Word Role
    جَاءَتْ Fi'l
    السَّيِّدَةُ Matbu' (Ma'rifah, Raf')
    الْعَاقِلُ Na't (Ma'rifah, Raf', masculine — agrees with وَلَد in gender)
    وَلَدُهَا Man'ut — Fa'il of الْعَاقِلُ
    السَّيِّدَةُ + الْعَاقِلُ وَلَدُهَا Fa'il of جَاءَتْ

    Example 4: شَاهَدْنَا قِطَارًا سَيْرُهُ سَرِيعٌ

    "We saw a train whose movement is fast."

    Word Role
    شَاهَدْنَا Fi'l + Fa'il
    قِطَارًا Mausoof (Nakirah, Nasb)
    سَيْرُهُ Mubtada (Mudaf + Mudaf Ilayh) — Raf'
    سَرِيعٌ Khabar
    سَيْرُهُ سَرِيعٌ Jumlah Ismiyyah acting as Sifah of قِطَارًا
    قِطَارًا سَيْرُهُ سَرِيعٌ Maf'ul Bihi of شَاهَدْنَا

    Note: This is Na't Haqiqi with a sentential Sifah — سَيْرٌ is a regular noun (not an adjective), so the middle word is not an adjective, indicating it is not Na't Sababi.

    Practice Examples

    Exercise Set 1 — Translate and Identify Na't Type

    1. رَكِبْتُ الْحِصَانَ الْجَمِيلَ سَرْجُهُ "I rode the horse whose saddle is beautiful." - رَكِبْتُ = Fi'l + Fa'il - الْحِصَانَ = Matbu' (Ma'rifah, Nasb) - الْجَمِيلَ = Na't (Ma'rifah, Nasb — agrees with حِصَان in I'rab and Ma'rifah; agrees with سَرْجٌ in gender — masculine) - سَرْجُهُ = Man'ut / Fa'il of الْجَمِيل (Raf') - الْحِصَانَ + الْجَمِيلَ سَرْجُهُ = Maf'ul Bihi → Na't Sababi

    2. هُوَ رَجُلٌ عَالِمَةٌ ابْنَتُهُ "He is a man whose daughter is learned." - هُوَ = Mubtada - رَجُلٌ = Matbu' (Nakirah, Raf') - عَالِمَةٌ = Na't (Nakirah, Raf', feminine — agrees with ابْنَة in gender) - ابْنَتُهُ = Man'ut / Fa'il of عَالِمَة (Raf') - رَجُلٌ + عَالِمَةٌ ابْنَتُهُ = Khabar of هُوَ → Na't Sababi

    3. هَؤُلَاءِ بَنَاتٌ عَاقِلَاتٌ "These are intelligent girls." - هَؤُلَاءِ = Mubtada - بَنَاتٌ = Mausoof (Nakirah, Raf') - عَاقِلَاتٌ = Sifah (Nakirah, Raf', feminine plural — agrees with بَنَات) - بَنَاتٌ عَاقِلَاتٌ = Khabar → Na't Haqiqi

    4. شَاهَدْنَا قِطَارًا سَيْرُهُ سَرِيعٌ (Analyzed above) — Na't Haqiqi with Jumlah Ismiyyah as Sifah

    5. أَوْقَدْتُ مِصْبَاحًا نُورُهُ قَوِيٌّ "I lit a lamp whose light was strong." - أَوْقَدْتُ = Fi'l + Fa'il - مِصْبَاحًا = Man'ut / Mausoof (Nakirah, Nasb) - نُورُهُ = Mubtada (Mudaf + Mudaf Ilayh) - قَوِيٌّ = Khabar - نُورُهُ قَوِيٌّ = Jumlah Ismiyyah → Sifah of مِصْبَاح → Na't Haqiqi with sentential Sifah

    6. هَذَا عَمَلٌ يَنْفَعُ "This is a beneficial deed." - هَذَا = Mubtada - عَمَلٌ = Mausoof (Nakirah, Raf') - يَنْفَعُ = Fi'l + Fa'il (Fa'il = Damir mustatir) → Jumlah Fi'liyyah acting as Sifah - عَمَلٌ يَنْفَعُ = Mausoof + Sifah → Khabar → Na't Haqiqi with Jumlah Fi'liyyah as Sifah

    7. هَذَا مَنْزِلٌ ضَيِّقٌ "This is a narrow house." - هَذَا = Mubtada - مَنْزِلٌ = Mausoof - ضَيِّقٌ = Sifah - مَنْزِلٌ ضَيِّقٌ = Khabar → Na't Haqiqi

    8. جَاءَ الرَّجُلُ الْمُهَذَّبُ أَخُوهُ "The man whose brother is polite came." - جَاءَ = Fi'l - الرَّجُلُ = Matbu' (Ma'rifah, Raf') - الْمُهَذَّبُ = Na't (Ma'rifah, Raf', masculine — agrees with أَخٌ in gender) - أَخُوهُ = Man'ut / Fa'il of الْمُهَذَّب (Raf') - الرَّجُلُ + الْمُهَذَّبُ أَخُوهُ = Fa'il of جَاءَ → Na't Sababi

    Exercise Set 2 — Distinguish Between Sentences

    هَذَا الْوَلَدُ ضَاحِكٌ "This boy is laughing." - هَذَا = Ism Ishara; الْوَلَدُ = Musha'run Ilayh - هَذَا الْوَلَدُ together = Mubtada - ضَاحِكٌ = Khabar

    هَذَا وَلَدٌ ضَاحِكٌ "This is a laughing boy." - هَذَا = Mubtada - وَلَدٌ = Mausoof; ضَاحِكٌ = Sifah - وَلَدٌ ضَاحِكٌ together = Khabar

    هَؤُلَاءِ الْبَنَاتُ عَاقِلَاتٌ "These girls are intelligent." - هَؤُلَاءِ = Ism Ishara; الْبَنَاتُ = Musha'run Ilayh - هَؤُلَاءِ الْبَنَاتُ together = Mubtada - عَاقِلَاتٌ = Khabar

    هَؤُلَاءِ بَنَاتٌ عَاقِلَاتٌ "These are intelligent girls." - هَؤُلَاءِ = Mubtada - بَنَاتٌ عَاقِلَاتٌ = Mausoof + Sifah → Khabar

    جَاءَ الْوَلَدُ ضَاحِكًا "The boy came laughing." - جَاءَ = Fi'l - الْوَلَدُ = Dhul-Hal + Fa'il of جَاءَ - ضَاحِكًا = Hal (Nakirah, Nasb)

    Key Takeaways

    1. Na't Sababi is indirect description. The Na't (adjective) does not describe the Matbu' (first noun) — it describes a noun connected to the Matbu'. The connection is typically through possession or relation (e.g., his father, her son, its frame).

    2. Reversed word order. Unlike Na't Haqiqi where Mausoof comes before Sifah, in Na't Sababi the Na't comes first and the Man'ut comes second. Remember: Na'at Sababi → Na't then Man'ut.

    3. Three agreement rules for Na't Sababi:

    4. Na't agrees with Matbu' in I'rab and Ma'rifah/Nakirah
    5. Na't is always singular (regardless of Matbu' number)

    6. Na't agrees with Man'ut in gender only

    7. Ism al-Fa'il ≠ Fa'il. A word can be on the فَاعِل pattern (Ism al-Fa'il) without being the actual doer (Fa'il) of the main verb in the sentence. An Ism al-Fa'il can govern its own Fa'il, which becomes the Man'ut in Na't Sababi.

    8. Quick identification test: Look at the middle word. If it is an adjective → Na't Sababi. If it is a regular noun (non-adjective) → the following Jumlah is a Sifah (Na't Haqiqi with a sentential description).

    9. Mubtada-Khabar vs. Mausoof-Sifah: Translate and insert "is/are" — what comes before "is" is the Mubtada. If a demonstrative pronoun shares its referent with the following noun (هَذَا الْوَلَدُ), both form the Mubtada together as Ism Ishara + Musha'run Ilayh.
