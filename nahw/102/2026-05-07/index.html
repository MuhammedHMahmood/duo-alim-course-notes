    Nahw 102 - Study Notes

    Date: 2026-05-07

    Session Overview

    This session focused on Section 3.7 — Marfu'at (roles of isms that are always in the state of Rafa'). Before diving into the specific marfu roles, the instructor laid crucial conceptual groundwork: explaining how many roles an ism can play in Arabic, why only three grammatical states are needed to identify all of them, and how grammarians categorized these roles into 8 marfu'at, 12 mansubat, and 2 majruurat (totaling 22). The session concluded with a brief introduction to Section 3.8 (Mansubat), which will be the starting point for the next class. The instructor also noted this may be his last session with the class.

    Grammar Rules Covered

    1. The 22 Roles of an Ism

    An ism (noun) — including pronouns (damir), demonstrative pronouns (ism al-ishara), proper names, and anything functioning as a noun — can play 22 distinct roles in Arabic sentences.

    Examples of roles mentioned: - Mubtada' (subject of a nominal sentence) - Khabar (predicate of a nominal sentence) - Fa'il (doer/subject of a verbal sentence) - Na'ib al-fa'il (deputy doer / passive subject) - Maf'ul bihi (direct object) - Maf'ul mutlaq (absolute object) - Tamyiz (specification/distinction) - Tawkid (emphasis) - Jar-majrur (prepositional phrase) - Mudaf ilayhi (possessive/genitive)

    2. Only Three States (Harakat) Are Needed

    Despite 22 possible roles, only three grammatical states are needed to identify any role:

    State Name Marker
    Rafa' Marfu' Dhamma (ـُ) or equivalent
    Nasb Mansub Fathah (ـَ) or equivalent
    Jarr Majrur Kasra (ـِ) or equivalent

    Why three are sufficient: The 22 roles never appear in combinations that would require more than three distinct markers to distinguish them. Roles that could cause confusion never appear side by side in the same sentence context.

    3. The 8 Marfu'at — Roles Always in the State of Rafa'

    The following 8 roles are always marfu' (carry dhamma or its equivalent on the final letter):

    # Role Definition/Context
    1 Fa'il The doer in an active-voice verbal sentence (Jumla Fi'liya ma'ruf)
    2 Na'ib al-Fa'il The deputy doer in a passive-voice verbal sentence (Jumla Fi'liya majhul)
    3 Mubtada' The subject of a nominal sentence (Jumla Ismiya)
    4 Khabar The predicate of a nominal sentence (Jumla Ismiya)
    5 Khabar Inna wa Akhawatiha The predicate of an Inna-type sentence (Inna gives nasb to the mubtada', but khabar stays marfu')
    6 Ism Kana The subject of a Kana-type sentence (Kana gives nasb to the khabar, but ism stays marfu')
    7 Ism Ma and La al-Mushabbaha bi-Laysa Subject of Ma and La that resemble Laysa
    8 Khabar La al-Nafi lil-Jins Predicate of La that negates the entire class (e.g., لا إله إلا الله — "illallah" carries dhamma)

    4. Fa'il vs. Na'ib al-Fa'il — Cannot Coexist

    A single verb cannot be simultaneously active (ma'ruf) and passive (majhul). Therefore: - If verb is ma'ruf (active) → Fa'il is present, no Na'ib al-fa'il - If verb is majhul (passive) → Na'ib al-fa'il is present, no Fa'il

    This means Fa'il and Na'ib al-fa'il can never appear together for the same verb in one sentence.

    5. The Effect of Inna and Kana on Jumla Ismiya

    Particle Affects Effect What Remains Marfu'
    Inna (and sisters) Mubtada' (closest) Gives Nasb Khabar remains marfu'
    Kana (and sisters) Khabar (farthest) Gives Nasb Ism remains marfu'

    Sisters of Inna include: أنَّ، كأنَّ، لكنَّ، ليتَ، لعلَّ

    6. One Word, One Role Only

    A single word in a sentence can only play one and only one grammatical role. If a word were allowed to play two roles simultaneously (e.g., both mubtada' and na'ib al-fa'il), translation and comprehension would become impossible. This is an inviolable rule of Arabic grammar.

    7. The 12 Mansubat and 2 Majruurat (Overview)

    The instructor gave a summary of the full breakdown:

    Category Count Total
    Marfu'at (Rafa' roles) 8
    Mansubat (Nasb roles) 12
    Majruurat (Jarr roles) 2 (Jar-Majrur & Mudaf Ilayhi)
    Total 22

    The Mansubat (Section 3.8) will be covered in the next class.

    8. The Implicit Fa'il (Damir Mustatir)

    When a verb is in a form that has no explicit (visible) fa'il — such as the 4th conjugation pattern (e.g., كُوِّرَتْ — feminine passive, tasukun ending) — the fa'il is a damir mustatir (hidden/implicit pronoun). The tasukun on the ta' is not a damir; it is simply a structural element of the conjugation pattern. The implicit damir must be extracted for full i'rab analysis.

    Important Vocabulary

    Arabic Term Meaning
    إسم (Ism) Noun (includes pronouns, proper names, demonstratives, etc.)
    مرفوع (Marfu') Noun in the nominative state, carries dhamma
    منصوب (Mansub) Noun in the accusative state, carries fathah
    مجرور (Majrur) Noun in the genitive state, carries kasra
    مرفوعات (Marfu'at) The category of roles that are always marfu'
    منصوبات (Mansubat) The category of roles that are always mansub
    مجرورات (Majrurat) The category of roles that are always majrur
    فاعل (Fa'il) Doer; subject of an active-voice verbal sentence
    نائب الفاعل (Na'ib al-Fa'il) Deputy doer; subject of a passive-voice verbal sentence
    مبتدأ (Mubtada') Subject of a nominal sentence
    خبر (Khabar) Predicate of a nominal sentence
    جملة اسمية (Jumla Ismiya) Nominal sentence (begins with a noun)
    جملة فعلية (Jumla Fi'liya) Verbal sentence (begins with a verb)
    معروف (Ma'ruf) Active voice
    مجهول (Majhul) Passive voice
    إنَّ وأخواتها (Inna wa Akhawatiha) Inna and its sisters (particles that give nasb to mubtada')
    كان وأخواتها (Kana wa Akhawatiha) Kana and its sisters (particles that give nasb to khabar)
    ما ولا المشبهة بليس Ma and La that resemble Laysa
    لا النافية للجنس (La al-Nafiya lil-Jins) La that negates the entire class/genus
    خبر إنَّ (Khabar Inna) Predicate of an Inna sentence (remains marfu')
    اسم كان (Ism Kana) Subject of a Kana sentence (remains marfu')
    ضمير مستتر (Damir Mustatir) Hidden/implicit pronoun
    مضاف إليه (Mudaf Ilayhi) Genitive noun in a possessive construction (always majrur)

    I'rab (Sentence Analysis) Examples

    Example 1: إِذَا الشَّمْسُ كُوِّرَتْ (Surah al-Takwir, 81:1)

    Word Role State Reason
    إذا Condition particle — Not analyzed as ism here
    الشَّمْسُ Mubtada' Marfu' (dhamma) Subject of the nominal sentence
    كُوِّرَتْ Khabar (Jumla Fi'liya) — Predicate, here as a verbal clause
    كُوِّرَ (verb) Fi'l Majhul (passive) — Pattern of passive (4th conjugation feminine: كُوِّرَتْ)
    Ta' in كُوِّرَتْ Not a damir — structural — Tasukun is a pattern feature, not a damir
    Damir Mustatir (هي) Na'ib al-Fa'il Marfu' Implicit pronoun referring to الشمس

    Full reading: الشمس is mubtada', كوّرت is khabar as a Jumla Fi'liya. Inside that jumla: the verb is majhul, the na'ib al-fa'il is a hidden damir (هي) referring to الشمس.

    Example 2: إِنَّ اللَّهَ غَفُورٌ رَّحِيمٌ

    Word Role State Reason
    إنَّ Particle of emphasis — Acts on the following ism
    اللَّهَ Ism Inna Mansub (fathah) Inna gives nasb to mubtada'
    غَفُورٌ Khabar Inna Marfu' (dhamma) Khabar remains in rafa'
    رَّحِيمٌ Na't (adjective) of khabar Marfu' (dhamma) Follows its mawsuf in state

    Example 3: لا إِلَٰهَ إِلَّا اللَّهُ

    Word Role State Reason
    لا La al-Nafiya lil-Jins — Class negation particle
    إلٰه Ism La Mansub (fathah, no tanwin) La gives nasb to its ism
    إلا الله Khabar La (or badal) Marfu' (dhamma) Khabar of La al-Nafiya lil-Jins is marfu'

    Example 4: جَاءَ زَيْدٌ

    Word Role State Reason
    جَاءَ Fi'l ma'ruf — Active verb
    زَيْدٌ Fa'il Marfu' (dhamma + tanwin) Doer in active verbal sentence

    Practice Examples

    Identify the role and state of the underlined word:

    1. جَاءَ زَيْدٌ — What is زيد? → Fa'il, Marfu'
    2. قُتِلَ أَصْحَابُ الْأُخْدُودِ (Surah al-Buruj, 85:4) — What is أصحاب? → Na'ib al-Fa'il, Marfu' (verb قُتِلَ is passive/majhul)
    3. إنَّ اللهَ غَفُورٌ رَّحِيمٌ — What is غفور? → Khabar Inna, Marfu'
    4. كَانَ زَيْدٌ عَالِمًا — What is زيد? → Ism Kana, Marfu'; What is عالمًا? → Khabar Kana, Mansub
    5. لا إِلَهَ إِلَّا اللَّهُ — What is the khabar? → Marfu' (La al-Nafiya lil-Jins)

    Determine whether Fa'il or Na'ib al-Fa'il is present: - نَصَرَ زَيْدٌ → Active voice → Fa'il = زيد - نُصِرَ زَيْدٌ → Passive voice → Na'ib al-Fa'il = زيد - Can both appear for the same verb? No — never.

    Key Takeaways

    1. An ism can play 22 distinct roles in Arabic. Knowing this total is foundational for understanding why we need grammatical states.

    2. Only 3 grammatical states (Rafa', Nasb, Jarr) are sufficient to identify all 22 roles — a profound miracle of the Arabic language. Roles are distributed such that three markers never cause ambiguity.

    3. The 22 roles are categorized as: 8 Marfu'at + 12 Mansubat + 2 Majrurat = 22 total.

    4. Every word in a sentence plays one and only one grammatical role. Dual roles are grammatically forbidden — this is what makes Arabic parsing unambiguous.

    5. Fa'il and Na'ib al-Fa'il can never coexist for the same verb, because a verb is either active or passive — never both.

    6. Inna gives nasb to the mubtada' (closer element); Kana gives nasb to the khabar (farther element). The element not affected remains marfu'.

    7. Tasukun (ـْ) on the ta' of a verb form is NOT a damir. When no explicit fa'il or na'ib al-fa'il follows such a verb, the doer is a damir mustatir (hidden pronoun) that must be identified in full i'rab.

    8. Section 3.8 (Mansubat — 12 roles) is the starting point for the next class.

    9. Critical study habit: Always ask — What role is this word playing? Why is it in this state? This approach prevents rote memorization and builds deep grammatical understanding that connects to Quranic analysis.
