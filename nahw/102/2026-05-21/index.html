    Nahw 102 - Study Notes

    Date: 2026-05-21

    Session Overview

    This session introduced two major grammatical concepts:

    1. Hal and Dhul-Hal — A word (or sentence) that describes the condition/state of the doer (fa'il) or object (maf'ul bihi) at the time an action takes place. Six detailed notes were covered, followed by sentence analysis practice.

    2. Tamiz and Mumayyaz — An indeterminate noun that removes vagueness or ambiguity created by a preceding noun, particularly in contexts of distance, weight, measure, or number. An overview of the rules for Arabic numerals was given as a preview for the next class.

    Grammar Rules Covered

    Concept 1: Hal (حال) and Dhul-Hal (ذو الحال)

    Definition: Hal is an ism that describes the condition or state of either the fa'il or the maf'ul bihi at the time the action takes place. The word whose condition is being described is called the Dhul-Hal.

    Note 1 — Definitions - Hal (حال): The word describing the condition/state. Linguistic meaning: state or condition. - Dhul-Hal (ذو الحال): The word whose condition is being described. - Pattern: Dhul-Hal generally comes first, Hal comes second.

    Note 2 — Hal is Mansub (in the Nasab case) - Hal is always in the nasab case. - Since Hal is generally nakira (indefinite, no alif-lam), it takes two fathas (tanwin nasb).

    Note 3 — Dhul-Hal is Ma'rifah; Hal is Nakira - Dhul-Hal is generally ma'rifah (definite). - Hal is generally nakira (indefinite). - The word generally indicates exceptions are possible.

    Note 4 — Switching Order When Dhul-Hal is Nakira - Normally: Dhul-Hal → Hal. - Exception: If the Dhul-Hal is nakira, the Hal may be brought before the Dhul-Hal (order is flipped for emphasis). - Example: جَاءَنِي رَاكِبًا رَجُلٌ — A man came to me while riding. (Hal comes first because Dhul-Hal is nakira.)

    Note 5 — Dhul-Hal Can Be a Dhamir (Pronoun) - The Dhul-Hal does not have to be an explicit noun; it can be a hidden/implied pronoun within a verb. - Example: زَيْدٌ أَكَلَ جَالِسًا — The implied huwa in akala is the Dhul-Hal; jalisan is the Hal.

    Note 6 — Hal Can Be an Entire Sentence (Jumlah) - Hal does not have to be a single noun. It can be a full sentence.

    A. If Hal is a Jumlah Ismiyyah (nominal sentence): - A waw haliyah (وَاو حَالِيَّة) is added before the mubtada. - Example (Quran): لَا تَقْرَبُوا الصَّلَاةَ وَأَنْتُمْ سُكَارَى — Do not come near salah while you are intoxicated. - وَ = waw haliyah | أَنْتُمْ = mubtada | سُكَارَى = khabar | The whole jumlah ismiyyah = Hal - Dhul-Hal = the dhamir inside لَا تَقْرَبُوا

    B. If Hal is a Jumlah Fi'liyyah (verbal sentence) with a Madi verb: - Both waw haliyah AND قَدْ must appear before the madi verb. - Example: جَاءَ زَيْدٌ وَقَدْ خَرَجَ خَادِمُهُ — Zaid came while his servant had left. - وَ = waw haliyah | قَدْ = harf | خَرَجَ = fi'l | خَادِمُهُ = fa'il of kharaja | Whole phrase = Hal - Dhul-Hal = زَيْدٌ

    Concept 2: Tamiz (تَمْيِيز) and Mumayyaz (مُمَيَّز)

    Definition: Tamiz is an ism nakira that removes the ambiguity or vagueness created by the preceding ism (the mumayyaz). The ambiguity may be in distance, weight, measure, or number.

    • Mumayyaz (مُمَيَّز): The ambiguous word — comes first.
    • Tamiz (تَمْيِيز): The clarifying word — comes second.
    • Like Hal, Tamiz is nakira and in the nasab case (two fathas if a regular singular indefinite noun).
    • Mumayyaz and Tamiz together function as Fa'il or Maf'ul Bihi, just like Dhul-Hal and Hal.

    Rule regarding numbers: If the mumayyaz is a number, the rules specific to Arabic numerals apply. The tamiz does not always have to be nakira mansub in this case — the rules vary by number range:

    Number Range Notes
    1–2 Special rules
    3–10 Special rules (noun after them is in jar + plural)
    11–12 Special rules (noun is singular mansub)
    13–19 Special rules
    20, 30 ... 90 (tens) Special rules (noun is singular mansub)
    21–29, 31–39, etc. (compound) Special rules
    100 Noun is singular majrur
    1000 Special rules

    (Full number rules to be covered in the next class — memorization of 1–100 assigned as homework.)

    Important Vocabulary

    Arabic Term Meaning
    حَال (Hal) State / condition — the word describing the condition of the fa'il or maf'ul
    ذُو الْحَال (Dhul-Hal) The one whose condition is being described
    فَاعِل (Fa'il) The doer of the action / subject
    مَفْعُول بِهِ (Maf'ul Bihi) The object of the action
    مَعْرِفَة (Ma'rifah) Definite noun
    نَكِرَة (Nakira) Indefinite noun
    مَنْصُوب (Mansub) Noun in the nasab case
    وَاو حَالِيَّة (Waw Haliyah) The waw that converts a jumlah into a Hal
    جُمْلَة اسْمِيَّة (Jumlah Ismiyyah) Nominal sentence (mubtada + khabar)
    جُمْلَة فِعْلِيَّة (Jumlah Fi'liyyah) Verbal sentence (fi'l + fa'il)
    ضَمِير مُسْتَتِر (Dhamir Mustatir) Hidden/implied pronoun
    تَمْيِيز (Tamiz) Noun that clarifies/specifies the mumayyaz
    مُمَيَّز (Mumayyaz) The ambiguous noun whose vagueness tamiz removes
    رَاكِبًا (Rakiban) Riding / mounted (hal in nasab)
    نَائِمًا (Na'iman) Sleeping (hal in nasab)
    جَالِسًا / جَالِسَيْنِ (Jalisan / Jalisayni) Sitting — singular / dual (dual in nasab)
    كَوْكَب (Kawkab) Star
    أَحَدَ عَشَرَ (Ahada Ashara) Eleven
    ذِرَاع (Dhira') Arm span / cubit (unit of measure)
    حَرِير (Harir) Silk
    مِلْء الْكَفّ (Mil' al-Kaff) A handful
    شِبْر (Shibr) A hand-span (unit of length)
    رَطْل (Ratl) A unit of measurement (roughly a pound)
    قُبَّة / قَدَح (Qubba / Qadah) Cup / vessel

    I'rab (Sentence Analysis) Examples

    Example 1: جَاءَ زَيْدٌ رَاكِبًا

    Zaid came while mounted.

    Word Role Reason
    جَاءَ Fi'l (verb) Past tense verb
    زَيْدٌ Dhul-Hal Ma'rifah (proper noun); not in nasab
    رَاكِبًا Hal Nakira + mansub (two fathas)
    زَيْدٌ + رَاكِبًا Fa'il of جَاءَ Dhul-Hal + Hal together = Fa'il

    Example 2: جِئْتُ عَمْرًا نَائِمًا

    I came to Amr while he was asleep.

    Word Role Reason
    جِئْتُ Fi'l + Fa'il (dhamir tu)
    عَمْرًا Dhul-Hal Maf'ul bihi of ji'tu — ma'rifah proper noun
    نَائِمًا Hal Nakira + mansub
    عَمْرًا + نَائِمًا Maf'ul Bihi of جِئْتُ Dhul-Hal + Hal together = Maf'ul

    Example 3: لَقِيتُ بَكْرًا وَهُوَ جَالِسٌ

    I met Bakr while he was sitting. (Hal = Jumlah Ismiyyah)

    Word Role Reason
    لَقِيتُ Fi'l + Fa'il
    بَكْرًا Dhul-Hal Maf'ul bihi; ma'rifah
    وَ Waw Haliyah Converts jumlah into Hal
    هُوَ Mubtada
    جَالِسٌ Khabar
    وَهُوَ جَالِسٌ Hal (Jumlah Ismiyyah) Full nominal sentence functioning as Hal
    بَكْرًا + وَهُوَ جَالِسٌ Maf'ul Bihi of لَقِيتُ Dhul-Hal + Hal together

    Example 4: زَيْدٌ أَكَلَ جَالِسًا

    Zaid ate while sitting. (Dhul-Hal = hidden dhamir)

    Word Role Reason
    زَيْدٌ Mubtada
    أَكَلَ Fi'l
    هُوَ (implied in أَكَلَ) Dhul-Hal Hidden dhamir referring to Zaid
    جَالِسًا Hal Nakira + mansub
    هُوَ (dhamir) + جَالِسًا Fa'il of أَكَلَ Dhul-Hal + Hal together
    أَكَلَ جَالِسًا Khabar of زَيْدٌ Fi'l + Fa'il = Jumlah Fi'liyyah functioning as Khabar

    Example 5: جَاءَ زَيْدٌ وَقَدْ خَرَجَ خَادِمُهُ

    Zaid came while his servant had left. (Hal = Jumlah Fi'liyyah with Madi)

    Word Role Reason
    جَاءَ Fi'l
    زَيْدٌ Dhul-Hal → Fa'il
    وَ Waw Haliyah
    قَدْ Harf (required before madi in Hal)
    خَرَجَ Fi'l (madi)
    خَادِمُهُ Fa'il of خَرَجَ
    وَقَدْ خَرَجَ خَادِمُهُ Hal (Jumlah Fi'liyyah)

    Tamiz Example: رَأَيْتُ أَحَدَ عَشَرَ كَوْكَبًا

    I saw eleven stars.

    Word Role Reason
    رَأَيْتُ Fi'l + Fa'il
    أَحَدَ عَشَرَ Mumayyaz The number creates ambiguity — eleven of what?
    كَوْكَبًا Tamiz Nakira + mansub; clarifies what the eleven refers to
    أَحَدَ عَشَرَ + كَوْكَبًا Maf'ul Bihi of رَأَيْتُ

    Key Takeaways

    1. Hal and Dhul-Hal always come in a pair — just like fi'l/fa'il, mubtada/khabar, sifah/mawsuf, and mudaf/mudaf ilayhi.

    2. Hal has two essential properties:

    3. It is nakira (indefinite).

    4. It is in the nasab case (usually two fathas for a regular singular noun; ya-noon for dual in nasab).

    5. Dhul-Hal is generally ma'rifah (definite) — this is how you distinguish Hal from Dhul-Hal when both appear together.

    6. Dhul-Hal + Hal together function as a grammatical unit — they jointly become either the Fa'il or the Maf'ul Bihi of the verb.

    7. Hal can be an entire sentence:

    8. Jumlah Ismiyyah → add waw haliyah before the mubtada.

    9. Jumlah Fi'liyyah with a madi verb → add waw haliyah + قَدْ before the verb.

    10. Waw has multiple meanings in Arabic — waw al-'atf (conjunction), waw al-qasam (oath), waw al-haliyah (hal), waw al-ma'iyyah (accompaniment). Context determines which meaning applies.

    11. The Dhul-Hal can be a hidden dhamir (dhamir mustatir) — e.g., the implied huwa inside a verb form.

    12. Tamiz and Mumayyaz parallel Hal and Dhul-Hal — Tamiz is nakira and mansub; Mumayyaz comes first; together they become Fa'il or Maf'ul.

    13. Number rules are critical for Tamiz — when the mumayyaz is a number, each range (1–2, 3–10, 11–12, 13–19, the tens, the compounds, 100, 1000) has its own rule for the following noun.

    14. Sentence analysis (i'rab) is the "lab" of Nahw — practicing it forces application of every grammar rule simultaneously and leads to deeper, lasting understanding.

    15. Homework for next class: Memorize Arabic numbers 1–100 fluently (say them 100 times). Mastery of the numbers is required before the number rules (which govern Tamiz) can be properly taught.
