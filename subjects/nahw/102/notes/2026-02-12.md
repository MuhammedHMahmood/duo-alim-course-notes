## Grammar Rules Covered

### Rule 3: Mudaf with Ismul-Ishara
- When musharun-ilayh is a mudaf (construct phrase), ismul-ishara comes **after** the mudaf-ilayh
- Example: **kitabu-ka hadha** (this book of yours) - NOT **hadha kitabu-ka**
- Reason: Placing hadha before creates a complete sentence (mubtada-khabar), not a phrase

### Rule 4: Ismul-Ishara as Mubtada
- When ismul-ishara functions as mubtada, the khabar is generally nakira (indefinite)
- Example: **hadha kitabun** (this is a book)
- When khabar is ma'rifah, add dameer al-fasil: **hadha huwa al-kitabu** (this is the book)

### Rule 5: Mudaf as Khabar
- When mudaf-mudaf-ilayh serves as khabar, no dameer is needed between mubtada and khabar
- Example: **hadha ibn al-maliki** (this is the son of the king)
- For ismul-ishara construction: **ibn al-maliki hadha** (this son of the king)

### Rule 6: Spatial Demonstratives
- **huna** or **hahunah** = here (near)
- **hunaka** = there (far)
- These are also asmul-ishara but have no special grammatical rules
- Word order depends on emphasis: **mata huna** (he died here) vs **huna mata** (HERE he died)

## I'rab (Sentence Analysis) Examples

### Example 1: **hadha al-qalamu nafees**
- **hadha** = ismul-ishara
- **al-qalam** = musharun-ilayh
- Together: mubtada
- **nafees** = khabar (nakira)
- Translation: This pen is precious

### Example 2: **tilka sayyaratun qadeemah**
- **tilka** = ismul-ishara
- **sayyarat** = musharun-ilayh
- Together: mubtada
- **qadeemah** = khabar
- Translation: That car is old

### Example 3: **ula'ika tujjarun sadiqoon**
- **ula'ika** = mubtada
- **tujjarun sadiqoon** = mudaf-sifah (khabar)
- Translation: Those are truthful businessmen

## Practice Examples

### Sentence vs. Phrase Distinction:
- **hadha kitabun** (sentence) = This is a book
- **hadha al-kitab** (phrase) = This book
- **hadha huwa al-kitabu** (sentence) = This is the book

### Mudaf Constructions:
- **kitabu-ka hadha** = this book of yours (phrase)
- **hadha kitabu-ka** = this is your book (sentence)
- **ibn al-maliki hadha** = this son of the king (phrase)
- **hadha ibn al-maliki** = this is the son of the king (sentence)

### Dual Forms:
- **hadhani waladani muhathabani** = these two are well-mannered boys
- **hadhani al-waladani muhathabani** = these two boys are well-mannered

## Key Takeaways

1. **Word order matters critically** - changing position of ismul-ishara can transform phrases into sentences and vice versa

2. **Presence or absence of alif-lam** determines whether a construction is ismul-ishara/musharun-ilayh (phrase) or mubtada/khabar (sentence)

3. **Mudaf constructions** require special attention to word order and cannot accept alif-lam on the mudaf

4. **Emphasis in spatial demonstratives** (huna, hunaka) is shown through word order flexibility

5. **Sentence analysis** requires identifying where "is/are" would be inserted in English translation to distinguish mubtada from khabar
