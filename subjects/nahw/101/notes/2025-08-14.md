## Session Overview

This session continued building the foundational map of Arabic grammar, focusing on the classification of the three main parts of speech (kalima): ism, fi'il, and harf. The class covered detailed categorization of each type, with emphasis on understanding how words relate to each other through derivation (ishtiqaq). Key concepts included the three types of ism (jamid, masdar, mushtaq), four types of fi'il (madhi, mudari', amr, nahi), and two types of harf (harf amil, harf ghayr amil).

## Grammar Rules Covered

### Subject Matter of Nahw
- Arabic grammar deals with three things: words, phrases, and sentences
- Grammar does not deal with thoughts, feelings, or rhetoric

### Parts of Speech Comparison
- English has 8 parts of speech; Arabic has 3 (ism, fi'il, harf)
- **Ism** corresponds to: nouns, pronouns, adjectives, and adverbs
- **Fi'il** corresponds to: verbs
- **Harf** corresponds to: prepositions, conjunctions, and articles

### Classification of Ism (Three Types)
1. **Jamid** - Frozen word: not derived from anything, nothing derived from it
2. **Masdar** - Source noun: not derived from anything, but many words derive from it
3. **Mushtaq** - Derived nouns: constructed from the masdar

### Classification of Fi'il (Four Types)
1. **Madhi** - Past tense verb
2. **Mudari'** - Present and future tense verb
3. **Amr** - Imperative/command verb
4. **Nahi** - Negative command/prohibition

### Classification of Harf (Two Types)
1. **Harf Amil** - Governing agent: changes the haraka of following noun
2. **Harf Ghayr Amil** - Non-governing agent: doesn't change haraka of following noun

## I'rab (Sentence Analysis) Examples

### Preposition Effect on Nouns
- **Al-baytu** (the house) - original form with dhamma
- **Fi al-bayti** (in the house) - preposition changes dhamma to kasra
- Rule: All prepositions (fi, min, ila, ala, etc.) cause the following noun to take kasra

### Non-Governing Particles
- **Waw** (and) - doesn't change the haraka of following nouns
- Example: Adding waw between nouns doesn't affect their original harakat

## Practice Examples

### Derivation Patterns from An-Nasr (Help)
- **Nasirun** - Helper (one who helps)
- **Mansurun** - One who is helped
- **Ansar** - Helpers (plural)

### Pattern Recognition
- **Fa'ilun pattern** (like Nasirun) = doer/agent
- **Maf'ulun pattern** (like Mansurun) = one acted upon
- **Daribun** - One who hits
- **Madrabun** - One who is hit
- **Qari'un** - Reciter
- **Hamidun** - One who praises

## Key Takeaways

1. **Mapping Concept**: Arabic grammar is like drawing a comprehensive map where each element has its specific place and classification

2. **Derivation System**: Arabic's organized derivation system (ishtiqaq) allows understanding of unknown words through pattern recognition and root knowledge

3. **Masdar Importance**: Knowing the masdar (source noun) helps understand the meaning of all derived words sharing the same root letters

4. **Dictionary Usage**: In classical dictionaries, look up words by their root letters, not their derived forms

5. **Governing vs Non-Governing**: Some particles (harf amil) change noun endings, others (harf ghayr amil) don't - this affects i'rab

6. **Practical Application**: Understanding these classifications helps in proper sentence construction and meaning comprehension

7. **Pattern Learning**: Recognizing patterns like Fa'ilun (doer) and Maf'ulun (acted upon) enables understanding of unfamiliar vocabulary
