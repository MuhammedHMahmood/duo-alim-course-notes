This session covered two hadith from the final eight remaining in the course. The instructor began with **Hadith #32** — the famous principle *Lā ḍarara wa lā ḍirār* (no harm and no reciprocating harm) — examining its meaning through the commentary of Ibn Ḥabīb and Imām al-Muḥsinī. ## Hadith Text and Chain of Narration

### Hadith #32 — Lā Ḍarara wa Lā Ḍirār

**Narrator:** Abū Saʿīd al-Khudrī (رضي الله عنه)
- Full name: Saʿd ibn Sinān; Abū Saʿīd was his *kunya* (title/nickname)

**Arabic Text:**
> لَا ضَرَرَ وَلَا ضِرَارَ

**Translation:**
> "There is no harm, and there is no reciprocating harm."

---

### Hadith #33 — Asās al-Qaḍāʾ fī al-Islām

**Narrator:** ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿAbbās (رضي الله عنهما), from the Prophet ﷺ

**Arabic Text:**
> لَوْ يُعْطَى النَّاسُ بِدَعْوَاهُمْ لَادَّعَى رِجَالٌ أَمْوَالَ قَوْمٍ وَدِمَاءَهُمْ، لَكِنِ الْبَيِّنَةُ عَلَى الْمُدَّعِي، وَالْيَمِينُ عَلَى مَنْ أَنْكَرَ

**Translation:**
> "If people were granted based on their claims, people would certainly claim the wealth and the lives of others. However, the burden of proof is upon the claimant, and the oath is upon the one who denies (the defendant)."

---

## Key Themes

1. **Prohibition of harm (ḍarar) in Islam** — causing harm to others is impermissible; this is a foundational Islamic legal principle.
2. **Individual accountability** — no person bears the burden or consequence of another's actions; every soul is responsible for itself.
3. **The limits of retaliation** — a wronged party may only respond in equal measure, not exceed the harm done to them.
4. **Due process in Islamic law** — Islam does not operate on a "free-for-all" claim system; structured legal principles govern disputes.
5. **The burden of proof (al-bayyina)** — the claimant must substantiate their claim with evidence.
6. **The burden of oath (al-yamīn)** — the defendant who denies the claim must swear an oath; this is weighty because of the sanctity of Allah's name.
7. **Divine justice (ʿAdl)** — Allah ﷻ is Al-ʿĀdil (The Most Just) and Al-Muqsiṭ; He will not oppress anyone, and neither should His servants.

## Explanation and Commentary

### Hadith #32 — Lā Ḍarara wa Lā Ḍirār

**What does "no harm" (*lā ḍarar*) mean?**

Ibn Ḥabīb explains: *lā ḍarar* means that no harm should be brought upon a person who has not brought it upon themselves. The exception is when someone's right has been betrayed — in that case, the wronged party is permitted by the Sharīʿa to respond, but *only to the same degree* of harm suffered. Exceeding that level becomes impermissible.

**What does "no reciprocating harm" (*lā ḍirār*) mean?**

Ibn Ḥabīb explains: no one should be harmed because of someone else's actions or deeds. This reflects the Quranic principle of individual accountability:

> وَلَا تَزِرُ وَازِرَةٌ وِزْرَ أُخْرَى
> *"No bearer of burdens shall bear the burden of another."*

A person cannot be punished for another's crime, nor can someone else take the fall in their place. Each individual is accountable for their own actions alone.

**Definition of harm (*ḍarar*):**

Imām al-Muḥsinī defines *ḍarar* as: **a situation where one party benefits while the other suffers a loss or detriment.** If my gain comes at the direct expense of another's loss, that constitutes *ḍarar*.

**Connection to a Ḥadīth Qudsī:**

The instructor referenced a ḥadīth qudsī in which Allah ﷻ says:
> "I have made oppression (*ẓulm*) forbidden upon Myself, and I have made it forbidden among you — so do not oppress one another."

Allah ﷻ will never punish beyond what is deserved (He is perfectly just), and He has likewise prohibited oppression among His servants.

**Further scholarly view:**

Some scholars noted that *lā ḍarar wa lā ḍirār* implies a person should take the higher moral ground — referencing the prophetic hadith:

> "Fulfill the trust of one who has entrusted you, and do not betray the one who has betrayed you."

This means: even when wronged, a Muslim should ideally not descend to the level of the one who wronged them — though Islamic law does grant the right of equal redress.

### Hadith #33 — Asās al-Qaḍāʾ fī al-Islām

**The hypothetical scenario:**

The Prophet ﷺ opens with a logical premise: *if* judgments were awarded simply on the basis of whoever makes a claim, then people would freely and falsely claim the wealth and even the lives (blood) of others. Society would collapse into a "survival of the fittest" free-for-all where oppression and injustice would reign unchecked.

**The two foundational principles:**

1. **البَيِّنَة عَلَى الْمُدَّعِي** — *The burden of proof is on the claimant.*
   The plaintiff who brings a case must present evidence to substantiate their claim. A mere statement or accusation is not sufficient.

2. **الْيَمِينُ عَلَى مَنْ أَنْكَرَ** — *The oath is upon the one who denies.*
   If the defendant denies the claim, they must swear an oath (by Allah's name) that the claim is false.

**Why the oath carries weight:**

Taking an oath by the name of Allah is not a trivial act. The instructor referenced a hadith illustrating how weighty Allah's name is — the Day of Judgment will not commence as long as there remains someone on earth uttering the name of Allah. The name of Allah is the very fuel sustaining this world. A false oath is therefore an enormously grave sin.

**The court of Allah — ultimate accountability:**

Even if a person escapes worldly judgment through a false oath, they will not escape divine justice. On the Day of Qiyāmah, they will be held accountable for:
- Taking Allah's name falsely
- Usurping the rights and property of others unjustly

**Grammatical notes from the instructor:**

- *ladda'ā rijālun* — **rijālun** is the *fāʿil* (subject)
- *amwāla qawmin* — **amwāl** is the *mafʿūl* (object), with *qawm* as the *muḍāf ilayh*
- *wa dimāʾahum* — **maʿṭūf** (coordinated object), also *mafʿūl* of *ladda'ā*

## Fiqh Rulings Derived

**From Hadith #32:**

1. Causing harm to others is **ḥarām** (prohibited) in Islam.
2. Retaliation is permitted but **only to the same degree** of harm suffered — exceeding it is impermissible.
3. No individual may be punished or bear consequences for another person's wrongdoing.
4. Oppression (*ẓulm*) in any form is forbidden, as Allah ﷻ has prohibited it upon Himself and upon His servants.

**From Hadith #33:**

1. In Islamic legal proceedings, **the burden of proof (*bayyina*) rests on the claimant** — the one making a claim must provide evidence.
2. **The burden of oath (*yamīn*) rests on the defendant** — the one denying a claim must swear an oath.
3. A mere claim or assertion without evidence does not entitle a person to any right, property, or legal remedy.
4. Taking a **false oath** by Allah's name is a major sin that will be answered for on the Day of Judgment even if it went unpunished in this world.
5. Islam has a complete and rational system of **due process** that protects individual rights and prevents oppression.

## Lessons and Takeaways

1. **Islam is a complete way of life.** These hadith demonstrate that Islamic teachings are not confined to ritual worship (ṣalāh, zakāt, fasting, Ḥajj) but encompass every aspect of life — including dispute resolution, legal systems, and interpersonal justice.

2. **The principle of no harm is foundational.** *Lā ḍarar wa lā ḍirār* is one of the five major legal maxims (*al-qawāʿid al-fiqhiyya al-kubrā*) of Islamic jurisprudence, underpinning countless rulings across all areas of fiqh.

3. **Individual accountability is absolute.** Every soul bears its own deeds. No one can escape responsibility by shifting blame onto others, nor can another person take your burden — a truth that should motivate personal vigilance and repentance.

4. **Divine justice is perfect.** Allah ﷻ is Al-ʿĀdil and Al-Muqsiṭ. He rewards and punishes exactly in accordance with what each person deserves. This should inspire both hope (in His mercy) and caution (in committing sins).

5. **Islam's legal system is rational and protective.** The Prophet ﷺ demonstrated with a clear logical argument (*law yuʿṭā al-nās bi-daʿwāhum*) why an evidence-based system is necessary. Without it, the strong would oppress the weak by simply making baseless claims.

6. **The sanctity of Allah's name.** Taking an oath is a serious act in Islamic law. The weight attached to Allah's name is so great that even this world persists as long as His name is being mentioned. A false oath is not an "easy way out" — it is a guaranteed path to divine accountability.

7. **Taking the higher ground.** Even when wronged, the ideal Muslim response is to not descend into reciprocal harm — acting with integrity and trusting in Allah's justice, as expressed in the hadith: *"Do not betray the one who has betrayed you."*
