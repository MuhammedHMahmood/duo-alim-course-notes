## Session Overview

This session focused on understanding the trilateral verb system in Arabic morphology, specifically the 14 verb conjugation patterns (sigha) of the past tense (madhi), and a critical rule governing how verbs interact with their doers (fa'il). The class worked through the distinction between verbs with built-in (attached/muttasil) pronouns versus verbs that can accept an explicit external doer, using multiple examples and student Q&A to clarify common points of confusion.

Key themes:
- Why the musarrифун (morphologists) begin their reference tables with the madhi
- The 14 patterns of the active past tense (fi'l madhi ma'ruf)
- The 14 patterns of the passive past tense (fi'l madhi majhul)
- The rule that a verb cannot have two fa'ils simultaneously
- When to use entry 1 (fa'ala) vs. entry 4 (fa'alat) with explicit external doers

## Word Patterns (Awzan) Covered

### The Six Babs (Categories) of the Trilateral Verb — Middle Letter Haraka Combinations

| Madhi Middle Haraka | Mudari Middle Haraka | Example (Madhi) | Example (Mudari) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fatha | Dhamma | nasara | yansuru |
| Fatha | Kasra | daraba | yadribu |
| Fatha | Fatha | fatha'a | yaftha'u |
| Dhamma | Dhamma | karuma | yakrumu |
| Kasra | Kasra | hasiba | yahsibu |
| Kasra | Fatha | (less common) | — |

The musarrифун derived these six babs by observing that not all nine theoretical combinations of (fatha/dhamma/kasra) × (fatha/dhamma/kasra) exist in the language — only six unique combinations appear.

### Passive Past Tense — The 14 Sighas (nusira as model)

Formed by placing **dhamma on the first letter** and **kasra on the second letter** of the madhi pattern:

- nasara (active, he helped) → **nusira** (passive, he was helped)
- The same 14-pattern structure applies: nusira, nusiru, nusirat, nusirata, nusirna … etc.

**Goal:** A student should be able to recite all 28 forms (14 active + 14 passive) in one breath for a given root.

## Verb Conjugation Tables

### Why Start with the Madhi?

The musarrифун (Arabic morphologists) always begin conjugation tables with the madhi (past tense) for two reasons:
1. **The madhi is mabni (fixed/built)** — like a constructed building, its forms do not change based on grammatical role. Once you know it, it stays.
2. **The singular 3rd person masculine (gha'ib) is the simplest** — it has no extra letters attached (unlike fa'alat which has ta' al-sukun). Starting with the simplest form makes it easier to derive others.

By contrast, the mudari has variations (i'rab — case endings that change), making it more complex to use as a starting reference.

### The Built-in Fa'il in Each Sigha

Of the 14 past tense sighas, **12 have an explicit, visible (baariz) fa'il attached directly to the verb**:

| Sigha | Built-in Fa'il (Dhamir) | Refers to |
|---|---|---|
| fa'ala (dual) | alif al-ithnayn | they two (masc.) |
| fa'alu | waw al-jama'a | they (masc. plural) |
| fa'alata | alif al-ithnayn | they two (fem.) |
| fa'alna | nun al-nuswa | they (fem. plural) |
| fa'alta | ta' (mutakallim form) | you (masc. sing.) |
| fa'altuma | ta' + uma | you two |
| fa'altum | ta' + um | you all (masc.) |
| fa'alti | ta' + i | you (fem. sing.) |
| fa'altuma | ta' + uma | you two (fem.) |
| fa'altunna | ta' + unna | you all (fem.) |
| fa'altu | tu | I (any gender) |
| fa'alna | na | we (any gender) |

**Entries 1 and 4 only** (fa'ala and fa'alat) have **no explicit attached fa'il**. Their implicit (mustatir) doers are:
- Entry 1 (fa'ala): mustatir = **huwa**
- Entry 4 (fa'alat): mustatir = **hiya**

## Root Analysis

### Dhamir Count: Why There Are 60 Dhameers

The total count of Arabic pronouns (dhamir) = 60, derived as follows:

| Type | Marfu (Nominative) | Mansub (Accusative) | Majrur (Genitive) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Muttasil (attached) | 12 | 12 | 12 |
| Munfasil (detached) | 12 | 12 | — (no detached majrur dhamir) |
| **Subtotal** | 24 | 24 | 12 |
| **Total** | | | **60** |

### The Dhamir "tu" in fa'altu

A student asked why the 13th sigha (first person singular) uses "tu" and not "ni" or "i" (as seen in dhamir muttasil tables).

**Answer:** The dhamir muttasil table distinguishes by grammatical role:
- **Marfu muttasil** (subject/fa'il attached to verb): the form for "I" is **tu** — because the fa'il is always in the rafa' case (marfu), and the marfu attached form of "ana" is "tu"
- **Mansub muttasil** (object attached to verb): the form for "me" is **ni/ya** — because the object is in the nasb case

These are different rows in the dhamir table. "fa'altu" uses "tu" because the dhamir is functioning as the fa'il (marfu), not as an object.

Analogy: In English, "I" (subject) vs. "me" (object) are two different forms of the same pronoun. Similarly in Arabic, "tu" (doer/subject attached) vs. "ni" (object attached) are two different forms.

### Broken Plural (Jam' Taksir) + Verb Gender Agreement

A Quranic example was referenced: even when a broken plural refers to males (e.g., rusul — messengers, all male), Arabic grammar allows treating the broken plural as a **feminine singular** for verb agreement purposes. This is because grammatical agreement can operate at the **word level** (rusul is a broken plural, which takes the feminine singular verb) rather than the **semantic/referential level** (the messengers are all male).

## Practice Examples

### The Golden Rule — Illustrated with Examples

**Rule:** When a verb already has an attached (muttasil) fa'il, it **cannot** accept any additional external fa'il. The built-in fa'il always takes priority. A single verb cannot have two fa'ils.

**Consequence:** Whenever you use an **explicit external doer** (a noun or detached pronoun placed after the verb), you **must** use either entry 1 (fa'ala) or entry 4 (fa'alat) — the only two forms without a built-in fa'il.

---

### Example 1: "The people did" — an-nasu fa'alu

| Attempt | Form | Verdict | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|
| fa'alu an-nasu | fa'alu + an-nasu | **Wrong** | fa'alu has built-in waw (they masc.), which is its fa'il. an-nasu cannot be a second fa'il |
| fa'ala an-nasu | fa'ala + an-nasu | **Correct** | fa'ala has no explicit fa'il; an-nasu (nominative/marfu) is the fa'il |
| fa'alat an-nasu | fa'alat + an-nasu | **Wrong** | fa'alat implies a singular female doer; an-nasu (people) is masculine or mixed |
| fa'alan nasa | fa'ala + an-nasa (accusative) | **Wrong** | an-nasa is in the nasb case (accusative) — a fa'il must be marfu (dhamma) |

**Correct:** **fa'ala an-nasu** (فَعَلَ النَّاسُ)

---

### Example 2: "The students did" — fa'ala at-tullaabu

- "The students" is explicit → must use entry 1 or 4
- Students are masculine (or gender unknown) → use entry **1** (fa'ala)
- **Correct:** fa'ala at-tullabu (فَعَلَ الطُّلَّابُ)

---

### Example 3: "The women did" — fa'alat an-nisa'u

- "The women" is explicit → must use entry 1 or 4
- All female → use entry **4** (fa'alat)
- **Correct:** fa'alat an-nisa'u (فَعَلَتِ النِّسَاءُ)

---

### Example 4: "Fatima did it" — fa'alat fatimatu

- Fatima is explicit → must use entry 1 or 4
- Fatima is female → use entry **4** (fa'alat)
- **Correct:** fa'alat fatimatu (فَعَلَتْ فَاطِمَةُ)

---

### Example 5: "The car did it" — fa'alat as-sayyaratu

- As-sayyara (car) is feminine in Arabic (ends in ta' marbuTa)
- Explicit doer → must use entry 1 or 4
- Female word → use entry **4** (fa'alat)
- **Correct:** fa'alat as-sayyaratu

---

### Example 6: When the doer is a dhamir (built-in)

- "They (two males) did it" → fa'ala — the alif IS the fa'il, no additional word needed
- "They (plural males) did it" → fa'alu — the waw IS the fa'il
- "We did it" → fa'alna — the na IS the fa'il
- "I did it" → fa'altu — the tu IS the fa'il

In all these cases, **no external noun or pronoun is added** because the verb form itself already encodes the fa'il.

### Active vs. Passive — Oral Fluency Goal

Students should practice reciting all **28 forms** (14 active + 14 passive) for a given root in one breath. Example for nasara/nusira:

Active: nasara, nasara (dual), nasaru, nasarat, nasarata, nasarna, nasarta, nasartuma, nasartum, nasarti, nasartuma, nasartunna, nasartu, nasarna

Passive: nusira, nusiru, nusirat, nusirata, nusirna … (same 14-pattern structure with dhamma on first letter and kasra on second)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Why start with madhi:** The past tense is mabni (fixed) — it does not change based on grammatical role. This makes it the most stable reference point, like a building's foundation. The mudari changes with i'rab, so it is less ideal as a starting reference.

2. **The 14 sighas of the madhi:** The verb system has 14 patterns covering all combinations of person (3rd/2nd/1st), number (singular/dual/plural), and gender (masculine/feminine). First person (mutakallim) has no gender distinction — only 2 forms.

3. **12 sighas have explicit built-in fa'ils; 2 do not:** Only entries 1 (fa'ala, huwa implicit) and 4 (fa'alat, hiya implicit) lack an attached doer. All other 12 carry their fa'il visibly within the word.

4. **The cardinal rule — one verb, one fa'il:** A verb cannot simultaneously hold a built-in fa'il and an external fa'il. The built-in fa'il always takes priority and rejects all external candidates. Violating this rule produces grammatically corrupt (and communicatively meaningless) Arabic.

5. **Choosing entry 1 vs. 4 with explicit doers:** When you have any explicit external doer — singular, dual, or plural — always use entry **1** (fa'ala) for masculine/mixed and entry **4** (fa'alat) for exclusively feminine. The number of the doer does NOT affect this choice; only the gender does.

6. **Dhamir "tu" vs. "ni/ya":** The attached dhamir for "I" when acting as fa'il (marfu, subject) is "tu." The attached dhamir for "me" when acting as an object (mansub) is "ni/ya." These come from different rows of the dhamir table and must not be confused.

7. **Passive formation rule:** To form the passive (majhul) of any madhi verb, place **dhamma on the first letter** and **kasra on the second letter**. The same 14 sigha structure then applies.

8. **Broken plural (jam' taksir) grammar:** Broken plurals can take feminine singular verb forms in Arabic grammar — this is a word-level agreement (the form of the word), not semantic agreement (the meaning of the word). This appears in Quranic Arabic (e.g., with rusul).

9. **Grammar serves communication:** Grammar rules exist not as arbitrary restrictions but to **prevent miscommunication**. If a verb could take two fa'ils simultaneously, the sentence would be ambiguous and the communication would break down. The rule enforces clarity.

10. **Study method reminder:** Mastery requires revisiting recordings, asking questions in the group, and repeated practice. The goal is to reach a level where verb selection and fa'il identification become immediate and automatic — not effortful recall.
