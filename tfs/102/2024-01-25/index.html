    TFS 102 - Study Notes¶

    Date: January 25, 2024 Surah Covered: Surah Al-Zilzal (Al-Zalzalah) - Chapter 99

    Session Overview¶

    This session covers the complete tafseer of Surah Al-Zilzal (The Earthquake), which describes the events of the Day of Judgment. The discussion includes the surah's theme, its connection to preceding surahs (Al-Alaq through Al-Bayyinah), detailed verse-by-verse explanation, and the concept of Alam al-Mithal.

    Key Themes and Topics¶

    • The Day of Judgment (Yawm al-Qiyamah): The surah describes the destruction of the earth and the events that follow, including resurrection and accountability.
    • Metaphysical vs. Physical Reality: The Day of Judgment is a metaphysical reality that cannot be understood or judged through the laws of physics. Miracles, Jannah, Jahannam, Mi'raj, and Qiyamah all fall outside the scope of scientific inquiry.
    • Allah's Complete Knowledge: Allah knows every action, public and private, and has established a multi-layered testimony system to ensure perfect justice.
    • Accountability for Every Deed: No deed, no matter how small (even the weight of an atom), will be overlooked -- whether good or evil.
    • Refutation of Three Mushrikeen Misconceptions:
    • The universe is eternal and will never be destroyed.
    • Allah cannot possibly know all the actions of all people at all times.
    • Even if there is accountability, the idols will intercede and guarantee their success.

    Connection with Previous Surahs (Al-Alaq to Al-Zilzal)¶

    The surahs from Al-Alaq onward form a connected sequence centered on the human being (insan):

    1. Surah Al-Alaq (96): Creation of the human being, Islamic epistemology, division of people into mu'min and kafir.
    2. Surah Al-Qadr (97): Deen cannot be understood by human reasoning alone; divine guidance (the Quran) was revealed on Laylatul Qadr.
    3. Surah Al-Bayyinah (98): Some people rejected the divine guidance and tried to stipulate conditions upon Allah; their recompense is Jahannam.
    4. Surah Al-Zilzal (99): The Day of Judgment when that recompense will actually be administered.

    Verse-by-Verse Tafseer¶

    Verse 1: Idha zulzilat al-ardu zilzalaha¶

    "When the earth is shaken with its [final] earthquake"

    • This refers to the actual Day of Judgment, not merely a sign of it. Ordinary earthquakes are signs of the end times (akhir al-zaman), but this mega earthquake IS the Day of Judgment.
    • It will destroy the entire planet -- incomparably greater than any earthquake in human experience.

    Verse 2: Wa akhrajat al-ardu athqalaha¶

    "And the earth brings forth its burdens"

    • First meaning: After destruction, Allah will resurrect the dead. The bodies buried in the earth are its "burdens" (athqal), and they will be brought forth for judgment. This includes all bodies -- buried, cremated, or drowned. Nothing leaves the planet earth.
    • Second meaning: The treasures hidden underground (gold, silver, diamonds, lithium, oil) will be brought forth. This is a sign preceding the Day of Judgment and is being witnessed today.

    Verse 3: Wa qala al-insanu ma laha¶

    "And the human being says, 'What is wrong with it?'"

    • During the mega earthquake, as the earth collapses, people will panic and cry out in confusion. They will run to save their lives but find no safe place.

    Verse 4: Yawma'idhin tuhaddithu akhbaraha¶

    "That day it will report its news"

    • The earth itself will testify for or against every person. Every piece of land where a good deed was performed will testify in favor of the person, and every piece of land where a sin was committed will testify against them.
    • This answers the mushrikeen's claim that Allah cannot know all human actions: not only does He know, He will have the very earth testify as a witness.

    Verse 5: Bi-anna rabbaka awha laha¶

    "Because your Lord will have inspired [i.e., commanded] it"

    • The earth will give its testimony because Allah will inspire and command it to do so.

    Verse 6: Yawma'idhin yasduru al-nasu ashtatan li-yuraw a'malahum¶

    "That day people will proceed in scattered groups to be shown their deeds"

    • People will not walk together in one mass. They will be grouped by their deeds:
    • Good groups: Anbiya (Prophets), Siddiqeen, Saliheen
    • Evil groups: Those who committed zina, those who spread corruption, kuffar, mushrikeen, munafiqeen, mulhideen, etc.

    Verses 7-8: Fa man ya'mal mithqala dharratin khayran yarahu / Wa man ya'mal mithqala dharratin sharran yarahu¶

    "Whoever does an atom's weight of good will see it. And whoever does an atom's weight of evil will see it."

    • Allah will not miss a single deed, no matter how small.
    • "Man" here is ism mawsool, used for intelligent beings (as opposed to "ma").
    • The word "yarahu" (will see it) is significant -- people will literally SEE their deeds, not just read about them.

    Important Vocabulary¶

    Arabic Term Meaning

    Zulzilat Was shaken (fi'l majhool / passive voice)
    Idha When (adverb of time / zarf al-zaman); when added to fi'l madhi, it gives the meaning of fi'l mudareh (future)
    Athqal Burdens, heavy loads; plural -- refers to the dead buried in the earth or underground treasures
    Tuhaddithu Will report, will narrate
    Akhbar News, reports
    Awha Inspired, revealed, commanded
    Ashtatan Scattered groups
    Dharrah Atom (smallest possible weight)
    Mithqal Weight, equivalent to
    Maf'ul Mutlaq Absolute/cognate object -- a grammatical construction where the masdar is used as an object (explains why zilzalaha is in the nasb case)
    Ism Mawsool Relative pronoun; "man" is used for intelligent beings, "ma" for non-intelligent
    Alam al-Mithal The parallel realm of forms/manifestation where every action has a tangible copy
    Istihala Conversion/transformation -- a fiqh concept where a substance completely changes its nature, name, and taste
    Yawm al-Qiyamah The Day of Judgment (encompasses destruction)
    Yawm al-Hashr The Day of Resurrection/Gathering
    Barzakh The intermediate realm between death and resurrection
    Hikmah Wisdom -- a step beyond ilm (knowledge); knowing when and how to act on knowledge

    Major Concepts Explained¶

    Multi-Layered Testimony System on the Day of Judgment¶

    Allah has established multiple layers of witnesses so that no person can deny their deeds:

    1. Angels on the shoulders: They record every good and bad deed continuously.
    2. One's own body/limbs: A person's skin and limbs will testify against them. When the kuffar ask their limbs why they testified, they will reply: "Allah who gave everything the ability to speak gave us the ability to speak" (referenced from Surah Fussilat).
    3. The earth itself: Every piece of land will testify about the deeds performed upon it.

    The purpose of this system is to establish complete justice -- so no one can make excuses or deny their actions.

    Alam al-Mithal (The Realm of Forms)¶

    • A parallel realm where Allah has created tangible forms/copies of every single thing that happens in this universe.
    • Every action (amal) we perform has a form or shape stored in Alam al-Mithal.
    • On the Day of Judgment, these stored forms will be shown to people, and they will immediately recognize their own deeds with full clarity, unable to deny them.
    • Shah Waliullah al-Muhaddith al-Dahlawi (12th century Hijri scholar) proved this concept through 19 ahadith.
    • This concept explains how people will literally "see" their deeds (yarahu) as stated in verses 7-8.

    Metaphysics vs. Physics¶

    • The Day of Judgment is a metaphysical reality (ma wara al-tabi'ah).
    • Scientific theories (Big Freeze, etc.) should not be used to validate or invalidate Quranic descriptions of Qiyamah.
    • For about 90 years, scientists have accepted the universe has a beginning and will eventually collapse -- but the Quranic narrative has stated this from day one.
    • Mu'jizat, Jannah, Jahannam, Mi'raj, and the events of Qiyamah cannot be understood through the laws of physics.

    Yawm al-Qiyamah Duration¶

    • The Day of Judgment is 50,000 years long (referenced from Surah Al-Ma'arij).
    • It encompasses multiple stages: destruction, then resurrection (hashr), then accountability.

    Lessons and Practical Takeaways¶

    1. Every deed matters: Even an atom's weight of good or evil will be seen. There is no such thing as a deed too small to count.
    2. No place is private from Allah: The very ground you stand on will testify about what you did. This should inspire mindfulness of our actions everywhere.
    3. Worldly success is not a sign of divine approval: The mushrikeen believed their worldly prosperity meant they were favored. This surah refutes that assumption entirely.
    4. Science cannot replace faith in the unseen: While science may align with some Quranic descriptions, metaphysical realities stand on their own authority and do not depend on scientific validation.
    5. No intercession without Allah's permission: The mushrikeen's belief that their idols would intercede for them is false. Success in the Hereafter depends on one's deeds, not false intercessors.
    6. Be conscious of the earth beneath you: Perform good deeds in many places so the earth becomes a witness in your favor, not against you.

    Additional Discussions (Q&A Session)¶

    Halal Food and Cheese (Fiqh Discussion)¶

    • Vegetarian food from non-Muslim restaurants (e.g., cheese pizza) is permissible if one is satisfied the ingredients are halal.
    • Cheese and Rennet: Rennet (used in cheese-making) can come from cows or pigs. The key fiqh concept is istihala (complete transformation) -- if a substance undergoes complete conversion where it loses its original nature, name, and taste, it may become permissible.
    • For cow-derived rennet, the animal must have been slaughtered properly (Islamically) for it to be halal without istihala.
    • Trusting a Muslim restaurant owner who claims halal is sufficient; one does not need to investigate packaging. Halal certification organizations add another layer of assurance.
    • Reference given: Sharia Board (New York), Mufti Idris -- they provide detailed presentations on halal standards.

    Difference Between Ilm and Hikmah¶

    • Ilm (Knowledge): Broad -- knowing information and facts.
    • Hikmah (Wisdom): More specific -- knowing how, when, and whether to act upon knowledge. Not every piece of knowledge should be acted upon in every situation.
    • Example: Knowing someone committed a sin is ilm; approaching them with encouragement toward tawbah rather than condemnation is hikmah.
    • Hikmah is gained over time through maturity and increased knowledge; there is no shortcut.

    Engaging with Doubters and Deists¶

    • A person who says "I believe in Allah but not in Islam, the Quran, or Salah" is classified as a kafir (specifically, a deist).
    • When engaging with such people, the advised approach is to ask questions rather than defend. Turn their objections back on them:
    • If someone rejects Islam for being "a political system," ask why they accept other political systems.
    • If someone says Islam is "not perfect," ask them to define perfection and then test their own standard against their own beliefs.
    • This method of engagement is rooted in Islamic dialectical theology (Ilm al-Kalam), which will be studied in the third year of the curriculum.

    Ilm al-Kalam¶

    • Translated as "dialectical theology" (not merely "theology").
    • Involves categorization, disputation, and reasoning tools beyond just discussing the attributes (sifat) of Allah.

    Arabic Grammar Notes (from the session)¶

    • Idha + fi'l madhi = future meaning: Idha reverses the tense of fi'l madhi to give a future (mudareh) meaning. This is the opposite of idh, which when used with fi'l mudareh gives a past (madhi) meaning.
    • Zilzalaha in nasb case: Because it functions as maf'ul mutlaq (cognate/absolute object). All five types of maf'ul take the nasb case.
    • Man vs. Ma (ism mawsool): "Man" is used for intelligent beings; "Ma" is used for non-intelligent entities.
    • Al-ardu as fa'il: In verse 2, al-ardu is the doer (subject) of the verb akhrajat.
    • Athqalaha -- idafah construction: Athqal is mudaf, ha (pronoun referring to al-ard) is mudaf ilayh.
