    TFS 102 - Study Notes

    Date: 2024-02-15 Surah Covered: Surah At-Takathur (Chapter 102)

    Session Overview

    This session covers the complete tafseer of Surah At-Takathur, the 102nd chapter of the Quran. The surah addresses humanity's obsession with worldly competition and accumulation, warning of the inevitable encounter with the Hereafter. The session also explains its connection to the preceding Surah Al-Qari'ah and includes a Q&A segment.

    Key Themes and Topics

    • Distraction through worldly competition: The core theme -- humanity is diverted from the Akhirah by the race to accumulate wealth, status, fame, and popularity.
    • Certainty of death and the Hereafter: Everyone will die and face what comes after, regardless of how distracted they are now.
    • Three levels of certainty (Yaqeen): A major conceptual framework explained through the surah -- Ilm al-Yaqeen, Ayn al-Yaqeen, and Haqq al-Yaqeen.
    • Everyone will see the Hellfire: Not necessarily enter it, but every soul will witness it.
    • Accountability for blessings (Naeem): Every blessing, even the simplest ones, will be questioned on the Day of Judgment.
    • Connection to Surah Al-Qari'ah: The sequence from destruction of the universe (Al-Qari'ah) to resurrection and reckoning (At-Takathur).
    • Regret on the Day of Judgment: Both the righteous and the wicked will experience regret, though for different reasons.

    Important Vocabulary

    Arabic Term Meaning / Explanation
    Alhakum "Diverted you / distracted you" -- fi'il madhi (past tense verb); "kum" is the attached pronoun (maf'ool, second person plural)
    Takathur Competition to increase; from the root "kathra" (abundance). When two people compete to outdo one another in accumulating something
    Hatta "Until"
    Zurtum "You visited" -- fi'il madhi from zara/yazuru
    Al-Maqabir "The graves" -- jamu' muntaha al-jumu' (a plural of plurals); takes tanween because of the definite article (alif-lam), so it is not ghair munsarif
    Kalla Aversion particle (harfu rad'in) -- translated as "Nay!" Very commonly used in the Quran
    Saufa Particle indicating the future; used for distant or uncertain future (as opposed to "sa-" for near future)
    Ta'lamoon "You will know" -- fi'il mudari', second person plural
    Ilm al-Yaqeen "Knowledge of certainty" -- certainty gained through reliable information
    Ayn al-Yaqeen "Eye of certainty" -- certainty gained through direct observation
    Haqq al-Yaqeen "Reality/truth of certainty" -- certainty gained through direct experience (mentioned in Surah Al-Waqi'ah)
    Al-Jaheem "The Hellfire"
    Latarawunna "Surely you will see" -- with lam (emphasis) and noon (emphasis) attached to the verb
    Naeem "Blessings / bounties"
    Alam al-Barzakh The transitional realm between death and the Day of Judgment
    Maf'ool Grammatical object (nasb case)
    Fa'il Grammatical subject (raf' case)
    Mudaf / Mudaf ilayh Possessive construction (e.g., Ilm al-Yaqeen: Ilm is mudaf, al-Yaqeen is mudaf ilayh)

    Main Tafseer Points and Explanations

    Ayah 1: "Alhakum at-Takathur"

    • The competition to increase in worldly things has distracted you from the Akhirah.
    • Takathur encompasses anything that gives name, fame, or status: wealth, celebrity, popularity, social media followers, likes, views, subscribers.
    • Modern examples given: people obsessing over TikTok/Instagram likes and shares, treating 10,000 likes as if it were a Nobel Prize.
    • Also applies to competition for financial status -- example of a man working 16 hours daily at a hotel with no days off, barely seeing his children, purely to make money.
    • The wealthiest people (e.g., owners of major companies) are engaged in takathur -- competing to be number one on rich lists.

    Ayah 2: "Hatta zurtum al-maqabir"

    • "Until you visit the graves" -- meaning this distraction continues until death.
    • The distraction only ends when you are buried.

    Ayahs 3-4: "Kalla saufa ta'lamoon, thumma kalla saufa ta'lamoon"

    • Why "saufa" instead of "sa-"? Saufa normally indicates the distant future, while sa- indicates the near future. Here, saufa is used because of uncertainty in timing -- death could come tomorrow or in 60-70 years. The timing is uncertain, not necessarily distant.
    • Repetition of "kalla saufa ta'lamoon" is for emphasis -- what Allah wants us to know is extremely important.

    Ayah 5: "Kalla law ta'lamoon ilm al-yaqeen"

    • "If you knew with the knowledge of certainty" -- Ilm al-Yaqeen is maf'ool (in nasb case).
    • Ilm al-Yaqeen: certainty through information from a trustworthy source. Since Allah Himself and Rasulullah (SAW) are the ones informing us about Jannah and Jahannam, and they never lie, this information constitutes knowledge of certainty.

    Ayah 6: "Latarawunna al-jaheem"

    • "You will surely see the Hellfire."
    • Important distinction: Allah says you will SEE it, not that you will GO to it.
    • Hadith: Every soul, even those destined for Jannah, will first see the Hellfire and the spot that would have been theirs had they been destined for it. Then they proceed to Jannah if they are among its people.
    • The lam at the beginning and the heavy noon are both for emphasis.

    Ayah 7: "Thumma latarawunnaha ayn al-yaqeen"

    • "Then you will surely see it with the eye of certainty."
    • "Ha" (the pronoun) is the maf'ool of tarawunna and refers back to al-Jaheem.
    • After death and resurrection, observation replaces information -- denial becomes impossible.

    Ayah 8: "Thumma latus'alunna yawma'idhin 'an in-Naeem"

    • "Then you will surely be asked about the blessings."
    • Hadith: Rasulullah (SAW) once ate fresh dates and drank cold water in an Ansari Sahabi's orchard and said, "By Allah, this is one of those blessings that Allah will ask us about."
    • Fresh dates and water were not rare in Madinah, yet even these common blessings carry accountability.
    • Every blessing -- homes, cars, jobs, health -- comes with accountability for how it was used.

    The Three Levels of Yaqeen (Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyyah's Example)

    The example of a bottle of honey, attributed to Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyyah (rahimahullah):

    1. Ilm al-Yaqeen (Knowledge of Certainty): A trustworthy friend tells you he has a bottle of honey. He has never lied, there is no motive to lie, and it is a perfectly ordinary claim. You believe him based on information. This is like believing in Jannah and Jahannam through the Quran and Hadith.

    2. Ayn al-Yaqeen (Eye of Certainty): You ask to see it, and he shows you the bottle. Now you have observed it with your own eyes. This is like seeing the Hellfire on the Day of Judgment.

    3. Haqq al-Yaqeen (Reality of Certainty): You taste the honey. There is no higher level of certainty possible beyond direct experience. This is referenced in Surah Al-Waqi'ah (the second-to-last ayah): "Wa tasliyatu jaheem... inna hadha lahuwa haqq al-yaqeen" -- the deniers and the astray will experience the Hellfire directly.

    Practical takeaway: We should develop Ilm al-Yaqeen now in this life. We will inevitably have Ayn al-Yaqeen after death. We must strive to avoid Haqq al-Yaqeen of the Hellfire.

    Connection to Surah Al-Qari'ah

    • Al-Qari'ah described the events and destruction of the Day of Judgment.
    • At-Takathur continues the sequence: after the destruction, resurrection occurs, people are presented before Allah and questioned.
    • The transition is thematic: from cosmic destruction to personal accountability.

    Alam al-Barzakh (The Intermediate Realm)

    • After death, the soul enters Alam al-Barzakh -- the transitional realm between this world and the Day of Judgment.
    • For the righteous: time passes like a deep, restful sleep -- like sleeping when very tired and waking up feeling as though only minutes have passed, even if hours went by.
    • For the wicked: time passes like a restless, sleepless night -- like drinking strong coffee at 11 PM and tossing and turning until morning, waiting for the night to end.
    • This explains the use of "saufa" -- the experience of time in the Barzakh varies by person.

    Regret on the Day of Judgment

    • Everyone will regret -- both the righteous and the wicked.
    • The wicked regret their sins (obvious reason).
    • The righteous regret not doing more: "Why didn't I fast every day? Why didn't I pray all night? I wasted time."
    • Even the Prophets (Anbiya) will be preoccupied with recalling their own judgmental errors.
    • Famous hadith: People will go from one Prophet to another seeking intercession, and each will decline, saying they cannot speak to Allah. Finally they will come to Rasulullah (SAW), who will intercede.
    • The message: instead of regretting on that day, focus on preparation now.

    Q&A Highlights

    Squeezing of the Grave

    • Hadith of Sa'd ibn Mu'az (RA): Rasulullah (SAW) said, "If anyone were to be saved from the trial of the grave, it would have been Sa'd ibn Mu'az, but even he was given a mighty squeezing, then he was relaxed."
    • The squeezing for the righteous is compared to a mother squeezing her child in a loving embrace -- it is felt but is not punishment.
    • The squeezing for the unrighteous is severe -- bones of one side press against the other. This is punishment.
    • The trial of the grave is universal; no one is exempt.

    Hadith about Carelessness with Urine

    • Separate hadith about a person (not a Sahabi) who was careless about urine drops on his clothes and body, leading to punishment in the grave and non-acceptance of ibadah.

    Sabab al-Nuzul (Reason of Revelation)

    • Not every surah has a specific sabab al-nuzul. Some do and some do not. It is not always necessary.

    Fire Under the Ocean

    • There is a hadith mentioning "naar" (fire) under the ocean, but the word used is "naar" (generic fire), not "Jahannam" (Hellfire specifically). The earth's core is obviously fire, but equating it with Jahannam is not established.

    Qada (Making Up Missed Prayers)

    • If someone did not know that five daily prayers were obligatory (e.g., due to living under oppressive regimes that suppressed Islamic practice), they are still required to make qada once they gain knowledge.
    • Principle: ignorance of the law does not exempt one from the law. Analogy: a driver who did not know the speed limit was 70 still gets a ticket for driving at 90.
    • However, if the person is sincerely making up years of qada and passes away before completing it, we hope there will be no accountability, insha'Allah.
    • Qada is made for: Fard prayers (2 Fajr, 4 Dhuhr, 4 Asr, 3 Maghrib, 4 Isha) and 3 Witr. Not for Sunnah prayers.
    • Minimum valid prayer for qada: recite at least three small ayahs (or one long ayah) after al-Fatiha, perform ruku and sujud, recite at-Tahiyyat, and give salam.
    • One may occasionally pray qada in place of Sunnah prayers, but should not abandon Sunnah prayers entirely for extended periods.

    Qada for Converts

    • Qada does not apply to those who were non-Muslim (kuffar or Ahl al-Kitab). Their obligations begin from the day they accept Islam.

    Historical Context: Uzbekistan and Islamic Suppression

    • A student shared personal experience of Islamic suppression under the former Uzbek government (prior to 2016): no open prayer in masajid, no adhan, forced beard shaving, punishment for visible religious practice.
    • The teacher noted the historical irony: this region was once a center of Islamic scholarship. Imam al-Bukhari, Imam Muslim (nearby), Imam al-Tirmidhi, Imam al-Sarakhsi, and many Hanafi and Shafi'i scholars were from this area. The Muhammadi graveyard in Uzbekistan has 5,000+ graves, all belonging to men named Muhammad who were fuqaha.

    Miscellaneous Rulings

    • Hair tied during wudu: Permissible. Only masah (wiping) of the head is required for wudu, not washing the hair. Hair must be left open for ghusl.
    • Praying the four Sunnah of Dhuhr after the Fard: If one was unable to pray them before, they may pray them after.

    Lessons and Practical Takeaways

    1. Diagnose your own takathur: Identify what worldly pursuit is distracting you from the Akhirah -- whether it is money, social media engagement, status, or career obsession -- and recalibrate priorities.

    2. Develop Ilm al-Yaqeen now: Strengthen conviction in the unseen realities (Jannah, Jahannam, Day of Judgment) through studying the Quran and Hadith. This is the only level of certainty available to us in this life.

    3. Every blessing carries accountability: Even the simplest pleasures -- food, water, shelter -- are blessings (naeem) that Allah will question us about. Use them with gratitude and in permissible ways.

    4. Death is certain; its timing is not: The use of "saufa" reminds us that death could come at any moment. Preparation cannot be delayed.

    5. Seeing the Hellfire is universal: The hadith clarifies that every soul will see the Hellfire, even those destined for Jannah. This should intensify our awareness and motivation.

    6. Avoid Haqq al-Yaqeen of the Hellfire: The goal is to never experience the Hellfire firsthand. Act now so that your encounter with it remains only at the level of observation (Ayn al-Yaqeen), not experience.

    7. Righteous people also regret: Even the best among us will wish they had done more. This should motivate maximizing good deeds now rather than settling for a minimum.

    8. Ignorance does not remove obligation: If you discover missed obligations, take responsibility and begin making them up. Allah's mercy is hoped for those who sincerely strive.

    9. Work-life balance matters spiritually: The example of the man working 16 hours daily illustrates how the pursuit of money can consume all of life, leaving no room for worship, family, or spiritual growth.
