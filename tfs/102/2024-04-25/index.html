    TFS 102 - Study Notes

    Date: April 25, 2024 Surah Covered: Surah Al-Feel (Chapter 105)

    Key Themes and Topics

    • Imperialism and its consequences: The surah serves as a divine warning against all forms of imperialism -- subjugating others to one's rule, whether through dictatorship, colonialism, kingdom, or even democratic facades.
    • Connection between Surah Al-Humaza and Surah Al-Feel: Surah Al-Humaza condemned capitalism (love of wealth); Surah Al-Feel condemns imperialism. Both are linked back to Surah Al-Asr, which identifies things that block success in the akhira.
    • Neo-colonialism as modern imperialism: The concept that colonialism has evolved -- countries now invade, extract resources, and install puppet governments while maintaining a veneer of democracy.
    • Divine protection of the Ka'bah: Allah directly intervened to protect His House from Abraha's army, demonstrating that He defends what He wills.
    • The difference between Khilafah and imperialism: Khilafah is based on shura (consultation), accountability, and the sovereignty of Allah's law -- not the personal rule of a king or emperor.

    Main Tafseer Points and Explanations

    Ayah-by-Ayah Breakdown

    1. "Alam tara kayfa fa'ala rabbuka bi-ashaabil feel?"
    2. Rhetorical question: "Have you not seen how your Lord dealt with the People of the Elephant?"
    3. "Alam" added to mudari' gives negative past tense meaning.

    4. The original verb ra'a (to see) has its last letter dropped due to the effect of "alam" -- a sarf (morphology) principle.

    5. "Alam yaj'al kaydahum fee tadleel?"

    6. "Did He not make their plan go astray?"
    7. The fa'il (subject) of yaj'al is the hidden pronoun referring back to "Rabbuka."
    8. Yaj'al is in the jazm case because of "alam."

    9. Allah completely ruined Abraha's plan.

    10. "Wa arsala 'alayhim tayran ababeel"

    11. "And He sent against them birds in flocks."
    12. Arsala (to send) is the root of Rasul (messenger).

    13. The exact species of the birds is unknown -- Allahu a'lam.

    14. "Tarmeehim bi-hijaratin min sijjeel"

    15. "Striking them with stones of baked clay."
    16. The stones were specifically described as baked clay, not ordinary stones.

    17. Each pebble acted like a bullet upon impact.

    18. "Fa ja'alahum ka-'asfin ma'kool"

    19. "And He made them like eaten-up straw."
    20. Like grass that has been grazed by animals -- completely crushed and ruined.
    21. The fa'il is Rabbuka; "hum" refers to Ashabul Feel.

    Why Details Are Brief in the Surah

    • The surah was revealed in Makkah approximately 45 years after the event.
    • The incident occurred roughly 50 days before the birth of the Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wasallam).
    • Many people alive at the time of revelation either witnessed the event firsthand or heard about it from eyewitnesses.
    • Since everyone already knew the story, Allah did not elaborate on the details.

    Historical Context: The Story of Ashabul Feel

    Background -- Ashabul Ukhdood (70 years before the Prophet's birth)

    1. A tribe called Himyar existed in Yemen, led by Abu Nawas, a mushrik (polytheist).
    2. Over 20,000 people in his land accepted Tawheed according to the religion of Isa (alayhi salatu wasallam).
    3. Abu Nawas dug a trench (ukhdood), threw them in, and burned them alive.
    4. Two or three survivors escaped to Rome, a Christian civilization, and reported the massacre.
    5. The Roman emperor ordered the Abyssinian king to intervene.

    Abyssinian Conquest of Yemen

    • Two army chiefs were sent: Aryat and Abraha.
    • They conquered Yemen, killed Abu Nawas, and established Abyssinian rule.
    • Yemen became an extension of the Abyssinian (Christian) empire.
    • Aryat and Abraha later fought each other; Abraha took sole control.

    Abraha's Rule and the Attack on the Ka'bah

    1. Abraha became an oppressive ruler, similar to Abu Nawas.
    2. The people of Yemen (and all Arabs) still revered the Ka'bah and traveled to Makkah for Hajj.
    3. Abraha built a massive church as a localized substitute for the Ka'bah and ordered people to go there instead.
    4. Two incidents enraged Abraha:
    5. An Arab man entered the temple and urinated in it as a gesture of insult.
    6. An Arab caravan camped nearby and the temple caught fire (intentionally or accidentally -- both opinions exist).
    7. Abraha decided to attack and destroy the Ka'bah as punishment.

    The March to Makkah

    • Abraha assembled a massive army featuring elephants; the plan was to tie ropes to the elephants and pull down the Ka'bah.
    • No tribe in the Arabian Peninsula attempted to stop him -- tribes were divided (pro-Persian, pro-Roman, or independent).
    • The army reached Wadi Al-Muhassar (between Muzdalifah and Makkah) and camped there.

    Abdul Muttalib's Meeting with Abraha

    • Abraha's army seized several hundred camels belonging to Abdul Muttalib, the chief and caretaker of the Ka'bah.
    • Abdul Muttalib went to negotiate and impressed Abraha with his personality.
    • Abdul Muttalib asked only for his camels back.
    • Abraha was surprised: "I came to destroy your temple and you are asking about camels?"
    • Abdul Muttalib replied: "I am the owner of the camels, so I came for them. The Ka'bah has its own Lord (Rabb) who will protect it."

    Divine Intervention

    • When the army marched toward Makkah, the elephants refused to move despite repeated attempts over several days.
    • Birds came in flocks carrying pebbles in their beaks and dropped them on the army.
    • Each pebble struck like a bullet, injuring upon contact.
    • Abraha was not killed on the spot but fled; every place a pebble touched his body became an open wound.
    • He died upon reaching Yemen, and everyone learned of his fate.

    Timeline

    • ~70 years before the Prophet's birth: Incident of Ashabul Ukhdood.
    • A few years later: Abyssinian conquest of Yemen and Abraha's rise to power.
    • ~50 days before the Prophet's birth: Abraha's attack on the Ka'bah and divine destruction.
    • ~45 years after the event: Surah Al-Feel revealed in Makkah.

    Lessons and Practical Takeaways

    1. Imperialism in all its forms leads to destruction. Whether ancient military conquest or modern neo-colonialism, subjugating others invites divine punishment and blocks success in both dunya and akhira.

    2. Even supporting imperialism is blameworthy. One does not have to be a king or dictator to fall under this warning -- supporting or enabling oppressive systems makes a person a manifestation of this story.

    3. Khilafah is not imperialism. Islamic governance is distinguished from imperialism by: shura (consultation), accountability of the leader, sovereignty of Allah's law (not the ruler's word), and the fact that conquered lands' resources stayed with their people.

    4. The spread of Islam vs. colonialism. When Muslims entered other lands historically, they did not extract resources to enrich the center. Non-Muslims were given options: peace treaty, jizya, or acceptance of Islam. Resources of conquered lands (Iraq, Sham, etc.) were not funneled back to Medina.

    5. Allah protects what He wills. The Ka'bah had no military defense, yet Allah sent birds with stones of baked clay to annihilate Abraha's army. Reliance on Allah (tawakkul) is demonstrated by Abdul Muttalib's statement: the Lord of the Ka'bah will defend it.

    6. Trust in Allah does not mean human passivity. Abdul Muttalib still went to negotiate for what was in his power (his camels) while entrusting the Ka'bah's defense to Allah.

    7. Critical thinking about modern ideologies is essential. Without understanding modern political systems, liberalism, secularism, and their mechanisms, one cannot fully comprehend 60-70% of the Quran and Sunnah, which address governance, justice, and societal order.

    8. Scholars must be evaluated by their positions, not their appearance. Having a title (Mufti, Mawlana, Sheikh), a turban, or a long beard does not validate a scholar's stance. What matters is whether their teachings align with the Quran and Sunnah.

    9. The hadith about end-times "huthala." The Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wasallam) said that near the Day of Judgment, most people will be like useless chaff -- easily manipulated, subscribing to ideologies without investigation.

    Connection to Previous and Upcoming Surahs

    • Surah Al-Asr: Identifies obstacles to success in the akhira, including love of capital (addressed in Al-Humaza) and imperialism (addressed in Al-Feel).
    • Surah Al-Humaza (previous session): Condemned capitalism and obsessive accumulation of wealth.
    • Surah Al-Feel (this session): Condemns imperialism and the subjugation of others.
    • Surah Al-Buruj: Contains the story of Ashabul Ukhdood, which is the historical precursor to the story of Ashabul Feel.

    Discussion Points Raised in Class

    • Is there such a thing as "Islamic imperialism"? The term was coined by Western writers. Khilafah is fundamentally different from imperialism due to shura, accountability, and divine law as the source of legislation.
    • Duty to spread Islam vs. imperialism: Muslims have a responsibility to spread the word of Allah, but this is distinct from resource extraction or governance imposition. If force was used, it was because other civilizations blocked the message, and non-Muslims were still given multiple options.
    • Response to scholars promoting problematic positions: One should engage them on evidence (daleel), not accept fatawa blindly, and measure scholars by the yardstick of whether they uphold traditional Islamic principles.
