    TFS 102 - Study Notes¶

    Date: February 1, 2024 Surah Covered: Surah Al-Aadiyat (Chapter 100)

    Session Overview¶

    A complete tafseer of Surah Al-Aadiyat, a short Makki surah. The surah uses the imagery of war horses to deliver a powerful message about human ungratefulness toward Allah and the consequences of being consumed by love of wealth.

    Key Themes and Topics¶

    • Human ungratefulness (kufran al-ni'mah): The central theme -- Allah directs attention to how ungrateful human beings are despite all they have been given.
    • The loyalty and sacrifice of horses vs. the ingratitude of humans: Horses sacrifice their lives for owners who merely provide them food, yet humans fail to show gratitude to Allah who both created them and sustains them.
    • Love of wealth (hubb al-maal) as the root cause of ungratefulness: Intense attachment to dunya distracts people from remembering and thanking Allah.
    • Accountability on the Day of Judgment: A reminder that death is certain, secrets will be exposed, and everyone will be held accountable.
    • Connection to Surah Al-Zilzal (Ch. 99): The previous surah described the Day of Judgment; this surah provides the reasoning for why it is necessary -- to establish justice for human ingratitude.

    Important Vocabulary¶

    Arabic Term Transliteration Meaning
    العاديات Al-Aadiyat The racers / racing horses (from adaa ya'du -- to run)
    ضبحا Dabhan Panting / casting their breath (sound horses make while racing)
    الموريات Al-Muriyaat The producers of sparks (from iraa' -- sparking)
    قدحا Qadhan Striking (producing sparks from hooves on stone)
    المغيرات Al-Mugheeraat The chargers / those that charge (from ighaara -- to charge/attack)
    صبحا Subhan At dawn / early morning
    نقعا Naq'an Dust
    جمعا Jam'an Collectively / together; also a gathering (of the enemy)
    كنود Kanood Extremely ungrateful
    شهيد Shaheed Witness
    بعثر Bu'thira Scattered / raised (from graves)
    حصل Hussila Made apparent / obtained / brought out
    خبير Khabeer All-Aware

    Arabic Grammar Points Discussed¶

    • Wow of Qasam (oath): The "wa" in "wal-aadiyaati" is harf al-jarr used for taking an oath.
    • Fa of 'Atf (conjunction): The "fa" in "fal-muriyaati" is a conjunction particle. The rule of 'atf: the i'raab of the ma'tuf (what comes after) follows the i'raab of the ma'tuf 'alayhi (what comes before). That is why al-muriyaati carries kasra -- it follows the jarr from the wow of qasam.
    • Verb conjugation -- jam'a mu'annath gha'ib: "Atharna" is the third-person feminine plural past tense (athara > atharun > atharat > atharna).
    • Inna and its ism: In "innal-insaana," insaan is in the nasab (accusative) case because the ism of inna is always mansub.
    • Lam of emphasis (tawkeed): In "lakanood" and "lashaheed," the lam is added on the khabar of inna for additional emphasis. This rule is studied in Tasheel.
    • Word order for emphasis: "Lirabbihi lakanood" -- the expected order would be "lakanood lirabbihi," but Allah placed "lirabbihi" first to emphasize that it is specifically toward the Rabb (Lord) that he is ungrateful.

    Tafseer Points -- Verse by Verse¶

    Verses 1-5: The Oath of the War Horses¶

    "By the racing horses, panting; producing sparks, striking; charging at dawn; raising dust; penetrating into the center collectively."

    • Allah takes an oath by war horses, describing five qualities:
    • They race at full speed (al-aadiyaat)
    • They pant heavily from exertion (dabhan)
    • Their hooves strike sparks from rocks (al-muriyaat qadhan)
    • They charge in battle at dawn (al-mugheeraat subhan)
    • They raise clouds of dust and plunge into the center of enemy lines (atharna naq'an, wasatna jam'an)
    • These horses face death willingly -- enemy soldiers would target the horse first to bring down the rider.
    • The horses never complain, never show laziness, and are always ready when their owner calls them to battle.

    Verse 6: "Indeed, mankind is ungrateful to his Lord"¶

    • The connection between the horses and ungrateful humans: The horses are grateful to owners who merely provide them food (food that Allah created). If animals with lesser consciousness can show such loyalty and sacrifice, what excuse does the human being have for being ungrateful to Allah -- who not only provides for him but actually created him?
    • The question posed: Have ungrateful humans become worse than animals?
    • "Kanood" indicates an intense, deep-rooted ungratefulness.
    • This applies to both mushrikeen and mu'mineen alike -- ungratefulness is not exclusive to disbelievers.

    Verse 7: "And indeed, he is a witness to that"¶

    • The human being himself is a witness to his own ungratefulness.
    • If you ask your own heart honestly, you know whether you are truly grateful or not.
    • Reference to a hadith: The Prophet (SAW) said to seek fatwa (ruling/verdict) from your own heart.
    • A related statement (not hadith): "One who recognizes himself recognizes Allah."
    • Self-recognition begins with acknowledging our own ignorance -- we do not even fully understand our own five senses (e.g., the science of vision: images land inverted on the retina but appear correctly in the mind, and science still cannot fully explain this).
    • Our ignorance about ourselves is proof (daleel) that there must be a Manager of all affairs -- Allah.

    Verse 8: "And indeed, his love of wealth is surely intense"¶

    • This verse identifies the root cause of ungratefulness: intense love of dunya and wealth.
    • No matter how much wealth a person has, they always want more -- this is human nature, amplified by capitalism and lifestyle inflation.
    • Constant focus on increasing wealth causes a person to forget Allah.

    Verses 9-10: "Does he not know -- when what is in the graves is scattered..."¶

    • A reminder that death is certain. Every person must choose: die with love of wealth, or die with love of Allah.
    • You will be raised on the Day of Judgment with whatever love dominated your heart.

    Verse 10: "...and what is in the hearts is obtained (made apparent)"¶

    • All secrets will be exposed on the Day of Judgment.
    • Even if a person denies his ungratefulness in this world and puts up a front of piety, the truth of his heart will be revealed.

    Verse 11: "Indeed, their Lord about them, that day, is surely All-Aware"¶

    • Allah is All-Aware of everything at all times, not only on that Day. But because the context is about the Day of Judgment, He mentions His awareness on that Day specifically.

    Connection Between Surah Al-Zilzal (99) and Surah Al-Aadiyat (100)¶

    • Surah Al-Zilzal described WHAT will happen on the Day of Judgment (earthquake, earth revealing its secrets, deeds displayed).
    • Surah Al-Aadiyat provides the REASONING for WHY the Day of Judgment is necessary: to establish justice. Allah created and blessed humanity, yet humanity is ungrateful -- this is injustice, and injustice demands accountability, which demands a Day of Judgment.

    Lessons and Practical Takeaways¶

    1. Self-assessment of gratitude: Regularly ask yourself whether you are truly grateful to Allah. Your own heart is a witness -- be honest with it.
    2. Beware of love of wealth: The constant desire for more money and material comfort is identified as the primary barrier to gratitude. Monitor your attachment to dunya.
    3. Learn from the example of the horses: If animals can show complete loyalty and sacrifice to an owner who merely feeds them, we should show far greater devotion to the One who created us and everything we have.
    4. Acknowledge your ignorance: Recognizing how little we truly understand -- even about our own bodies -- is the starting point for recognizing Allah's greatness and our dependence on Him.
    5. Death is certain -- choose wisely: You will be raised with whatever love fills your heart. Make sure it is love of Allah, not love of dunya.
    6. Nothing is hidden from Allah: Even if you outwardly claim to be grateful, Allah knows the reality of your heart, and it will be exposed on the Day of Judgment.
    7. The Quran is interconnected: Every surah connects to the ones around it. Understanding these connections requires study, knowledge of Arabic, tafseer, and hadith -- not casual reading.

    Historical and Contextual Notes¶

    • Direct vs. indirect addressees of the Quran: The Arabs of Makkah and Madinah were the direct addressees. They understood horses and camels intimately. We are secondary addressees who learn through scholarship and study.
    • Battle strategy -- attacks at dawn: The Sunnah of the Prophet (SAW) was to attack at dawn, not at night. The reasoning: at dawn, people are awake and visible, so fighters can distinguish combatants from women and children. The pre-Islamic Arab practice was to attack at night, but the Prophet changed this.
    • Universality of the Quran: A question was raised about how the Quran can be universal when it references things (like camels) that some cultures may not know. The answer: this is true of every field of knowledge, including science. Not everyone understands everything by reading alone -- knowledge is transmitted through those who understand it to those who do not. The Quran was revealed to the Arabs, and it was their responsibility to explain it to others.
    • Justice encompasses both rights: The Day of Judgment establishes justice for both huquq Allah (rights of Allah) and huquq al-ibad (rights of people).

    Du'a Request¶

    • Du'a for the father of a student (Abdullah) who had spinal disc surgery and developed a blood clot, leaving him unable to walk for four weeks. May Allah grant him shifa and a speedy recovery.
