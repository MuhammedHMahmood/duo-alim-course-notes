    TFS 101 - Study Notes

    Date: September 28, 2023

    Surah Covered: Surah Al-Duha (Ayaat 5-11) with continuation into closing discussion

    Review: Sources of Tafseer (Recap from Previous Classes)

    1. The Quran - Quran explains itself
    2. Hadith - Prophetic narrations
    3. Statements of the Sahaba - Companions' explanations
    4. Statements of the Tabi'een - Next generation's explanations
    5. The Arabic Language - Linguistic analysis
    6. Tadabbur / Istinbat - Contemplation and deriving meaning

    Ruling on Biblical Narrations (Isra'iliyyat)

    • Many tafseer books contain narrations from Jewish and Christian sources because of shared stories (e.g., names of Ashabul Kahf are found only in biblical sources, not in Quran or Sunnah).
    • Ruling: We neither confirm nor deny biblical narrations.
    • We do not confirm them because they could be false.
    • We do not deny them because they could be true.
    • We simply do not rely on them.

    Tafseer bir Ra'i (Interpretation by Personal Opinion)

    • This is when a person gives an interpretation that contradicts the established sources (Quran, Hadith, statements of Sahaba/Tabi'een, language).
    • Tafseer bir Ra'i is not acceptable -- it is not the same as Istinbat.

    Surah Al-Duha: Context and Overview

    • Surah Al-Duha and Surah Al-Inshirah (Al-Nashrah) are twin surahs addressing the same theme: consoling the Prophet (SAW).
    • Reason for revelation: The Prophet (SAW) was saddened because:
    • Revelation (Wahi) had paused and he did not know when or if it would resume.
    • He faced harsh treatment from his own family members.

    Two Promises Across Two Surahs

    Surah Promise
    Al-Duha Promise of Khayr (goodness)
    Al-Inshirah Promise of Yusr (ease)
    • Sometimes a person has khayr without ease (e.g., earning halal money through hard work but not having enough).
    • Sometimes a person has ease without khayr (e.g., earning haram money easily).
    • The Prophet (SAW) was promised both khayr and yusr, which were fulfilled after the migration to Madinah.

    Ayah-by-Ayah Tafseer

    Ayah 5: Promise of Al-Maqam Al-Mahmoud

    Arabic: wa la sawfa yu'teeka rabbuka fa tarda

    Translation: And surely your Lord will give you, and then you will be satisfied.

    Key Grammatical Points

    • Lam (la) = for emphasis, indicating certainty
    • Sawfa + present tense (yu'teeka) = indicates far future (i.e., the Akhirah, not this dunya)
    • If it were near future, the particle Seen (sa) would be used instead (e.g., sa yu'teeka)
    • Fa tarda = then you will be satisfied -- this links to al-Maqam al-Mahmoud

    What Will Allah Give?

    • Not specified in this ayah. Explained in Surah Al-Isra (17:79): "It may be that your Lord will raise you to Maqaman Mahmooda (a praiseworthy station)."
    • Al-Maqam Al-Mahmoud = the praiseworthy position where the Prophet (SAW) will intercede (shafa'ah) for his entire Ummah.
    • The Prophet (SAW) will not enter Jannah until the last person of his Ummah -- even with the weakest iman -- enters Jannah.
    • A narration states that when this ayah was revealed, the Prophet (SAW) said: "I will not be satisfied while even one person from my Ummah is in the Fire."

    Practical Connection: Du'a After Adhan

    • The Prophet (SAW) instructed that after hearing the adhan, one should make du'a including: "...wab'athhu maqaman mahmoodan alladhi wa'adtah" (and raise him to the praiseworthy station You promised him).
    • Reflect: if we consistently make this du'a for the Prophet (SAW), will he forget us on the Day of Judgment when we need his intercession?

    Ayahs 6-11: Three Blessings and Three Responsibilities

    Core Principle: Nothing is free in this dunya. Every blessing comes with a corresponding responsibility. This applies to divine law and even worldly systems (e.g., welfare states provide free education/healthcare but raise taxes).

    Structure: Blessings Paired with Responsibilities

    Ayah Blessing Ayah Corresponding Responsibility
    6 Orphanhood -- Allah gave shelter 9 Do not oppress the orphan
    7 Lost -- Allah guided 10 Do not repel the one who asks
    8 Dependent -- Allah made self-sufficient 11 Proclaim the favor of your Lord

    Ayah 6 (Blessing): a lam yajidka yateeman fa aawa

    Translation: Did He not find you an orphan and gave you shelter?

    • The Prophet's father passed away before his birth; his mother passed away after; his grandfather by age 8; then raised by uncle Abu Talib.
    • Orphans in harsh societies typically cannot reach leadership positions and remain dependent.
    • Allah protected the Prophet (SAW) from psychological trauma -- his intellectual ability was never compromised despite these losses.

    Ayah 9 (Responsibility): fa ammal yateema fa la taqhar

    Translation: So as for the orphan, do not oppress him.

    • Because the Prophet (SAW) experienced orphanhood, he understands an orphan's pain.
    • Hadith: "I and the caretaker of an orphan will be like this in Jannah" (indicating closeness with two fingers).

    Ayah 7 (Blessing): wa wajadaka dallan fa hada

    Translation: And He found you lost, so He guided.

    Clarification on the Word "Dall"

    • Dall does NOT mean "astray" or "misguided" in this context -- it means lost (knowing the destination but not knowing how to get there).
    • Before prophethood, the Prophet (SAW):
    • Felt an intuitive urge to worship his Creator.
    • Knew the idols were false.
    • Did not know how to properly worship the one true God.
    • Would go to the Cave of Hira for days and weeks, contemplating and sometimes weeping at the Ka'bah.
    • Cross-reference: Surah Al-Shura -- "You did not know what was the Book and Iman" -- the details of faith (belief in angels, all books, Jannah, Jahannam, Qadr) were not yet revealed to him.

    Ayah 10 (Responsibility): wa ammas saa'ila fa la tanhar

    Translation: And as for the one who asks, do not repel him.

    • Sa'il = anyone who asks -- whether for money, knowledge, or guidance.
    • Connection: Just as the Prophet (SAW) was lost and Allah guided him, his responsibility is to guide everyone who comes seeking.
    • If someone does not know how to pray, teach them. If someone asks for financial help and you can help, help them.

    Ayah 8 (Blessing): wa wajadaka aa'ilan fa aghna

    Translation: And He found you in need/dependent, so He made you self-sufficient.

    • The Prophet (SAW) did not inherit wealth and was financially dependent on his uncle Abu Talib.
    • Allah made him self-sufficient in two ways:
    • Through marriage to Khadijah (RA) -- she was the main businesswoman of Makkah, and through managing her trade, he became financially independent.
    • Through internal contentment (ghina al-nafs) -- true richness is richness of the heart.

    Key Hadith on True Wealth

    • "Being rich is not having many possessions; rather, richness is the richness of the soul (heart)."
    • Example: Sayyida Aisha (RA) had only a single date at home. A woman came with two daughters asking for food. Aisha gave away the only date she had, and the woman split it between her daughters. This illustrates the ghina (contentment) the Prophet (SAW) trained his household with.
    • A person who does not chase the dunya and is happy with whatever little they have possesses a priceless form of happiness.

    Ayah 11 (Responsibility): wa amma bi ni'mati rabbika fa haddith

    Translation: And as for the favor of your Lord, proclaim/narrate it.

    Meanings and Applications

    1. Share blessings openly -- do not hide the favors Allah has given you.
    2. Use the blessings Allah gave you -- if you can afford good, healthy food, eat it; if you can afford nice clothes, wear them. Keeping blessings locked away and living miserably despite having means is stinginess (bukhl).
    3. Narrating blessings is a form of gratitude (shukr) to Allah.

    Supporting Hadith

    • A man came to the Prophet (SAW) wearing patched clothes. The Prophet asked if he had better clothes. The man confirmed he had wealth (sheep, camels, etc.). The Prophet (SAW) said: "Indeed Allah loves to see the effect/impact (athar) of His blessing on His servant."

    Important Distinction: Gratitude vs. Showing Off

    • Thin line between expressing gratitude and riya (showing off).
    • Driving a nice car because you can afford it and are grateful to Allah = permissible.
    • Driving a nice car to show off in front of those who cannot afford it = riya/sum'ah (sinful).
    • Only the individual truly knows their own intention -- no one else can judge.

    Lessons and Practical Takeaways

    1. Every blessing carries a responsibility. When Allah gives us something, we are accountable for how we use it and what we do in return.

    2. Care for orphans. The Prophet (SAW) experienced orphanhood firsthand. The Ummah is instructed never to oppress orphans and to actively care for them.

    3. Never turn away someone who asks. Whether they seek knowledge or material help, be open and generous.

    4. True wealth is contentment. Richness is not measured by possessions but by satisfaction of the heart. No matter how much a greedy person accumulates, they will never feel rich.

    5. Use and enjoy Allah's blessings. It is not piety to hoard wealth and live miserably. Use what Allah has given you -- good food, good clothing, good shelter -- with gratitude, not with the intention to show off.

    6. Make du'a after the adhan. Specifically the du'a requesting al-Maqam al-Mahmoud for the Prophet (SAW) -- this is directly connected to his intercession for us.

    7. Be grateful openly. Expressing gratitude through words and through visibly using blessings is encouraged, so long as the intention is thankfulness, not arrogance.

    8. Follow a madhhab with discipline. For ordinary Muslims without deep scholarly knowledge, following one madhhab consistently is necessary for discipline. Switching opinions based on personal convenience (desire) is not acceptable, but switching for genuine excuses or after thorough scholarly evaluation is permitted.

    Q&A Highlights

    On Following Madhahib (Schools of Thought)

    • It is mandatory for ordinary Muslims to follow a madhhab for the sake of discipline.
    • Picking opinions from different madhahib based on personal comfort = following desire, not scholarship.
    • Switching is permissible when:
    • There is a genuine excuse (e.g., living in a country where only one madhhab is practiced).
    • One has thoroughly studied and compared the evidences of both positions.
    • Switching constantly and inconsistently = against the principle of discipline.

    On the Prophet's Voluntary Poverty

    • The Prophet (SAW) chose poverty (ikhtiyari) -- it was not imposed on him.
    • Following his exact lifestyle is virtuous but not obligatory (fard or wajib).
    • If one has wealth, they should use it, help the poor, and be grateful -- not hoard it or refuse to spend on themselves.

    On Zahir ul-Riwayah and Nadir ul-Riwayah (Hanafi Jurisprudence)

    • Zahir ul-Riwayah: Positions found in the six books of Imam Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Shaybani (student of Imam Abu Hanifa). These are given preference.
    • Nadir ul-Riwayah: Positions found in Imam Muhammad's other works. These are secondary.
    • This classification was established during the time of Imam Abu Hanifa's students.
